From the DeskNo. 17

"Insist on yourself; never imitate."

— Ralph Waldo Emerson, Self-Reliance (1841)

Emerson wrote his great sermon against borrowed selfhood for individuals. This week it reads like a memo to a trillion-dollar company. Apple — the firm that makes its own chips, its own glass, its own operating systems — unveiled a Siri that thinks with a Google model it rents for a reported billion dollars a year. The thread runs through the whole book: a home that listens with someone else's intelligence, a heart that confides in a chatbot, a body that hopes a pen will do the work of a barbell, and a peace every capital can describe but neither party will sign.

Iran · Israel · Lebanon

Described in every capital — and now, briefly, at war again

For the first time since the April ceasefire, Tehran and Tel Aviv fired on each other. By the close, both said they'd stopped. Oil spiked past US$98, then breathed out to US$94.

The unsigned thing finally moved — in the wrong direction. Late on the weekend, Israel's renewed offensive against Hezbollah in Lebanon drew a direct Iranian missile reply, the first time the two states had struck each other since the 8 April ceasefire that ended five weeks of open war.1 By Monday afternoon Iran's foreign ministry told reporters it had "ended" its operations against Israel; Prime Minister Netanyahu said his country had halted strikes "for now" but that the fight against Iran and Hezbollah was "not over."2

The market read it the way markets read chokepoints. Brent crude jumped almost 5% intraday, crossing US$98 a barrel, before easing back to settle at US$94.25; West Texas Intermediate closed near US$91.30.3 That is still some 31% above the level on the eve of the conflict a hundred days ago — but the whipsaw told the real story: the 60-day memorandum that would reopen the Strait of Hormuz, demine it within 30 days and bar Iranian tolls remains, as it has for a fortnight, described in every capital and signed in none.4

President Trump insisted the framework was still reachable — he needed, he said, "a few more points" — and urged both sides to hold their fire.2 The prediction markets were less sure: Polymarket's odds of a signed deal by 15 June collapsed from a brief 60%-plus toward single digits.5 Three structural facts now hold the price down despite the noise: China has cut crude imports to a ten-year low and is drawing inventory; OPEC+ nodded through another 188,000 barrel-a-day quota rise for July; and the strait, though throttled, has kept trickling.3

The chokepoint didn't close. The conversation about it did.

Analysts split on what comes next. J.P. Morgan's base case — a June reopening — keeps Brent near US$100 for the rest of 2026, with a longer closure adding US$5–15 by the fourth quarter. Fitch takes the other side: a late-July reopening would send Brent toward US$70 from September, on the view that this is a logistics shock, not lost capacity.3

Colombia · 21 June runoff

A polarised country picks between two opposites

The first-round numbers held: far-right lawyer Abelardo de la Espriella took 43.7% on 31 May, leftist senator Iván Cepeda 40.9%, sending the two to a 21 June runoff for the right to succeed Gustavo Petro, who cannot stand again.1 The first head-to-head poll gave de la Espriella a narrow 49–47 edge, and prediction markets have him a heavy favourite at around 83% as he consolidates the centre-right.2

The arithmetic is the suspense. Third-placed Paloma Valencia (6.9%) endorsed de la Espriella; her running mate, the centrist technocrat Juan Daniel Oviedo, pointedly did not — leaving roughly a million votes in play. De la Espriella models himself on Bukele and Trump and promises a hard line on crime; Cepeda offers continuity with Petro's reforms and negotiation with armed groups.1

The contrarian read

Polls under-counted de la Espriella once already. But runoffs compress; turnout among the third of the country that backed neither finalist will decide it, and an 83% market is pricing in a consolidation that hasn't actually voted yet.

— composite of regional analysts

Ukraine · the long-range war

The drones keep finding the refineries

While the Middle East absorbed the headlines, Ukraine pressed the one advantage it has scaled this year — reach. Long-range strikes again targeted Russian energy infrastructure, with a refinery at Volgograd reported offline and a drone audaciously reaching toward St Petersburg, where President Putin had been hosting an economic forum.3 The pattern is now the strategy: not territory, but the cost of pumping and refining oil — the same barrel the rest of the world is watching.

Opposition politics

A rival party tells the Workers' Party how to vote

With Pritam Singh's leadership heading to a 28 June secret ballot, the loudest new voice this week came from outside the party entirely — and was promptly told to mind its own business.

The mechanics are now fixed. On 28 June the Workers' Party convenes a special cadres conference, requisitioned by 25 cadre members and chaired by MP Gerald Giam, with a three-part agenda: Singh must first account to cadres for his conviction for lying to Parliament's Committee of Privileges; cadres then call on him to step down as secretary-general; and if he declines, a secret ballot decides whether he stays.1 The conference sits the same day as the party's scheduled CEC elections, so the leadership question and the leadership itself will be settled in a single afternoon.

The fresh peg is who weighed in. People's Power Party chief Goh Meng Seng published a long open letter urging WP cadres to remove Singh, repeatedly branding him a "convicted liar" and arguing that the only question that matters is whether the WP can present the integrity a ruling-party-in-waiting needs.2 The pushback was immediate and largely against Goh: a rival party leader instructing WP members how to run their own house struck many supporters as both presumptuous and politically convenient. It is a small drama with a large subtext — who gets to define another party's standards.

Two procedural facts sit behind the noise. The CEC, when it accepted its disciplinary panel's findings, also recorded that Singh had no intention to act against the party's interests and that his choices were "judgement calls." And separately, the Law Society has referred the matter to the Court of Three Judges, the apex disciplinary tribunal for lawyers — a track that runs regardless of how the cadres vote.1

The ballot is secret. The pressure on it is anything but.

Public housing · The June launch

A Prime-heavy BTO, and flats where there were none for forty years

June's Build-To-Order exercise puts roughly 6,900 flats across seven projects on the table — nearly half of them Prime or Plus — and reopens the Lakeview and Shunfu pockets to new public housing for the first time in over four decades.

The story this month is supply, not the cost of money. HDB's June exercise — the second of three this year — offers about 6,900 flats across seven projects in Ang Mo Kio, Bishan, Bukit Merah, Sembawang and Woodlands.1 What makes it unusual is the mix: analysts put roughly 47% of the flats in the Prime band and another 5% in Plus, leaving barely half as Standard — a far more central, far more competitive round than the suburban launches buyers are used to.1

The marquee site is at Lakeview in Bishan — about 1,200 units beside Marymount MRT and MacRitchie Reservoir, expected to be the tallest development in the area and the first new public housing in the Lakeview and Shunfu pockets in over forty years.2 For a family hoping to live near ageing parents in Bishan, Toa Payoh or Ang Mo Kio, it is exactly the kind of plot that draws double-digit subscription rates.

The read-through for an owner lives in the fine print of "Prime." Those flats carry a ten-year minimum occupation period, a subsidy clawback on resale, and — the part that matters most — an income ceiling on whoever eventually buys them from you.1 That deliberately thins the future resale pool: a richer address today in exchange for less liquidity tomorrow. It is the lever HDB is pulling to stop central flats becoming lottery tickets, and it quietly reshapes the resale market a buyer should price in now.

A more central ballot — and a deliberately shallower resale pool behind it.

WWDC 2026 · the keynote

Tim Cook's last act: Apple rents its mind from Google

The most vertically integrated company on earth — its own chips, glass, operating systems — unveiled a Siri that thinks with a Google model it pays a reported billion dollars a year to use. A refinement year, and a quiet surrender.

For two decades Apple's whole argument was that it built the entire stack itself. On Monday, in Tim Cook's final keynote before John Ternus takes the chief executive's chair on 1 September, the company conceded the one layer it could not build in time: the mind.1 The rebuilt, AI-powered Siri runs on a custom ~1.2-trillion-parameter Google Gemini model, its heaviest queries routed through Nvidia B200 GPUs inside Google Cloud, under a deal reported at roughly US$1 billion a year.2

Apple dressed it as a three-tier "Foundation Models" system: a small on-device model for private, instant tasks; an Apple-run cloud model for harder ones; and — for the most demanding — "AFM Cloud Pro," which Apple says is comparable in quality to Google's frontier Gemini and runs on Google's own infrastructure.2 The new Siri is free with a daily allowance, with heavier use billed through iCloud+. It will not ship at launch in the European Union, which the Digital Markets Act complicates, nor in China.1

Around the rented brain sat the usual refinement year: macOS 27 "Golden Gate" formally ends the Intel era; a no-drops device list keeps the iPhone 11 alive on iOS 27; the Passwords app gains an agent that fixes weak credentials site by site; and a US$250 million settlement closes out last year's Siri-delay suit.1 The stock wobbled during the presentation, then steadied.

The model is rented. The routing is owned. The devices are manufactured. That is the new Apple.
The new Siri's routing: owned at the top, rented at the bottom. Source: CNBC; TechTimes; FourWeekMBA reconstruction of the keynote. Meridian figure.
The sovereignty sceptic

Renting the best model is rational — capital-light, and Apple's privacy routing is real. But the dependency is now structural: the company's flagship feature lives on a rival's silicon, in a rival's cloud, on a renewable contract. Leverage flows to the landlord.

— analyst composite

The optimistic reading is that Apple has simply specialised — own the surfaces and the trust layer, rent the raw intelligence, the way every modern firm rents cloud and capital. The Emersonian reading is darker: a company defines itself by what it refuses to outsource, and Apple just outsourced the faculty it spent a decade promising to build. Which it is depends on whether the model stays a tool — or becomes the thing the tools were for.

Build vs. rent

The memory tax has come for your rig

We named the memory supercycle last issue. Here is what it means at the workbench: the box you'd build to run a model at home now costs far more — and the same logic that pushed Apple to rent is leaning on you.

The number that should stop a home-labber cold is the price of plain DDR5. A 32GB kit that ran about US$95 in mid-2025 has been tracked toward US$550–600 at this cycle's peak — a near-fivefold move — as manufacturers pour wafers into high-bandwidth memory for AI servers and starve the consumer channel.1 Contract DDR5 chip prices quadrupled from roughly US$6.84 in September to US$27.20 in December; Micron has warned it can fill only 55–60% of core demand, and analysts now see the rally extending past 2028.2

The physics is unforgiving: a bit of HBM consumes about three times the wafer area of a bit of DDR5, so every AI accelerator built quietly subtracts several sticks of ordinary RAM from the world.2 For the self-hoster, that reshapes the build sheet. The unified-memory boxes that made local inference attractive — and the big-RAM workstations behind them — are exactly where the tax bites hardest, because capacity is the whole point.

Which is the home-lab version of Monday's keynote. Apple looked at the cost and complexity of owning frontier intelligence and chose to rent. You face the smaller mirror of the same sum: spend up now for a rig whose RAM has doubled, or rent cloud inference and accept the dependency. Self-reliance, it turns out, has a spot price — and it is rising.

Owning your stack used to be a preference. The memory market is repricing it as a luxury.

The buying climate

Why the gear you want quietly got dearer

Not a launch this week, a levy. The memory squeeze is now a line on every spec sheet — and it changes what's worth buying now versus parking until the channel loosens.

If a phone, laptop or handheld on your shortlist crept up in price, you are not imagining it. The same DRAM and NAND reallocation driving the home-lab tax (see Technology) is rippling into finished kit: Counterpoint expects global smartphone shipments to shrink around 2% this year on memory costs, IDC sees PC shipments down roughly 4.9%, and SSD makers — with NAND up a reported 246% since the start of 2025 — say they have no room left to absorb it.1 Makers are choosing between thinner margins, higher prices and quietly smaller base configurations; mostly you pay.

So the verdict for an optimiser is unusually simple. Anything memory-heavy — a high-RAM laptop, a big-storage handheld, a NAS refresh — is the wrong thing to buy at a cyclical price peak unless you need it today; these are the line items the supercycle inflates most.1 Anything whose value is in glass, sensors or sound — a camera body, a good pair of headphones, a watch — is far less exposed and fine to buy on its own merits.

The one genuinely new toy from this week is small and welcome: iOS 27 finally brings a proper custom EQ to AirPods, alongside Apple's "Reframe" trick that re-angles the perspective of a flat photo.2 Both are software, both arrive in the autumn betas, and neither costs a wafer.

The contrarian

"A peak is a terrible time to spec up." The optimiser's discipline this cycle is buying the sensor and skipping the silicon — and waiting out the RAM tax on anything you can defer.

— enthusiast forums

homeOS · HomePad

Apple finally commits to the home — should you wait for it?

The keynote's quietest reveal may matter most to this house: a dedicated home operating system, a counter-top hub, and a camera that knows your face. For a migrator already going local-first, it sharpens the oldest question — own it, or rent it?

Alongside the rented Siri, Apple previewed homeOS — its first new operating system since visionOS in 2023 — built for a long-rumoured HomePad: a HomePod speaker fused with a seven-inch display and an A18 chip, designed to sit on a counter and respond to presence, not just commands.1 The hardware ships in the autumn; the developer preview dropped this week, the familiar Apple sequence of seeding software first so apps are ready when the device arrives.2 Tellingly, the HomePad can run FaceTime without an iPhone in the room — Apple's first real attempt at a hub that isn't a phone accessory — and it anchors a wider push that includes a HomeKit security camera with Face ID and refreshed HomePod mini and Apple TV.1

For the reader mid-migration — Lutron downstairs, Home Assistant taking over upstairs, Matter and Thread stitching it together — this is a genuine fork. The ambient, presence-aware hub is exactly the experience local-first setups struggle to match; but it also routes the home's "intelligence" through the same rented Gemini brain, in Apple's cloud, on Apple's terms. The migrator's instinct — keep the automations, the logs and the triggers on hardware you own — is the household echo of this whole issue's argument.3

The pragmatic move is to let the two coexist. Keep the local brain — Home Assistant's latest release already does Matter onboarding, two-way IR and local lock PINs without a cloud round-trip — and treat a HomePad, if it earns its place, as a pretty voice surface, not the system of record. The walls can listen with someone else's ears as long as the memory stays in your house.

Let the hub be charming. Keep the brain at home.

Escapes · The soft-yen window

A weak yen, a quiet season, and the case for going to Japan now

The currency sits near a thirty-year low, the summer crowds have not yet arrived, and the hydrangeas are out. For the Singapore traveller, the maths has rarely been kinder — but the bargain comes with an asterisk.

There is a particular pleasure in a country that has, in effect, gone on sale. The yen has slid to around ¥160 to the dollar1 — territory it last visited in the mid-1990s — and for anyone holding Singapore dollars, the discount on a Tokyo dinner, a Kyoto ryokan or a Ginza watch boutique is real and immediate. Japan logged a record 42.7 million foreign visitors in 2025, and the weak currency is the engine.2

The authorities are not entirely comfortable with the slide. The Ministry of Finance is estimated to have spent on the order of ¥5.4 trillion defending the currency across April and May — its first intervention since 2024 — which steadied the range around ¥160–165 without reversing the trend.1 For the visitor, that intervention is the difference between a soft landing and a free fall; the bargain is durable, not a flash.

The connoisseur's move this year is the timing. June is rainy-season Japan — tsuyu — which the guidebooks treat as a deterrent and the seasoned traveller treats as an invitation: hydrangeas in full colour at temple gardens, mist on the cedar slopes, and crucially the pre-August lull before the domestic holiday crush. The Mishima Skywalk and the hill temples around Kamakura are at their best now, and half as crowded as they will be in six weeks.

The asterisk is overtourism, and Japan has begun to push back. Kyoto has barred tourists from certain private alleys in Gion; Mount Fuji has installed barriers and a toll to thin the climbing crowds. None of this should deter a considered trip — but the era of treating the country as an unmanaged theme park is closing.

The contrarian view

Before you ban it: the case against the moral panic

There is a respectable argument that the alarm is overdone, and it deserves a hearing. Sweeping bans, the sceptics note, tend to push children toward the least-supervised version of a technology rather than away from it: the family device with controls is safer than the borrowed phone with none. A child who is told the assistant is forbidden simply uses a friend's.

The more durable finding across the research is unglamorous: parental modelling beats prohibition. Children calibrate their own use to what they see at the dinner table. A parent narrating why they ask a machine something — and when they choose not to — teaches a judgement that no setting enforces. The goal is a child who can use the tool without being used by it.

None of this is an argument for a free-for-all. It is an argument for proportion: that the eleven-year-old who asks the kitchen speaker to settle a homework dispute is doing something closer to using a calculator than confiding in a friend, and that conflating the two is its own kind of error.

Australia. The under-16 social-media restriction remains the most aggressive age-gate in the democratic world — a live experiment whose results the rest of us will read.1

United States. KOSA and the App Store Accountability Act push the verification burden toward the platforms and stores — a structural shift from the current model where parents do the policing.2

The other side

Civil-liberties researchers warn that age-verification at scale means identity-verification at scale — a privacy cost borne by every adult to gate a minority of accounts. The cure, they argue, may import a surveillance the family never asked for.

The evidence, brieflywhat the research actually supports

Strip away the headlines and the developmental literature is fairly consistent, and fairly boring. What predicts a healthy relationship with technology is not the strictness of the controls but the warmth and consistency of the supervision around them: children whose parents monitor and model do better than children who are either policed or left alone. Reward-and-punishment regimes around screens tend to backfire, turning the device into contraband; calm co-use and clear, predictable limits do not.

The corollary worth holding onto is that an eleven-year-old's day is mostly not about the assistant at all. Sleep, unstructured play, time outdoors and the boredom that breeds curiosity are the things the evidence keeps returning to — the protective factors a panic about any single device tends to crowd out of the conversation. The machine in the kitchen is a small part of a much larger picture.

The takeaway

The reassuring reading: the same un-glamorous parenting that worked before the assistant works after it. Presence beats settings; modelling beats banning; and a curious, well-rested child is the best filter ever built.

Cosmochemistry · A fragment of a vanished world

A meteorite turns out to be a piece of a planet that no longer exists

A rock recovered in Africa preserves chemical evidence of a large, differentiated protoplanet that broke apart in the solar system's first few million years — a body that never finished becoming a world.

Meteorites are usually read as debris from the asteroid belt, but a recently analysed specimen tells a stranger story.1 Its mineralogy points to a parent body large enough to have melted and separated into layers — a crust, a mantle, the beginnings of a metallic core — before it was shattered. In other words, it is a shard of a protoplanet that started down the road to becoming a planet and never arrived.

What makes it valuable is precisely that it is a failure. The worlds that succeeded — Earth among them — buried and reprocessed their earliest material beyond recognition. A body that died young, by contrast, froze its infancy in place. Reading it is closer to archaeology than astronomy: a piece of a lost world, recovered intact because nothing ever overwrote it.

The researchers are careful about how much they claim; the inference of a specific vanished parent body rests on isotope ratios and modelling, not a return address. But the broad picture — that the early solar system was a demolition derby of half-formed planets — gains another data point.

A pill might protect the muscle a weight-loss drug strips — but the barbell still wins

The worry with the GLP-1 class of weight-loss drugs has always been that the weight lost is not all fat — that muscle goes with it, which for a person in their forties is exactly the tissue you least want to surrender. None of this is an argument against the drugs, which are doing real good for real people. It is an argument that the strength you build is yours in a way that the strength a pill preserves is not — and that for the reader who already trains, the headline changes very little.

The Essay · On borrowed faculties

The Rented Mind

This week a trillion-dollar company rented its intelligence from a rival, a household installed a voice it does not own, and a generation began outsourcing comfort, memory and muscle to machines. The oldest question in the file is whether you can borrow a faculty without losing it.

The most revealing sentence in this week's news was not spoken aloud. It was an architecture diagram. When Apple rebuilt Siri, it did not build the mind — it rented one, running a custom version of Google's Gemini on Google's own machines, for something like a billion dollars a year, and wrapped it in hardware and routing that Apple still controls.1 The company kept the harness and rented the horse. Almost every other story in this issue turns out to be a variation on that single decision.

Consider the spread. The home now answers in a borrowed voice from a speaker on the counter. Companion apps offer a borrowed intimacy that, a two-year study found, soothes in the moment while quietly thinning the human ties around it.2 A weight-loss drug rents the result of a discipline its users never had to practise, and a second drug is proposed to fix what the first one strips. Even the week's fragile peace in the Gulf is, in its way, a rented settlement — a deal drafted by mediators and held together by outside guarantors rather than authored by the principals themselves. Five desks, one motion: a faculty that used to be internal is being relocated outside the self.

The instinct to call this new should be resisted. The anxiety is at least twenty-four centuries old, and Plato wrote it down. In the Phaedrus, Socrates tells of the Egyptian god Theuth, who presents the gift of writing to King Thamus as a remedy for memory and a path to wisdom.3 Thamus is unconvinced. Writing, he replies, will not strengthen memory but replace it; relying on external marks, people will cease to remember from within. Worse, they will acquire "the conceit of wisdom" — the appearance of knowing, without the substance — and will be a burden to their fellows, having heard much and learned nothing.

It is the original review of an outsourced faculty, and it is mixed in exactly the way our own reviews are mixed. Thamus is right that the faculty atrophies; he is also, plainly, wrong about the wider verdict, because civilisation chose writing and was enlarged rather than diminished by it. The lesson is not that outsourcing is ruinous. It is that outsourcing a faculty is never free, and that the bill is paid in the faculty itself.

What Thamus could not see — and what we can — is that the bargain has a shape. Writing took something (the trained memory of the bard, who could hold the whole Iliad in his head) and gave back something larger and more durable (a civilisation that could accumulate knowledge across centuries instead of losing it with each death). The trade was good because what we gained could not be repossessed by the lender. The question to put to every rented faculty in this issue is exactly that one: is the thing we get back ours to keep, or are we merely leasing it from someone who can change the terms?

If Plato supplies the warning about the faculty, history supplies the sharper warning about the power. The recurring pattern is not merely that an outsourced capacity withers, but that it can turn and capture the one who outsourced it. The hired faculty becomes the master.

The Ottomans built the most formidable infantry in Europe out of boys taken in a levy and trained as slave-soldiers of the sultan: the Janissaries.1 For a time it was the perfect arrangement — a professional standing army loyal, in theory, to the throne alone. But a faculty the state could not perform for itself became a faculty the state could not control. The Janissaries learned that the hand which guards the palace can also empty it; they deposed sultans, blocked reforms for two centuries, and were finally destroyed only by force in 1826, when Mahmud II turned the artillery on his own elite guard. The same arc runs through the Mamluks of Egypt, slave-soldiers who simply became the dynasty, ruling for two and a half centuries the realm they had been bought to defend.2

Rome wrote the version that ends a civilisation. As the Western empire thinned, it increasingly contracted its frontier defence to Germanic foederati — federated tribes paid and settled in exchange for soldiering Rome no longer wished to do itself.3 It worked until it didn't. The army that defended Rome stopped being Roman, and in 476 a commander of those same federate troops, Odoacer, deposed the last emperor in the West and ruled in his own name. The lights did not go out because Rome was conquered from outside. They went out because Rome had outsourced the one faculty — its own defence — on which everything else rested, and the contractor foreclosed.

"The hand which guards the palace can also empty it."

The modern reader should feel the parallel without being bullied by it. A billion-dollar-a-year dependence on a rival's model is not a barbarian on the Rhine. But the structural question is identical: which part is the harness and which is the horse? Apple's wager is that owning the routing, the devices and the customer relationship is the durable position, and that the model itself is a rented commodity — interchangeable, replaceable, a horse you can swap.4 That is a reasonable bet. It is also exactly the bet Rome made about its soldiers, and the Ottomans about their guard: that the thing they had outsourced was the replaceable part. The faculty you rent has a way of discovering it is load-bearing.

What separates the survivors from the casualties is not whether they outsourced — everyone outsources — but whether they kept the capacity to do the thing themselves if the contract ever turned. The empires that endured kept a core army of their own. The firms that endure keep the ability to swap horses. The faculty you can reclaim is leverage; the faculty you cannot is dependence wearing leverage's clothes.

Apple, to its credit, seems to understand this. The whole point of the three-tier design — an on-device model it owns, a cloud model it runs, and only the frontier tier rented from Google — is to keep the horse swappable: if the terms sour, the routing and the silicon and the billion customers remain Apple's, and another model can be slotted into the harness. That is the institutional memory of a company that once let Intel become load-bearing and spent a decade clawing the faculty back with its own chips. It is outsourcing designed, from the first day, to be reversible.

"The faculty you rent has a way of discovering it is load-bearing."

Which is the test that survives the analogy. The Janissary state could not, when the moment came, fight without its guard; that is what made the guard its master rather than its tool. The reversible dependence is a convenience; the irreversible one is a slow transfer of sovereignty dressed as a procurement decision. Empires fell on the difference. So, in smaller ways, will the rest of the week's stories — which is where we should bring the argument home.

Bring it down from empires to the kitchen, because that is where the reader actually lives. The week's smaller stories are the same wager in domestic scale. The weight-loss drug is a rented metabolism; the proposed second compound to preserve muscle is a rented repair for the rented result.1 Against both stands the un-rentable thing — the barbell, the protein, the boring repetition — which confers a strength that is yours because you made it. The companion app rents the experience of being understood, and the study's quiet finding is that the rental can crowd out the real lease.2 The home's new voice rents you an answer to every question, at the cost, Thamus would say, of the habit of working some of them out yourself.

This is the moment to be honest about the other side, because the moral-panic reading is lazy and this file does not traffic in it. Writing did not make us stupid; it made libraries. The calculator did not end arithmetic; it freed it for harder problems. A patient machine may be a genuine bridge for the isolated, and a rented model may be exactly the right call for a company that wants to ship a good assistant this year rather than a mediocre one of its own. Outsourcing is how anything large gets built. The error is never outsourcing as such. The error is forgetting which faculties are load-bearing — the ones that, if the contract turns, you must still be able to perform yourself.

"The faculty you can reclaim is leverage; the one you cannot is dependence in leverage's clothes."

Which is where Emerson earns his place at the top of this issue. "Insist on yourself; never imitate," he wrote in Self-Reliance, and the line reads now less as a romantic flourish than as risk management.3 Emerson was not against learning from others — he read voraciously. He was against the surrender of the centre: the point past which a borrowed faculty stops being a tool you use and becomes a self you no longer have. The Janissary state could not fight without its guard. The Roman frontier could not hold without its contractors. The danger is not the borrowing; it is the day you discover you can no longer do the thing alone.

We have kept this argument company before, and it is worth the callback. The Cicero of our No. 16 essay knew that a borrowed memory is a borrowed self; the Thoreau of No. 10 wanted only the tools he could put down; the Pasteur of No. 14 reminded us that fortune favours the prepared mind — and a mind is prepared only by faculties it has actually exercised. The throughline is not nostalgia for self-sufficiency. It is a simple test to carry into a week that will keep offering to do your thinking, your feeling and your training for you: rent freely, but keep the horse you can ride home. Insist on yourself. The harness is worth nothing if you have forgotten how to walk.
