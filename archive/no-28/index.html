Tehran · Geneva · The Strait

The ceremony fell through. The countdown began anyway.

Days after a signing that wasn't, American and Iranian negotiators sat across a table at a Swiss resort and started a 60-day clock. First item on the agenda: 440.9 kilograms of near-bomb-grade uranium.

Representative — the Palace of Nations, the UN's Geneva home and the usual stage for talks of this kind; not the specific room. Source: Wikimedia Commons — File:Palais des nations.jpg · CC BY-SA 2.0 · Yann Forget.

The image the cameras wanted never came. When negotiators gathered Sunday at a lakeside resort, Iran's delegation declined to be photographed alongside the American, Pakistani and Qatari principals1 — a small refusal that said something large about who, in this arrangement, still controls the frame. What followed the missing photo-op was more consequential than any handshake: a face-to-face working session, the first in this round, and the start of a 60-day sprint to turn an interim framework into a verifiable disarmament.

The fourteen-point memorandum signed in principle by the American president and Iran's president on Wednesday 17 June set the architecture. The Sunday session set the hardest brick into it first. Before sanctions relief, before the reconstruction clause, before the choreography of a ceremony, sits a number the International Atomic Energy Agency has been repeating for months: 440.9 kilograms of uranium enriched to 60 percent2 — a stockpile a short technical step from weapons grade, and the single asset that gives Tehran its leverage and its liability at once.

The strait did its own talking. With a separate Israel–Hezbollah ceasefire renewed on 19 June, tankers moved freely through Hormuz; the blockade was lifted and Iran was, by the American account, selling oil without obstruction.3 Yet the same week the US Treasury added fresh sanctions on the arm that handles those very sales — a reminder that an easing and a tightening can travel together when neither side fully trusts the other to keep its keys where it says they are.

A refusal to be photographed is also a claim: we will decide what this looks like.

The fault lines inside Washington are not hidden. Secretary of State Marco Rubio and the president's envoys are reported to differ on how hard to press Lebanon; Senator Lindsey Graham is pushing to fold a Saudi normalisation and an expanded Abraham Accords into the same window.4 The 60 days, in other words, are being asked to carry more than a nuclear file. They are being asked to re-key a region.

What is genuinely new since our last issue is sequence, not slogan. The Hormuz framework and the MOU have recurred here for weeks; the delta is that the parties have now named what must be solved first, and set a clock against it. A stockpile is not a position paper. It can be diluted, shipped out, or kept — and which of those happens in the next two months is the only headline that will matter.

Foreign Policy · Kazan

A small state keeps its own keys

At the ASEAN–Russia summit, Lawrence Wong met Vladimir Putin at Moscow's request — and used the room to restate the one position Singapore never trades away: principle over alignment.

Representative — Singapore's Marina Bay at dusk; illustrative of the city-state, not the summit. Source: Wikimedia Commons — File:Singapore Marina Bay Dusk 2018-02-27.jpg · CC BY-SA 4.0 · Benh Lieu Song.

Prime Minister Lawrence Wong attended the ASEAN–Russia Commemorative Summit in Kazan on 17–18 June and met President Putin in a bilateral held at Russia's request.1 For a city-state that has spent the war insisting its stance on Ukraine is a matter of principle rather than alliance, the optics mattered — and Wong used the meeting to draw the distinction sharply.

Singapore's objection to the invasion, he reiterated, rests on a rule it cannot afford to see eroded anywhere: that the sovereignty and territorial integrity of small states must be respected, and disputes settled within international law and a rules-based order.2 That is not anti-Russian positioning; it is self-interested in the most honest sense. A world where large powers redraw borders by force is a world in which a island of six million has no defensible keys of its own.

For a small state, the rules are not an abstraction. They are the lock on the door.

The meeting also looked forward. Singapore takes the ASEAN chair in 2027, and the Kazan engagement reads as early groundwork — keeping every channel open while conceding no principle. It is the diplomatic equivalent of the issue's question: you hold your position best not by shouting it but by repeating it, calmly, in every room you are invited into.

At home, the relief calendar ran on schedule. June's Community Development Council vouchers were released on 11 June, the next tranche in a cost-of-living programme pitched against an energy bill that still lags the recent oil moves.3 The arithmetic of the household, like the arithmetic of the strait, is governed by lag: today's tariff was set on older gas.

Policy · The Executive Condo Reset

The flip-after-five playbook is dead

The biggest overhaul of the EC scheme since 2013 meets its first live test as the Sembawang Drive site goes to tender. The rules just re-pointed a whole rung of the ladder away from flippers and back toward owner-occupiers.

For a decade the Executive Condominium was Singapore's great wealth-escalator: buy with a subsidy, serve a five-year minimum occupation, sell into the open market and book a gain. The numbers were extraordinary. EC median new prices climbed from about $782 psf in 2016 to roughly $1,836–1,843 psf across January–April 2026 — a 134.8% rise.1 In 2025, 162 EC resales each booked a million-dollar gain, on an average hold of 9.6 years; the largest cleared more than $2 million.

That escalator is being re-geared. Under rules announced on 8 May by Minister Chee Hong Tat and applying to sites tendering on or after that date, the minimum occupation period doubles from five years to ten; full privatisation moves from ten years to fifteen; the first-timer quota rises from 70% to 90% with a two-year priority window; and the Deferred Payment Scheme is abolished outright.2 The first live test is now: the Sembawang Drive GLS site goes to tender in June, following Canberra Drive in May.

Double the lock-in, and the buyer you attract is the one who means to stay.

The behavioural target is plain in the data. From 2021 to 2025 roughly 75% of EC open-market sales happened within five years of MOP — up from 45% in the prior window.3 A ten-year MOP doesn't tweak that; it dismantles it. Rivelle in Tampines sold 92.5% of 572 units at a record $1,893 psf in March, 87.9% of them on the now-abolished deferred scheme — a snapshot of the model in its final form.

For owners, two read-throughs. First, scarcity: five old-rule projects still in the pipeline — Senja Close, two on Woodlands Drive 17, Sembawang Road and Miltonia Close — become the last of their kind, and may trade at a premium for it. Second, supply: developers are expected to bid perhaps 10% lower for the new-rule sites, while HDB resale flats hitting MOP surge from 2026, lifting the resale pool just as the EC exit narrows.

Brussels · Sovereign AI

Europe decides to build its own brain

The Commission has picked a consortium to train a sovereign, open-source frontier model natively across all 24 EU languages — so that European institutions can use advanced AI without routing their data through someone else's cloud.

On 19 June the European Commission named the EUROPA Consortium — led by Italy's Domyn — to build a sovereign, open-source model of more than 400 billion parameters, trained natively across all 24 official EU languages.1 The compute is as pointed as the politics: a dedicated cluster of roughly 6,000 NVIDIA Blackwell chips plus access to as much as 2.5% of EuroHPC's supercomputing capacity, so that EU bodies can deploy frontier AI without their data leaving European jurisdiction.

The contrast with the American build-out is the whole story. The United States is pouring an estimated $700 billion into 2026 data-centre capacity and hitting a wall not of chips but of power; Microsoft's Satya Nadella spent June arguing that "tokens" are becoming a form of capital, and that whoever controls their generation controls a new commanding height.2 Europe's answer is not to out-spend but to out-own: a model whose weights are open, whose languages are native, and whose servers sit inside the bloc.

Europe can't out-spend the build-out. So it is trying to out-own it.

For a reader who runs models on his own hardware, the symmetry is the point. The sovereignty argument made at continental scale — keep the keys, keep the data home — is exactly the argument for a local model on a desk. EUROPA and a home lab are the same instinct at different magnitudes. (The H200-into-China saga, by contrast, earns only a line this issue: still cleared, still barely shipping, Beijing still steering buyers to domestic silicon.)

The Open-Weights Wave · June

The same instinct, on your desk

EUROPA keeps the continent's data home; the June open-weights refresh lets you keep yours. Frontier-grade reasoning now fits in 24 gigabytes — GDPR-ready, no telemetry, nobody's server.

The wave refreshed again on 16 June. GLM-5.2 (Z.ai) folded into the Nous Hermes Agent stack; Kimi K2.7 Code HighSpeed claims roughly 6× faster code generation; MiniMax M3 posted 59.0% on SWE-Bench Pro, edging past GPT-5.5 and Gemini 3.1 Pro on that benchmark; and Nvidia shipped Nemotron 3 Ultra.1 The most striking entry is the smallest: WeiboAI's VibeThinker-3B, an MIT-licensed fine-tune of Qwen2.5-Coder-3B, reaching frontier-reasoner parity at three billion parameters.

Under the hood, two architecture moves are doing the lifting: DeepSeek Sparse Attention and Gated DeltaNet, the latter now shipped as a from-scratch PyTorch implementation by Sebastian Raschka.2 The effect for a home lab is concrete. At the 24GB sweet spot — a single RTX 5090 — Gemma 4 31B runs at 89.2% on AIME 2026, 84.3% GPQA and 80.0% LiveCodeBench v6; Qwen3.6-27B and the 35B-A3B variant clear 40–55 tokens/sec at Q4.

The Commission built EUROPA so its data stays home. You run Gemma locally for the same reason.

Home Assistant 2026.6 · The Local-First Lock

The keys stay in the house

The new release lets you manage smart-lock credentials and PINs entirely on-network — no vendor app, no cloud — extending the issue's sovereignty thread all the way to the front door.

Home Assistant 2026.6, released 3 June, does something quietly radical for anyone migrating off a proprietary system: it manages credentials and PIN codes for both Z-Wave and Matter smart locks locally, with no vendor app and no cloud round-trip.1 Add a guest code, revoke a cleaner's PIN, rotate the family's keys — all of it stays on your own network. For a reader moving from Lutron toward Home Assistant, that is the domestic edge of the same argument running through Technology this issue: keep the keys, keep them local.

The release also flips on an OpenThread Border Router 1.4 by default and adds infrared universal-remote receive, so legacy gear folds into the local fabric rather than a manufacturer's. Matter 1.6 and the Joint Fabric "Unify" work, covered in No. 26, earn only a line here — the spec keeps advancing; we won't re-explain it.

A lock whose codes live on your own network is a lock you actually own.

The Spec Shift · Why "1.4 by Default" Matters

Flipping OpenThread Border Router 1.4 on by default is not housekeeping. Since 1 January 2026 the Thread Group certifies only Thread 1.4 border routers — applications on the older 1.3 are no longer accepted — and from Matter 1.4.2 the Connectivity Standards Alliance requires border routers and network-infrastructure managers to be on 1.4 too.2 A release that ships the current version by default puts a self-hoster on the only spec the standard will still certify, rather than stranded on one being phased out.

The Object · A. Lange & Söhne

The watch that won't need you until 2100

Lange's new Lange 1 Tourbillon Perpetual Calendar "Lumen" is the issue's object of ownership: a heirloom whose calendar is correct until March 2100, set against a market that mostly asks when you'll sell.

There is a particular kind of luxury in an object that does not require your attention. The new Lange 1 Tourbillon Perpetual Calendar "Lumen" — limited to fifty pieces, driven by the new L225.1 calibre — keeps a perpetual calendar that will run correct, without intervention, until March 2100.1 You will not outlive its accuracy. That is the whole proposition, and it sits squarely on the issue's spine: the difference between owning a thing and merely holding an option on its resale.

It is a deliberate counterpoint to the flipping reflex that runs through the rest of the book — the EC ladder re-pointed away from quick exits, the auction frenzy that treats watches as positions. The Lumen is the maker's hand answering the flipper's market: a piece built to be inherited, not traded. Lange's semi-transparent "Lumen" dial, glowing its calendar indications in the dark, is almost a thesis statement — the thing keeps its own time whether or not anyone is watching.

Some objects ask to be traded. A few ask only to be kept.

Two grace notes from the wider week. The soft yen — SGD/JPY around ¥124.9, USD/JPY near ¥161 (carried, mark approximate) — keeps Japan unusually open to the collector heading to Ginza.2 And from High End Vienna, premium audio offered its own heirlooms: Bowers & Wilkins' 800 D5 in 60th-anniversary trim and DALI's Vega, objects bought to live in a room for decades rather than cycle through a portfolio.

Also This Friday · Muse

A record named after a signal heard once

The same release Friday brings Muse's tenth album — and a title borrowed from the one cosmic message we caught, then never heard again.

Muse release The Wow! Signal on 26 June via Warner — their first album since 2022's Will of the People, co-written and co-produced with Dan Lancaster.3 The band announced it in characteristically grand fashion, launching the reveal on a screen sent some 33 km into the atmosphere.

The title reaches back to August 1977, when astronomer Jerry Ehman, scanning printouts from Ohio State's Big Ear telescope, found a 72-second narrowband burst from the direction of Sagittarius — so strong and so close to the hydrogen line that he circled the sequence and wrote "Wow!" beside it. It has never been heard again. On a week when our Curiosities desk watches a robot chase a falling telescope, rock's most cosmically-minded band hands the same sky a question mark.

Technoference · The Parent's Phone

When the distraction is yours

A new study flips the screen-time conversation onto the adult holding the phone: it is the child's perception of a distracted parent — not measured screen time — that tracks with anxiety and withdrawal.

For years this desk has tracked the research on children's screens. A new paper in Frontiers in Psychology, reported by CNN on 20 June, turns the lens around.1 Surveying roughly 600 American adolescents aged 12 to 17, researchers found that teens who perceive their caregivers as distracted by their phones report more relationship anxiety, more emotional withdrawal and more signs of insecure attachment. The term for it is "technoference" — the small interruptions a device introduces into the moments between parent and child.

The crucial nuance is what predicts the harm. It is not the parent's measured screen time. It is the child's perception of being deprioritised — the glance at the phone mid-sentence, the half-attention at the dinner table.2 Two parents with identical screen-time logs can land very differently depending on whether the child feels seen. That reframes the fix away from a perfect daily total and toward something more achievable: predictable, protected device-free windows.

It isn't the minutes on the phone. It's whether the child feels deprioritised by them.

Dr Leana Wen's framing, cited in the coverage, is that the goal is not perfection but predictability — a few windows the child can count on, when the parent is fully present.3 That is a kinder standard than the usual screen-time scolding, and an honest one: presence is a habit you keep, not a score you hit.

The Evidence Page

What the wider research actually supports

The technoference finding sits inside a consistent body of work. Data from the ABCD study links parent, mealtime and bedroom screens to more problematic adolescent use — and finds, repeatedly, that monitoring and modelling beat reward-and-punishment regimes (the Nagata line this desk has cited before).4

A 2026 APA meta-analysis pooling 117 studies across some 292,000 children describes the relationship as bidirectional: distress and heavy use feed each other in a vicious circle rather than running one way. The practical guidance that survives all of it is unglamorous — protect sleep, protect a few shared device-free windows, and model the behaviour you want.5

Phubbing · The Adult Counterpart

The snub you don't notice you're giving

Where the Family Desk watched the parent's phone, here it is the partner's. Being "phubbed" makes people feel less loved — and the mechanism researchers name is affection deprivation.

"Phubbing" — phone-snubbing a partner — has a fresh evidence base. Work by Denes and colleagues in the Journal of Social and Personal Relationships (UConn and Columbia, reported 12 March 2026) finds that being phubbed makes people feel less loved, which in turn lowers relationship satisfaction.1 The mechanism they name is "affection deprivation": the phone doesn't just interrupt a conversation, it withholds a small, expected signal of being valued.

It is the same scarce resource the whole issue circles — attention — read at the scale of a marriage rather than a strait. Supporting findings sharpen it. The mere presence of a phone face-up on the table reduces felt closeness (the Dwyer/UBC result); the psychologist David Myers has called phubbing a form of "micro-ostracism," dozens of tiny exclusions a day; and a meta-analysis confirms lower satisfaction, intimacy and responsiveness where it runs unchecked.2

The phone doesn't only interrupt the talk. It withholds the small signal of being valued.

The fix the researchers point to is unromantic and effective: talk about phone norms explicitly, agree device-free times, involve the partner rather than legislating at them, and — the part that does the actual work — be affectionate in the windows you protect. For a couple in a long marriage, this is less a rule than a maintenance habit: the keeping of a watch, again.

The Long Read

Who Holds the Keys

A strait, a model, a housing ladder, a marriage's attention — this week the same question kept surfacing in different clothes. The answer the history offers is awkward: sovereignty is less a thing you own than a watch you keep, and the safest grip is the one you are willing to share.

The oldest symbol of authority in the Western imagination is not a crown or a sword but a pair of keys. When the Gospel of Matthew has Christ promise Peter the keys of the kingdom, it fixes an idea that outlived its theology: the one who holds the keys decides who passes.1 The Vatican still carries two crossed keys on its arms, one gold and one silver, and every locksmith's shop is a faint echo of the same claim. To hold the key is to hold the threshold.

Which is why surrender, for most of history, was staged as a handing-over of keys. In 1347, after a siege of eleven months, six leading citizens of Calais walked out to Edward III of England barefoot, with nooses around their necks, carrying the keys of the town to lay at his feet.2 Froissart's chronicle made them famous; Rodin's monument, five and a half centuries later, made them tragic. The detail that matters is the choreography. Sovereignty did not evaporate at Calais; it was transferred, and everyone present understood that the keys were the thing being moved.

Read this week's news with that image in mind and a pattern jumps out. Almost every lead was a story about keys — who holds them, who is handing them over, who is quietly being relieved of them while looking the other way.

To hold the key is to hold the threshold. Surrender was always staged as a handing-over of keys.

In The World, the entire US–Iran negotiation reduced to a single set of keys: 440.9 kilograms of near-bomb-grade uranium, and a sixty-day clock to decide whether Tehran dilutes them, ships them out, or keeps them. Iran's refusal to be photographed beside the other principals was a small assertion that it still controls at least the frame, if not yet the stockpile. The strait itself is a chokepoint — the most literal key of all, a door through which a fifth of the world's oil must pass — and the week's quiet drama was tankers moving freely through it again while a mandatory-insurance line reminded everyone whose permission still mattered.

In Technology, Europe announced it would build its own brain rather than rent one: a sovereign, open-source model trained natively in all twenty-four of the bloc's languages, on chips inside its own borders, so that European data need never leave the room.3 On a desk in Singapore, the same instinct runs a frontier-grade model in twenty-four gigabytes — "no telemetry," nobody's server. The continent and the home lab are making one argument at two magnitudes: keep the keys, keep them local.

Property told a quieter version. The overhaul of the Executive Condominium scheme — ten-year occupation, fifteen-year privatisation, the deferred-payment trick abolished — does not change who can buy a flat. It changes who can flip one, re-pointing a whole rung of the housing ladder away from the option-trader and back toward the person who means to live there. A rule-change is a re-keying: the lock looks the same, but the key that used to open the quick exit no longer turns.

And then the most intimate keys of all. The Family Desk and Love & Life landed, independently, on the same finding from different literatures: that the scarce resource a parent or a partner controls is not time or money but attention, and that withholding it — the glance at the phone mid-sentence, the device face-up at dinner — registers as a small theft of being valued. "Technoference" and "phubbing" are ugly words for an old wound. The phone does not steal the conversation; it withholds the signal that the other person matters.

A rule-change is a re-keying: the lock looks the same, but the old key no longer turns.

Here the epigraph earns its place. John Philpot Curran, arguing in 1790 for the right of election, warned that liberty is held only on the condition of "eternal vigilance" — that a freedom you stop watching is a freedom already slipping away.4 The line is usually quoted at nations. It applies just as well to a marriage, a model and a strait. Keys do not keep themselves.

The locksmiths learned this the hard way. For sixty years the Bramah "Challenge" lock sat in a London shop window, unpicked, beside a banknote promising a reward to anyone who could open it — a standing monument to perfect security. At the Great Exhibition of 1851, an American named Alfred Charles Hobbs sat down in front of it and, over roughly fifty hours spread across several days, opened it; he had already defeated the supposedly impregnable Chubb detector lock.5 The "Great Lock Controversy" that followed was really an argument about vigilance. The lock had not been secure; it had merely been un-attacked. Its owners had mistaken the absence of a successful thief for the presence of safety.

That is the trap every key-holder falls into, from the Bramah company to the smart-home owner who assumes the cloud will always answer. It is why the local-first lock — credentials managed on your own network, no vendor round-trip — is more than a convenience. It narrows the surface someone else has to attack, and it keeps the watch where you can keep it.

But vigilance is only half the lesson, and the smaller half. There is a second, harder truth running under the week, and it points in the opposite direction from "keep your keys close." It is this: the most resilient way to hold power is to distribute it, so that no single key, lost or picked, brings the whole house down.

The American founders built a government on exactly this suspicion of concentrated keys. In the fifty-first Federalist paper, Madison argued that the cure for ambition was more ambition — power set against power, so that no one office could capture the rest.6 The Roman Republic had reached for the same device with two consuls, each able to check the other. The point in both cases was not efficiency. A single key-holder is always faster. The point was survivability: a system that does not fail catastrophically when one hand proves untrustworthy.

The most resilient way to hold power is to distribute it — so no single lost key brings down the house.

This is where the Macro Desk quietly rejoins the essay. The reader who owns the world in two instruments — global equities and global bonds — is practising Madison's insight with money. Diversification is not a way to win bigger; it is a way to ensure that no single failure is fatal. And the uncomfortable note this week was precisely that the two keys have started turning together: with the equity–bond correlation positive again, the distribution is doing less work than usual. Even a well-spread grip can tighten into a single point of failure when the same force moves everything at once.

So the week's answer to its own question is double-edged, and worth holding both halves. Some keys you must keep close and watch without rest — the stockpile, the model, the front door, the attention you owe the people across the table. Curran's vigilance is not optional for those. But the keys to your own destiny are best not held in one fist at all. They are best scattered, deliberately, across enough hands and instruments and habits that no single theft, no single picked lock, no single hawkish dot on a chart, can take the house.

The Burghers of Calais survived because a queen interceded; the Bramah lock fell because its owners mistook quiet for safety; a republic endures because it trusts no one office with all the keys. Sovereignty, it turns out, was never a possession. It was always a practice — the keeping of a watch, and the wisdom to know which keys to guard and which to give away.

The Last Word

The watch is the work

A strait stays open only while someone keeps clearing it. A model stays yours only while it runs on your own machine. A ladder serves owners only while a rule keeps pointing it their way. And the people across your table stay sure they matter only while you keep looking up.

None of it is a possession you can set down. Curran had it right two centuries ago: liberty is rented from vigilance, and the rent is due every day. Hold the few keys that must be guarded; scatter the rest so no single loss can take the house. Then keep the watch — that is the whole of it.

"The condition upon which God hath given liberty to man is eternal vigilance." — John Philpot Curran, 1790
