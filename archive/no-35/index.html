From the Desk

"Every day is a journey, and the journey itself is home."

— Matsuo Bashō, The Narrow Road to the Deep North (Oku no Hosomichi, 1694)

Bashō set out on a walk he did not expect to survive and discovered that home is not the hut you leave but the practice you carry. This week the theme surfaced everywhere. Christopher Nolan drags Homer's oldest homecoming into an IMAX; an interstellar comet older than the Sun turns for the exit, never to return; a supreme leader's coffin is carried the long way to Mashhad. And quietly, in the culture, a turn toward the real — analog childhoods, friendships tended on purpose, homes that run on your own hardware, a market rotating back to fundamentals. The through-line: home is not a place you reach. It is a thing you keep coming back to.

The World · After the war
A Body Goes Home the Long Way
Iran carries Ayatollah Khamenei to his grave in Mashhad while the guns fall quiet for a week — and the real fight, over who controls the Strait of Hormuz, waits on the far side of the funeral.

Four months after an airstrike killed him at the opening of the 2026 war, Ayatollah Ali Khamenei is finally being buried.1 On Saturday, tens of thousands filed through Tehran's vast Grand Mosalla to view the flag-draped coffins of the supreme leader and the members of his family killed in the same 28 February strike — a daughter, a son-in-law, a fourteen-month-old grandchild.1 Volunteers sprayed cooling water over the black-clad crowd in the summer heat; some beat their chests and chanted for revenge.

Over the coming week the body travels: paraded through Tehran, then south to the seminary city of Qom, on to Karbala in Iraq, and finally to Mashhad, Khamenei's birthplace, for burial at the Imam Reza shrine.2 Iran expects fifteen to twenty million mourners across the processions — potentially the largest state funeral in its history, and a deliberate show of strength for a battered theocracy now led by Khamenei's son and successor, Mojtaba, who was named leader a week after the killing and has not been seen in public since.2

The funeral doubles as diplomacy by other means. Indirect US–Iran talks in Doha are paused for the week of mourning; both sides claim "positive progress" under the interim deal signed in mid-June, which opened a 60-day window to negotiate a permanent end to the war and unfroze billions in Iranian assets.3 Ahead of America's 250th-anniversary festivities, Washington and Tehran even agreed a one-week de-escalation in the Strait.3

The security of Hormuz, Tehran says, "lies with the coastal states." London and Paris disagree — and the argument is only paused, not settled.

That is the sentence that matters. Iran has signalled it will begin charging tolls on tankers and container ships transiting Hormuz in mid-August, once the negotiating window closes — upending the strait's status as an international waterway.4 When Emmanuel Macron and Keir Starmer said in a joint statement that Britain and France stood ready to deploy a multinational mission to protect freedom of navigation, Tehran's chief negotiator answered on X: the crisis-makers "will be held accountable… This is a serious warning."4 Commercial traffic is recovering nonetheless — roughly 335 vessels transited last week, per maritime-risk consultancy Marisks.4

The Chatter

Analysts read the pageantry two ways. A mass turnout lets the state project unity six months after nationwide protests were crushed; but a crowd in the millions revives memories of the 1989 Khomeini funeral and the 2020 Soleimani stampede that killed 56.

— Reuters, AP, CNN wires (3–4 Jul)

Two framings of one strait
TehranPress TV / state framing

The martyr's blood "marks another turning point in the victories of beloved Islam," a Revolutionary Guard general told state television. Iran struck US bases, "asserted its control" of Hormuz, and forced the enemy to the table; the strait's security is a matter for its coastal states alone, and foreign patrols are "adventurism." Friendly nations, the negotiator adds, will get "special treatment" on any future transit arrangements.

London · Paris · WashingtonFT / Reuters framing

A fifth of the world's seaborne oil and gas once passed through Hormuz in peacetime; a toll regime would be a unilateral rewrite of freedom of navigation. Britain and France offer a multinational mission; Washington's envoys, per Axios, are trying to talk Tehran out of tolls entirely. The worry is less the funeral week than 15 August, when the negotiating clock runs out.

Between the two sits the uncomfortable arithmetic every capital already knows: the announcement of a reopening and the practical reopening of a waterway are months apart — mines, insurance premiums and rerouted hulls all lag the diplomacy. It is the same lesson the Suez closure taught in 1975, and the reason Brent has drifted back to the low-$70s rather than spiking (see the Macro Desk, p15).1

The Record That Keeps Its Nerve
The Straits Times Index grinds to fresh highs while the world's AI trade wobbles — a quiet, dividend-heavy home market doing the diversification job the reader's global fund can't.
Marina Bay at dusk — the dividend-heavy home market the desk is really about. Singapore Marina Bay Dusk 2018-02-27.jpg · Benh Lieu Song · CC BY-SA 4.0 · Wikimedia Commons

There is a certain weekend comfort in an index that refuses to flinch. As Wall Street's momentum names lurched through a sell-off, the Straits Times Index closed around 5,244 on Thursday — up roughly 3.5% on the month and more than 30% on the year.1 The composition is the story: banks, REITs and industrials, the unglamorous cash-generators that pay you to wait. When the world rotates out of expensive growth, Singapore's bourse is where some of that money comes home.

For a globally diversified investor this is less a stock tip than a lesson in correlation. A world equity fund is now heavily tilted toward a handful of American mega-caps; a market like this one — safe-haven flows, AAA bonds, predictable policy — behaves differently, which is precisely the point of holding both. The caveat is valuation: an index at records is not cheap, and the yield, not the price, is doing the heavy lifting.

Notably, the strength is not a strong-currency story. The Singdollar has quietly softened to about 1.292 to the US dollar, near its weakest of 2026 — a reminder that the STI's record and the currency can point in opposite directions at once.2

The neighbourhood grows

Prime Minister Lawrence Wong spent Thursday and Friday on an official visit to Timor-Leste, ASEAN's newest member — conferred the Order of Timor-Leste in Dili.3 It is an easy story to overlook, but a bigger, more integrated bloc on the doorstep is exactly the kind of slow, structural tailwind that outlasts any quarter's headlines.

Property · The Q2 flash
The Market Rotates to the Top
Private-home price growth halved in the second quarter — but the average hides a split: prime and landed strengthened while the mass market gave back gains, all with a supply wave now loading.

URA's flash estimate, out 1 July, put private residential prices up just 0.5% quarter-on-quarter in Q2 — a marked cool-down from Q1's 0.9%, and the softest pace in seven quarters.1 But the headline masks a rotation worth a weekend read. The Core Central Region — the prime districts — rose 2.0%, the only positive non-landed segment, despite no new launches. Landed homes reversed a slide to gain 2.6%. Meanwhile the Rest of Central Region fell 1.4% and the Outside Central Region, red-hot in Q1, slipped 0.2%.1

The mass-market softness is partly a base effect and partly realistic pricing at the quarter's launches: Tengah Garden Residences, the first private condo in Tengah, cleared 99% of units at about $2,120 psf on its launch weekend, while Hudson Place Residences moved at a median $2,467 psf — 2% below the preceding Media Circle project.1 Take-up stayed strong even as prices eased: developers sold roughly 2,100 new units, up on Q1.2

A flight to the top: the money moving is moving up-market, just as a decade's worth of supply prepares to flood the middle.

Two other signals reward attention. The HDB resale index fell 0.3% — a second straight quarterly decline, the first back-to-back dip in nearly seven years — hinting at softer upgrading power beneath the private market.2 And the pipeline is enormous: the 2H2026 Confirmed List adds 4,745 units, taking the full-year Government Land Sales supply to 9,320 — over 50% above the ten-year average, with some 61,000 units due to complete in the next few years.3

The read-through for an owner

Prime holds; mass cools. If you own in the CCR or landed, the quarter went your way; if you're an OCR upgrader, the queue is thinning and the discount to city-fringe is narrowing.

Supply is the story now. A 9,320-unit Confirmed List is the government leaning against froth — good for buyers, a brake on sellers.

Rates? A one-line delta: 3-month SORA still near 1%, still supportive — but no longer the headline it was.

The Chatter

CBRE's Tricia Song holds a full-year forecast of 2–4% price growth and 7,500–8,500 new-home sales — a deliberate moderation from 2025's 10,815, on fewer launches and pent-up demand normalising. Developers, tellingly, are still bidding up prime land: benchmark tenders at Peck Hay Road, Dunearn Road and River Valley all cleared above 2025 comparables.

— CBRE Research; ERA; 99.co (1–4 Jul)

Technology · The state as shareholder
Washington Wants a Stake
OpenAI has floated handing the US government 5% of itself — worth some $42.6 billion — as the price of political peace. It is the clearest sign yet that frontier compute is being treated as a national asset.

According to the Financial Times, Sam Altman has proposed giving the United States government a 5% equity stake in OpenAI — roughly $42.6 billion against the company's $852 billion March valuation — as part of a broader scheme in which every leading American lab would cede the same share into a public fund modelled on the Alaska Permanent Fund.1 The idea is conceptual, early, and would likely require an act of Congress; Google, Meta and Anthropic have not signed on, and a source says the administration and Anthropic have held no such talks.1

The framing is generous — "sharing the upside of AI with the public" — but the timing is instructive. The proposal surfaced days after Washington delayed OpenAI's GPT-5.6 launch at the government's request, and after Anthropic spent much of June with its most capable Claude models offline worldwide under the first US export controls ever applied to a model rather than a chip; access was restored on 1 July.2 Google, Microsoft and xAI have separately agreed to let the government test models before release.

The template is already built: Washington took 9.9% of Intel, ~15% of MP Materials, and a cut of Nvidia and AMD's China sales. Now it is being offered the crown jewel.

Set against that backdrop, an equity stake looks less like philanthropy and more like the logical next move in an industrial policy that treats compute the way earlier eras treated steel, oil and uranium. Senator Bernie Sanders has gone further, filing a bill seeking 50% of US AI firms' voting shares for a $7 trillion fund; Vice-President Vance says the President prefers equity to cash payouts.3 Meanwhile SoftBank closed a second $10 billion tranche of its OpenAI follow-on — cash from private capital funding the compute, equity to the public managing the politics.3

The Chatter

Reaction split cleanly. Some read it as a bribe dressed as a dividend — the same administration that regulates these firms being handed shares in them. Others note the elegance: it costs OpenAI no cash, sidesteps a stalled AI-tax debate, and locks in Washington's position ahead of an IPO now reportedly slipping to 2027.

— SiliconANGLE, Tom's Hardware, CNBC (2–3 Jul)

The Sceptic

Anti-industrial-policy conservatives are horrified; a government that owns a slice of the frontier has every incentive to protect it from competition. And the deeper worry is downstream: while Washington negotiates equity, cheaper open-weight Chinese models keep improving with fewer guardrails.

The Home Lab · The glut that helps you
When Even Meta Has Spare GPUs
Meta wants to rent out its excess compute. For the self-hoster, the overbuild at the frontier may be the first crack of daylight in a brutal year for RAM and silicon.

The most quietly consequential tech story of the week was not a chip but a landlord. Meta unveiled "Meta Compute," a plan to sell its surplus AI infrastructure to outside customers — turning a $115–135 billion capex line into revenue.1 The market read the subtext instantly: if one of the largest buyers of GPUs has more than it needs, the supply-demand story that justified premium pricing may be tipping. Meta jumped ~9%; the GPU-cloud pure-plays cratered — CoreWeave −14%, Nebius −17% — and memory names sold off hard.1

For a home lab, a glut at the top is a gift at the bottom. It is the mechanism by which yesterday's data-centre silicon becomes tomorrow's second-hand bargain, and by which the RAMpocalypse this desk tracked last month — DRAM up 300–600% off the lows — eventually loosens. Nobody should expect relief tomorrow: South Korea just launched a $584 billion push with Samsung and SK hynix, and Anthropic is reportedly in talks with Samsung to build a custom chip.2 But the direction of travel matters.

The self-hoster's real windfall, though, is software. The open-weight wave keeps making a 16–32GB box genuinely useful, and — as the sovereignty story on the facing page underlines — the case for owning your model, running locally and cloud-proof, has rarely been stronger. Own the weights; rent the watts only when you must.

The rotation, in one session: the hyperscaler up, the picks-and-shovels down.
The Chatter

"The Big Short" investor Michael Burry — short the trade — called Korea's chip splurge "the beginning of the end." Bulls counter that spare capacity today is just prudent over-provisioning for a demand curve that keeps bending up. Both can be right for a while.

— SiliconANGLE; MLQ (2–3 Jul)

The Kit · The screenless wars
The Tracker That Owns You
Oura's Ring 5 and Whoop's 5.0 are the best body-trackers ever made — arriving just as a 2026 backlash asks whether we've handed too much of ourselves to a subscription and a score.

The premium screenless tracker is having its finest hour and its first real reckoning at once. Oura's Ring 5 — shipping since 4 June, 40% smaller, with a re-engineered twelve-pathway sensor and a push into GLP-1 and "Health Radar" insights — is the most passive, most accurate ring yet.1 Whoop's 5.0 answers with a fourteen-day battery and the sharpest strain-and-recovery coaching in consumer wearables; the MG tier adds FDA-cleared ECG and AFib screening. For an Oura-and-Whoop household, both are genuinely excellent.

And yet the discourse has curdled. A widely shared TechRadar essay this week argued that wearable tech is sliding "from cool to surveillance-capitalism dreck" — as Oura, Whoop, Garmin and Meta all move features behind subscriptions, and as an always-on ring quietly becomes a thing that watches you rather than a thing you own.2 The cultural tell was a resurfaced clip of a podcaster lamenting that two glasses of wine had "ruined three days" of his recovery data — and being roundly mocked for it.

The question a 2026 wearable poses is no longer "how am I sleeping?" but "who is keeping the answer, and what did it cost me to know?"

The healthy reframe, and the one this desk keeps landing on: read the multi-week trend, not the nightly number; own your data where you can; and don't let a score run your wedding. The most optimised thing you can do with a recovery metric is occasionally ignore it and dance.

The Chatter

The subscription creep is now the whole conversation: Google's screenless "Fitbit Air" strips community features and pipes advice through a paid AI coach — one that testers say dispenses the occasional "unhinged" workout. The counter-movement is small but real: rings like Ultrahuman's, sold without a mandatory subscription, are cited constantly in the threads.

— TechRadar; T3; Gadgetflow (Jun–Jul)

Connected Home · HA 2026.7
A House That Explains Itself
Home Assistant's July release makes a local-first home both easier to build and — at last — easy to debug. For a Lutron migrant, the moment to move keeps getting more inviting.

Home Assistant 2026.7 landed on 1 July with a title that doubles as a thesis: "automations that speak your language."1 The headline change graduates purpose-based triggers from the Labs sandbox to the default editor — so instead of knowing a sensor's exact entity ID, you search "Motion," pick a room, and the system watches every motion sensor in that area. "More power, less complexity," as lead developer Frenck puts it. For a newcomer migrating off Lutron, it collapses the intimidating middle of the learning curve.2

The quieter, deeper change is under the hood. The Python Matter server has been rewritten in TypeScript as matter.js — now final — bringing Matter 1.5.1 compliance, faster device reconnection, a Thread network visualiser, and, crucially, native diagnostic sensors: Reboot Count, Uptime, Boot Reason, plus Hardware, Radio and Network fault flags and Wi-Fi RSSI.1 These answer the question every Matter owner has muttered at a vanished smart plug — was it a power blip, a firmware crash, or the mesh? — locally, with no vendor app and no cloud.

The whole point of a local-first home is that when something breaks, the house can tell you why — without phoning a server you don't control.

It rhymes with the week's larger theme. The same instinct that's driving people back to analog childhoods and to trackers they actually own is the one that makes a cloud-proof home feel less like hobbyism and more like prudence. Matter 1.5.1 also unlocks cameras, doorbells and closures; a dedicated IR/RF settings pane continues last month's work pulling decades-old "dumb" devices into the fold.

The Chatter

Power users are mostly delighted and slightly wary. The intent editor abstracts away the entity model that veterans learned to love; a few worry the "friendly" layer hides the plumbing they occasionally need. Consensus in the forums: keep areas tidy, and the new triggers are a clear win.

— Smart Home Junkie; XDA; Matter Alpha (Jul)

A Hundred Years of Keeping the Water Out
Rolex's Oyster case turns 100 — an object built for one dull, durable purpose. It's the right anniversary for a weekend about homecoming: the things that simply keep working while everything else rotates.

Watchmaking spends a fortune chasing the exotic; the centenary worth marking this year honours the mundane. In 2026 the Rolex Oyster case — the first waterproof wristwatch case, patented in 1926 — turns one hundred, and the brand celebrated with an Oyster Perpetual whose lacquer dial spells "Rolex" in a mosaic of more than ten colours, from about US$6,750.1 The playful dial is beside the point. The point is the case: a sealed, screw-down promise that the thing on your wrist will keep working after a swim, a decade, a life.

That is a fitting object for a homecoming issue. In a market whose auction rooms just set records for independent craft (No. 34), and whose store-of-value obsessions swing from gold to bitcoin week to week, there is something clarifying about a design whose whole ambition is to not change — to hold. The luxury here is durability, not novelty; the flex is that it will outlast the trend.

The Chatter

Grand Seiko's 2026 "Ushio" Ultra-Fine-Accuracy diver (±20s a year, ~US$12,400) and its hand-engraved Micro Artist pieces prove the Japanese houses are climbing the same durability-as-luxury ladder — the soft-yen backdrop (¥ still near multi-decade lows) only sweetening the case for buying a keeper on your next trip north.

— Oracle Time; Gear Patrol (2026)

The Escape · buy the experience

If the object this week argues for keeping, the escape argues for spending — on the thing you can't resell. The reader's calendar has an unusually easy option hiding in plain sight: a great city a five-hour hop away, cheaper than almost anywhere comparable, and photo-rich in a way that flatters a Leica. This weekend's Grand Tour goes deep on Taipei (p21); read it as the Good Life's travel pick too — camera streets, hot springs, and the best night-market food per dollar in Asia.

The counsel is the same one the durability object makes, inverted: own the watch, but rent the memory-making. One holds its value by lasting; the other by being spent.

The soft-yen window

With the yen still weak and Japan's tax-free reform bedding in, a keeper bought in Ginza or Kanazawa remains a live idea — cross-referenced to the Macro Desk's FX read, p15.

The Summer of the Homecoming
Christopher Nolan drags Homer's oldest story — a man trying to get home — into IMAX 70mm, and the demand is Oppenheimer-plus. It anchors the most crowded, most confident July at the box office since before the pandemic.

On 17 July, Nolan releases The Odyssey — the Trojan-War aftermath rendered as a summer tentpole, with Matt Damon, Tom Holland and Zendaya in the tunics.1 The tell is the presale: advance IMAX demand has outpaced Oppenheimer's 2023 run, and at one New York cinema, pairs of 70mm tickets have resold for up to US$1,500.2 There is a lovely rhyme in it — the culture's most technically maximalist filmmaker choosing, of all stories, the one about the long way home.

He lands into a genuinely healthy summer. 2026's domestic box office is running ~13% ahead of last year and pacing within a whisker of 2019; the four-month season should top $4 billion for only the second time since the pandemic.3 Toy Story 5 (No. 34) opened to a franchise-record $160M and keeps earning; two weeks after Nolan, on 31 July, Sony's Spider-Man: Brand New Day arrives with sell-outs already reported.2

The most maximalist filmmaker alive chose the oldest homecoming story ever told — and the tickets are reselling for the price of a flight.

Supergirl, demoted here under the three-consecutive-lead rule, faded after a soft open — a one-line close. The season's real oddity: A24's Backrooms and Focus's Obsession, both by directors who built their craft on YouTube, have become breakout hits, with Obsession on course to clear $240M domestic without ever being the week's number one — a feat matched only by Oppenheimer, Sing and My Big Fat Greek Wedding.2

The Chatter

Exhibitors are giddy and a little superstitious: "week after week, it's not been just one film." The nervous question is whether Nolan and Spider-Man are genuine strength or quarter-end sugar — and whether the YouTube-auteur horror boom is a fluke or the new pipeline.

— Variety; Screendollars; AMC's Adam Aron (Jun)

The Family Desk · The analog turn
The Analog Summer
Dumbphones, board games, a bike ride to a friend's house: 2026's most talked-about parenting trend is a deliberate turn away from screens and toward the real — and the summer holiday is where it gets tested.

The buzz-phrase in parenting circles this year is "going analog," and for once the trend and the evidence point the same way. Families are reaching for old-school defaults — landline-style "tin-can" phones for kids, board games, VHS nights, and, above all, unsupervised outdoor time — as a conscious push-back against passive screens and the AI-saturated internet.1 It isn't Luddism; it's a reset of defaults, and it dovetails with a run of research this desk has tracked, from last month's case for the boredom of an empty afternoon to the developmental value of a little risk.

The summer holiday is where the theory meets the calendar. Children's health behaviours — physical activity, diet, sleep, mood — reliably slip over the long break; a 2026 Delphi study of "levelling-up summer" programmes found that some structure helps, provided it leaves room for genuine autonomy.2 The synthesis isn't "ban the iPad" versus "let them roam." It's the same line as last month: structure the unstructured. Anchor the day — a swim, a chore, a shared meal — then defend the long, undirected middle where a bored eleven-year-old invents her own afternoon.

Analog isn't anti-technology. It's the small, unglamorous work of choosing the default — and then getting out of the way.

For a tween specifically, the stakes are also social and physical: girls commonly enter puberty at nine to eleven, and the research keeps returning to the same protective factor — high-quality, low-drama conversation with a parent, which predicts better decision-making far more reliably than any gadget ban.3

The Chatter

The "dumbphone" is no longer fringe. 2026's parenting trend round-ups put brick phones, wired home lines and deliberately "low-tech summers" near the top of the list — and, tellingly, parents who grew up online are visibly tiring of performative "gentle parenting," trading the scripts for plainer authority paired with warmth. The mood is less anti-tech than pro-real.

Don't be alarmist · the practical version

The alarmist reading of "analog" is that a phone or a screen is poison and abstinence is the cure. That's neither true nor useful. A capable smartphone is, for many families, also how an eleven-year-old reaches you, finds her way home, and keeps up with friends — and a "dumbphone" can be a sensible bridge rather than a moral victory. The move that actually pays off is duller and harder: deciding, per household, what the defaults are — where devices live overnight, what the first hour after school looks like, which afternoons are deliberately empty — and then holding them lightly enough to bend on a rainy day.

Watch for the difference between developmental boredom, which is generative and passes, and a persistent flatness that doesn't lift with company or a change of scene. The first is the raw material of a good summer. The second is a conversation, and — if it lingers — a reason to talk to a professional. Neither is a reason to panic, and neither is fixed by a gadget.

The independence piece deserves its own line, because it is where "analog" does real developmental work. A tween biking to a friend's house, ordering her own bubble tea, or navigating a small errand alone is practising the competence that a supervised, screen-mediated childhood quietly postpones. The best-evidenced parental move is not surveillance but scaffolding — widen the leash in deliberate steps, then debrief warmly rather than interrogate. The communication, again, is the intervention.

The through-line to this whole issue is worth naming for the family too: the pull toward the real — a friend on a bike, a game on the table, a walk with no plan — is the same instinct showing up in grown-up life as a friendship revival (p17) and a return to homes and tools we actually control. The kids are just better at it.

Don't Be Alarmist

"Analog" is a default, not a purity test. Screens aren't the enemy; unexamined defaults are. A dumbphone can be a bridge; a board game is not a cure; and the best-evidenced protective factor for a tween remains an unhurried, non-judgemental conversation with a parent.

The Oldest Traveller Leaves
A year to the day after it was spotted, the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS — older than the Sun — turns for the exit, never to return. Its farewell science is a small marvel: a message from a colder, more ancient galaxy.
3I/ATLAS by Hubble, 21 July 2025 — a teardrop cocoon of dust; the streaks are background stars, smeared as the scope tracked the comet. 3I-ATLAS Hubble heic2509a.jpg · NASA, ESA, D. Jewitt et al. / ESA-Hubble · CC BY 4.0

It arrived a year ago — flagged on 1 July 2025 by the ATLAS survey in Chile — only the third confirmed object ever seen passing through our solar system from beyond it.1 Now 3I/ATLAS is on its way out for good, on a hyperbolic path that will carry it back into interstellar dark, never to return. Before it goes, it has left astronomers a parting gift of data.

Webb and the VLT caught its chemistry as it warmed past the Sun. The verdict, published in Nature Astronomy, is that this is something genuinely alien: a deuterium — "heavy water" — signature unlike any solar-system comet, plus fingerprints of methane, methanol, ethane and an unexplained trace of nickel vapour.2 The heavy-water ratio points to a birth in extreme cold; the object is estimated at ten to twelve billion years old — meaning it was already ancient when our Sun was born.

It formed in the deep cold of another star system, older than our Sun — and we got one year to say hello before it left forever.

And no, it isn't a spaceship. A SETI search through nearly 74 million radio signals turned up 211 candidates, every one of them human or satellite interference — ruling out any transmitter of even modest power.3 The romance is the science, not the rumour.

The Friendship Recession
The quiet crisis of midlife isn't romance — it's the slow evaporation of friendship, especially for men, especially for parents. The good news: unlike the weather, this one you can do something about.

The numbers are startling because they are so gradual. The share of American men with no close friends rose from 3% in 1990 to 15% by 2021 — a fivefold jump — and the share reporting at least six close friends fell by half.1 Women's circles shrank too, from a lower base. The Survey Center on American Life's Daniel Cox calls it a "friendship recession," and the causes are structural, not personal: longer work hours, geographic churn, later marriage, and the slow substitution of screens for the standing table.1

This is not a soft concern. The 2023 US Surgeon General's advisory placed loneliness alongside smoking and obesity as a mortality risk; social isolation is associated with a 29% higher risk of premature death — the oft-cited equivalent of fifteen cigarettes a day.2 And the longest-running study of human happiness — Harvard's, now 88 years in — keeps returning the same finding: the quality of your relationships predicts a long, good life better than wealth, fame, IQ or genes.3

Adult friendship doesn't end in a fight. It ends in a series of postponed coffees — which is exactly why a calendar beats good intentions.

For the reader most exposed — a partnered parent in his mid-forties — the trap is specific: childcare and work quietly consume the bandwidth friendship used to occupy, and the loss is invisible until it's large. The fix is unromantic. Because the causes are structural, the answer is too: recurring, low-effort systems — the standing monthly dinner, the walk on the same day, the group chat with a purpose — beat willpower every time.

The Sceptic

A caveat before you audit your social life at midnight: some of these figures are correlational, and a few real friends beat a wide, shallow network — Dunbar's work suggests the number that matters is small. This is a nudge, not a diagnosis. But the direction is clear enough to act on.

— from Cox (2021); Dunbar, Friends (2021)

Fitness · The evidence, revisited
The Zone-2 Myth, Examined
The internet's favourite longevity hack gets a cold-eyed review — and the verdict is liberating: build the base, but stop worshipping the zone. What survives the debate is an unglamorous, durable plan.

Zone 2 — the low, conversational aerobic pace — became gospel on the strength of its mitochondrial magic. A 2026 narrative review in Sports Medicine, with exercise scientist Brad Schoenfeld among the authors, asks the awkward question: is Zone 2 uniquely effective, or does its reputation outrun the data? Their answer: when total training volume is matched, higher-intensity work produces equal or greater mitochondrial adaptation.1 In other words, the base-building is real; the zone-worship is not.

This complicates — rather than overturns — the bookended week this desk floated last month. The polarised "mostly easy, a little hard" model still stands; what falls is the idea that a heart-rate number is doing something magic. For a time-crunched forty-something, that's freeing: you don't need a lab test and a strict zone to get most of the benefit. You need consistency, some genuinely hard efforts, and — the part the cardio debate keeps underselling — strength.

Build the aerobic base. Add a couple of genuinely hard efforts. Lift twice a week. Then stop optimising and go do it for a decade.

On strength, the number that keeps holding up is a June 2026 BJSM analysis of 147,000 adults: a "sweet spot" of about 90–120 minutes of resistance training a week for the biggest longevity return (No. 32) — muscle as an under-rated organ of aging well.2

The plan that survives the debate

Base: 3–4 easy aerobic sessions/wk — walk, ride, row. Don't fuss the zone.

Sharpen: 1–2 genuinely hard efforts (intervals, hills).

Lift: 2–3 strength sessions, ~90–120 min total.

Repeat for years. Consistency beats any single protocol.

The Sceptic

Two honest caveats: the Zone-2 studies are volume-matched and often in trained subjects, so for a beginner easy cardio (a brisk incline walk) is still the friendliest on-ramp. And your wearable's VO₂max estimate is a trend line, not a truth — read it the way you read your recovery score (p9). Not medical advice; clear new intensity with a clinician.

The Grand Tour · Taipei, Taiwan
The Great City an Easy Hour Away
Under five hours from Changi, cheaper than almost anywhere comparable, and quietly one of Asia's best cities for food, cameras and hot springs. Most Singaporeans give Taipei a long weekend. It deserves a week.
Taipei from Elephant Mountain — the short, sweaty climb the piece recommends for the postcard shot. Taipei Skyline 2022.06.29.jpg · Heeheemalu · CC BY-SA 2.0 · Wikimedia Commons

Taipei is the rare capital that over-delivers and under-charges. A world-class MRT knits together night markets, thousand-year temples, a mountain skyline and volcanic hot springs; a comfortable day runs roughly US$90–150, a fraction of Tokyo, Seoul or home.1 The most common planning mistake, the guides agree, is giving it too little time — a 48-hour stopover — or too much in the city when a couple of those days belong on day trips. Three days is the minimum to "get it"; five is the sweet spot.

When to go — and it isn't now

Be honest with yourself about the calendar: July and August are the wrong window. This is peak summer heat — 33–35°C and humid enough that locals compare it to standing in a rice cooker — and the heart of typhoon season, which peaks in August and can shut trails and flights.2 The city is famously typhoon-resilient (covered arcades, universal air-con, hot springs for a wet afternoon), so a summer trip is doable with buffer days — but the trip to plan is October to early December or March to April, when the humidity drops, the skies clear, and Taipei 101 finally emerges sharp against the mountains.2

Give it three days and you'll like it; give it five and you'll start plotting how to come back for a week.

Getting there & where to base

SIA, Scoot (Taoyuan is its Asian focus city), EVA and China Airlines all fly it in ~4h45; land at Taoyuan (TPE, Airport MRT into town) or the downtown Songshan (TSA).1 For this reader, base in Zhongshan (Japanese-era alleys, chic cafés, department stores) or leafy, foodie Da'an (Yongkang Street on your doorstep); Ximending for youthful buzz and proximity to the old city; Taipei Main Station if day trips are the priority.3

Getting around

Buy an EasyCard at any MRT station and stop thinking about it: it taps you onto the metro, buses, the Maokong gondola, most convenience stores and even some taxis. The MRT is clean, cheap, punctual and blessedly air-conditioned — the single best reason a summer trip is survivable. English signage is thorough; Google Maps handles the buses. Budget roughly NT$500 (~US$16) a day of stored value for a couple who ride a lot, more if you're day-tripping to the coast.1

The experiences that earn the trip

The set-pieces are real: Taipei 101 and the short, sweaty hike up Elephant Mountain for the postcard skyline; the incense hush of Longshan Temple; the National Palace Museum's imperial hoard; the Maokong gondola up to the tea plantations. The classic loop is Jiufen — the lantern-lit mountain village — usually paired with Shifen's sky lanterns and waterfall, and Yehliu's wind-carved rock. What the forums actually say

The Long Read · An essay
The Long Way Home
Nolan drags Homer's oldest story into an IMAX; a comet older than the Sun turns for the exit; a culture quietly turns back toward the real. This week, everything was about coming home — and the surprise is that home was never a place.

The oldest story we still tell is about a man trying to get home. Not to conquer, not to discover — to return. The Odyssey spends twenty-four books and ten years getting one middle-aged veteran back to a small, rocky island and a wife who has been holding it together without him. That Christopher Nolan — the most technically maximalist filmmaker alive — has chosen this, of all epics, to render in 70-millimetre IMAX, and that people are paying scalpers the price of a flight to see it, tells you the appetite is not for spectacle alone.1 It is for the shape of the thing. Departure, trial, and the long way back.

The Greeks had a word for it: nostos, the homecoming — the root that, twenty-six centuries later, a homesick Swiss medical student named Johannes Hofer would splice with algos, pain, to coin "nostalgia" in his 1688 Basel dissertation.2 Hofer was describing a real and sometimes fatal affliction among Swiss mercenaries far from their valleys; a particular herding tune, the Kuhreihen, was said to be banned in the ranks lest it kill the men with longing.2 Homesickness as a diagnosable disease. We have softened the word into a fondness for old songs, but its origin is severe: the pull of home was once strong enough to be lethal.

The oldest story we still tell is not about conquest or discovery. It is about a middle-aged man trying, for ten years, to get back to a small rocky island.

You can feel that same pull, gentler and un-lethal, running under this whole edition. It surfaces as a cultural turn toward the real — the thing you can touch, own, and be present for. In the Family Desk (p13), parents are reaching for analog childhoods: board games, bikes, a bored afternoon with no screen in it. In Love & Life (p17), the quiet crisis of midlife turns out to be not romance but the slow evaporation of friendship, and the cure is a homecoming to the standing table. In the Connected Home (p10) and The Kit (p9), the instinct is sovereignty: a house that runs on your own hardware, a tracker whose data you actually own. Even the market (p15) came home this week — the money rotating out of the speculative and back toward the broad, dull, durable economy.

Four different desks, one gesture: a turning-back. The question worth an essay is why now — and what, exactly, we think we are returning to.

Here is the twist Homer buries in the middle of the poem, and it is the whole argument. Odysseus does reach Ithaca. He wins the hall back, he passes the test of the marriage bed — the one carved into a living olive tree, rooted and immovable, a home that cannot be faked or moved.1 And then, in the underworld, the prophet Tiresias has already told him the catch: reaching home is not the end. He must set out again, carrying an oar inland until he meets people so far from the sea they mistake it for a winnowing-fan.1 The homecoming is not a finish line. It is a turn in a longer road.

And what was Penelope doing all those years? Weaving. Every day she worked at a shroud on the loom, and every night she unravelled what she had woven, to hold off the suitors — buying time by refusing to finish.1 It is the most quietly radical image in the poem. Home is not the completed cloth. Home is the daily act of weaving and re-weaving — the thing you do again each morning precisely because it is never done.

Home is not the finished cloth. It is Penelope's loom — woven each day and unravelled each night, a thing that only exists in the doing.

This reframes everything the issue is reaching for. The analog afternoon is not a state you achieve; it is a default you choose again on Tuesday, and again the Tuesday after. The lost friendship does not come back because you resolved, once, to be a better friend; it comes back through the recurring dinner, the same day each month, the low-effort system that beats willpower (p17). The local-first home is not a project you complete; it is a house you keep, patch and understand. Even the index fund the reader holds — the boring global tracker at the centre of the Macro Desk — is homecoming as practice: you feed it on the days nothing happens, and the compounding is entirely in the repetition. None of these is an arrival. All of them are looms.

Which is why the counter-image matters so much. In Curiosities (p16), the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS — older than the Sun, born in the deep cold of another star system — swings once past our Sun and leaves for good, on an orbit that never closes.1 It is the anti-Odysseus: a traveller with no nostos, no Ithaca, no loom. We got one year to say hello and it is already gone forever. And in the harder register of The World (p3), a coffin is being carried the long way to Mashhad — homecoming in its oldest and most final sense, the body returned to the birthplace, the one journey every one of us makes exactly once.2

Set the closed loop against the open curve and the value of the return becomes obvious. The one-way roads — the comet's, the mortal's — are what make the round trips precious. A homecoming is not guaranteed by the physics; most of the universe is the comet's story, not Penelope's. Which is exactly why the daily weaving is not sentimental housekeeping but a kind of defiance: the deliberate construction of a loop in a cosmos that mostly runs one way.

The one-way roads are what make the round trips precious. Home is a loop you build by hand, against a universe that mostly runs one way.

So why now? Perhaps because the last few years taught us the opposite lesson — that everything could be outsourced, streamed, optimised and held in a cloud we don't own, and that the frictionless life is also a strangely homeless one. The turn this week is a correction: back to the touchable afternoon, the friend you actually see, the tools you actually control, the fundamentals a portfolio actually rests on. It rhymes with older corrections — the "return to normalcy" an exhausted America voted for in 1920 after its own long war.3 Every era eventually walks its oar inland and comes home.

You could see the smaller version of it play out on a trading screen this week. For two years the market's story was a handful of enormous names and a single, thrilling idea; this week the money finally rotated back toward the broad, unglamorous economy — the equal-weight index and the small-caps making records while the megacaps caught their breath (p15). Call it a homecoming to fundamentals: mean reversion is just nostos with a ticker. The boring anchor at the centre of a sensible portfolio was, all along, the loom you kept feeding on the days nothing happened.

The reader who keeps this magazine will notice the callbacks: the "small self" that awe produces (No. 33), the way big changes arrive gradually and then suddenly (No. 34). This is their sequel. If awe is the antidote to a self-magnifying feed, and if change has two speeds, then nostos is the discipline that survives both — the practice of returning, on the ordinary days, to the few things that are actually yours.

Bashō, setting out in 1694 on a walk he did not expect to survive, got there first and said it plainest: the journey itself is home.4 Not the hut he left. Not the shrine he was walking toward. The daily act of going — and, wherever he stopped, of making it home for the night. Odysseus finally understood the same thing, oar over his shoulder, walking away from Ithaca to secure it. Home was never the island. It was the weaving. Do it again tomorrow.

The Last Word
The Oar Over the Shoulder

A traveller from another star passed through this week and left for good; a filmmaker spent a fortune to remind us that the hardest voyage in literature is the one back. Between the comet and the coffin, the homecoming and the loom, one small instruction survives: on the ordinary day, when nothing happens, return to the few things that are actually yours — the friend, the afternoon, the home you can hold. That is not nostalgia. That is the work.
