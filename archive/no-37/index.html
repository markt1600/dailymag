The World · Iran & the Gulf
The sixty-day peace lasted twenty
Three tankers burned on Tuesday; eighty targets burned overnight; by Wednesday the President was calling the deal "over" from a NATO podium. The ceasefire signed on 17–18 June has collapsed in the very week Iran buried the leader whose death began the war.

It unravelled in roughly forty-eight hours. On Tuesday 7 July, Iran struck three commercial vessels in and near the Strait of Hormuz — a Qatari LNG carrier and a Saudi oil tanker among them — and the US-led Joint Maritime Information Center raised its threat assessment to "severe."1 Overnight, CENTCOM answered with strikes on more than eighty targets inside Iran, including Kharg Island, the terminal handling the bulk of Iranian crude (Mr Trump said the oil facilities were spared); Washington revoked the crude-sales waiver and reimposed sanctions.2

On Wednesday, from the NATO summit in Ankara, the President declared the 60-day framework "over," threatened to reinstate the naval blockade, and — most strikingly — to "take over Kharg Island… there's not a thing they could do about it."3 Iran's foreign minister called the strikes "wholly unprovoked"; President Pezeshkian warned that "global order will be shaken." Whether the Doha channel survives was left ambiguous — Mr Trump said it was for the negotiators, then softened: "I don't think it's going to start again."4

The bitter arithmetic is that the deal was working slowly — which may be why it died. Doha had produced a communication channel and a meeting on releasing $6bn in frozen Iranian funds; Hormuz transits had crawled back to ~34 ships a day by Sunday.5 The market's verdict was instant: Brent jumped 5.2 per cent to $78.02, the war premium priced straight back in (see Macro, p15). And the strikes landed mid-funeral: the rites for Ayatollah Khamenei — assassinated 28 February — end today with burial at Mashhad, while his son and successor Mojtaba, wounded in the same strike, has not appeared once. Iran has crowned a leader its public has neither seen nor heard.6

The World · The Wider Board
A quarter-final without hosts, a summit without harmony
Brazil is out, the United States is out, and the World Cup's last eight is nearly all-European. In Ankara, NATO's leaders restated their vows while their most powerful member ran a war from the corridor.

The Cup. The round of sixteen ended the hosts' tournament and one of football's oldest certainties. Norway beat Brazil 2–1 on Sunday — Erling Haaland scoring twice in the final eleven minutes for his sixth and seventh goals of the tournament — to reach the first quarter-final in the country's history and hand Brazil its earliest exit since 1990.1 A day later Belgium dismantled the United States 4–1 in Seattle; coach Mauricio Pochettino declined the ready-made excuse: "We were not good enough today."2 Spain edged Portugal 1–0 in Arlington, England survived Mexico 3–2, Morocco swept Canada 3–0, France ground past Paraguay 1–0, Argentina needed a 92nd-minute Enzo Fernández header to see off Egypt 3–2, and Switzerland outlasted Colombia on penalties.

The last eight: France–Morocco tonight in Foxborough (a 2022 semi-final rematch), Spain–Belgium at SoFi on Friday, and Saturday's double bill of Norway–England in Miami and Argentina–Switzerland in Kansas City.3 Of the eight, only Argentina and Morocco interrupt an otherwise European bracket at an American party — the "other party," as FIFA's president keeps calling it, of the republic's 250th summer.

Ankara. All 32 leaders convened at the Beştepe compound for a summit chaired by Mark Rutte and overshadowed by the man running the Iran escalation from its margins (p3). Mr Trump attacked Spain as "a terrible partner in NATO"; Hegseth announced a review of the entire US force posture in Europe, to conclude in "six months or less."4 The communiqué reaffirmed that an attack on one is an attack on all; the corridor conversation was about which one, and whether all still means all.

Rehabilitation by instalments. The IOC on Tuesday provisionally restored Russia's Olympic Committee, opening a path to Los Angeles 2028 — flag and anthem still banned, each federation to decide its own terms. Kyiv's foreign ministry called it "a troubling signal for the entire international community" — delivered the same week Russian missile waves killed at least 22 Ukrainians and Ukrainian drones reached 3,000 km into Siberia (see In brief).5

Paris. An appeals court upheld Marine Le Pen's EU-funds embezzlement conviction — three years (two suspended, one at home under electronic monitor) and a €100,000 fine — but trimmed the office ban so she stays eligible for the April 2027 presidential race. She says she will run; a further appeal suspends the ankle tag in the interim. France may get a frontrunner campaigning in an electronic bracelet.6

Singapore · Transport & the Cost of Moving
The loop closes on Sunday
Seventeen years after its first stations opened, the Circle Line finally becomes a circle: Keppel, Cantonment and Prince Edward Road open 12 July. In the same week, the right to drive a car to any of them cost more than it ever has.

There is a satisfying finality to it: on Sunday 12 July, Circle Line Stage 6 opens its three stations — Keppel, Cantonment and Prince Edward Road — and the orange line on the map stops being a horseshoe. The 4 km closing arc links HarbourFront to Marina Bay, completing a 39 km, 33-station loop that now interchanges with every other line on the network; roughly 17 years separate the first CCL passengers (2009) from the last brick.1 The public got a preview on 4 July — free rides, open house — and the crowds treated it like what it is: the rare piece of national infrastructure that finishes. For the reader's map, the practical wins are the southern waterfront: Cantonment sits by the old Tanjong Pagar railway land and Keppel serves the districts the Greater Southern Waterfront plan (No. 36) will spend two decades filling in.

The counterpoint arrived on Wednesday, at the first July COE tender. Premiums rose in every category. A Category A certificate — the mass-market car — closed at a record S$129,000, past October's all-time high; Cat B hit S$130,889, the Open category S$129,801, and commercial vehicles set their own record at S$95,000. Even motorcycles firmed past S$10,000.2 The juxtaposition is the story: in one week the state finished a loop that makes the car less necessary, and the market priced the car further out of reach than it has ever been. A household weighing a replacement car against two EZ-Link cards has rarely had the arithmetic drawn so starkly.

The exuberance is not confined to the forecourt. The STI, which set a record 5,244 last Friday, kept climbing — 5,256 on Monday and roughly 5,379 by Wednesday's close (a provisional print), intraday above 5,400 for the first time.3 Asset prices at records, certificates at records, and a power tariff up 17 per cent this month (No. 36): the Little Red Dot's ledger is booming and expensive at once — a theme the Macro Desk picks up on p15.

The state finished a loop that makes the car less necessary; the market priced the car further away than ever.

In Parliament on Monday, the transport ministry told members the network the loop joins is holding its standard — the rail system has met its 1-million-train-km-between-failures reliability target — and that the Singapore Rail Academy's mandate will expand to standardise training across all operators, with LTA co-funding upskilling in predictive maintenance. The pipeline behind CCL6 rolls on.4

Property · Launches & the Ladder
Turf City returns at three thousand a foot
The first private homes in the old Turf City in a generation preview tomorrow at close to S$3,000 psf — while Lentor sells the same week at S$2,350, and a record 188 HDB flats changed hands above a million dollars in June. The ladder is stretching at both ends.

Dunearn House opens its showsuite tomorrow, and with it a new market: 380 units by Frasers Property, CSC Land and Sekisui House, the first private condominium of the Bukit Timah Turf City transformation — the first new launch in the old Swiss Club orbit in some 33 years. The indicative pricing is the headline: two-bedders from S$1.475M (about S$2,799 psf) and three-bedders from S$2.597M (S$2,978 psf), with booking day set for 25 July.1 District 11, the school belt, and a precinct masterplanned for two decades of build-out: the developers are asking the market to pay tomorrow's neighbourhood prices for its first mover — the same long-fuse supply logic as the Jurong Lake District white site tendered last week (No. 36).

The same fortnight offers the control experiment. Kingsford's Lentor Gardens Residences — 502 units across four towers — previewed on 4 July at an indicative average of S$2,350 psf, with bookings from 18 July. Kingsford won the land at S$920 psf ppr in April 2025, among the lowest in the Lentor enclave, which leaves more margin room than most of its neighbours had.2 Two previews, S$600-odd psf apart, will say a great deal about how much premium the market will still pay for postcode and promise in a quarter when the URA flash showed prices barely rising (+0.5%, No. 35) and buyers rotating to the top end.

Below the private ladder, the public one keeps producing millionaires: 188 resale flats crossed S$1M in June — a monthly record, 8.8 per cent of all resale deals, led by Bukit Merah and Toa Payoh (28 each), with a S$1.65M five-roomer at SkyTerrace @ Dawson at the top of the pile. Overall resale prices were flat on the month.3 A flat market with a fattening million-dollar tail is not a contradiction; it is the CCR-and-landed "flight to the top" (No. 35) reflected in public housing's premium tier.

Two previews, six hundred dollars a foot apart, will price the premium on promise.

For the owner's file: MinLaw has confirmed the en-bloc consent thresholds (80/80 for estates over ten years old) are under review, with no timeline — industry voices float 75/75, and a lower tier for 50-year-old estates. Watch it: any loosening re-arms the collective-sale cycle that supply-side policy spent 2018–2025 calming.4 And next Wednesday, 15 July, the Bayshore Drive GLS tender closes — the East Coast price benchmark this desk has tracked since June. SORA-linked mortgage rates remain near their lows; nothing new to say.

Technology · The AI Trade's Earnings Test
A nineteen-fold profit, and it wasn't enough
Samsung's operating profit multiplied nineteen times over — and the stock fell seven per cent, torching chip shares worldwide, two days before SK hynix prices the largest US listing a foreign company has ever attempted. "Priced for perfection" just met its first earnings season.

On Tuesday, Samsung Electronics posted the sort of preliminary quarter that would once have been a national holiday: operating profit of ₩89.4 trillion (about US$58.4bn), roughly nineteen times the year before, on record DRAM, HBM and NAND prices — ahead of consensus. The stock fell as much as 10 per cent intraday and closed down 6.9.1 The revenue line missed, and the buy side had quietly marked expectations far above the published bar. Contagion was immediate: the Philadelphia semiconductor index dropped 5.7 per cent to a four-week low, Intel lost 9, AMD 8, Micron 5, and the memory complex slid formally into bear-market territory.2

The same morning supplied the demand-side scare: Reuters reported that DeepSeek — the Chinese lab whose training-cost shock opened 2025 — is designing its own inference chip, in talks with design houses, foundries and memory suppliers, to route around Nvidia and Huawei at once.3 It joins OpenAI's Broadcom-built "Jalapeno" and Anthropic's rumoured silicon in the customer-turned-competitor column. The export-control chessboard (Nos. 08–24) has produced its logical endgame piece: a sanctioned lab building sovereign silicon so that nobody's licence — American or Chinese — sits between it and inference.

Into this walks SK hynix. The bookbuild for its Nasdaq ADR closed a day early on Wednesday, multiple times oversubscribed, for a raise of up to US$28.07bn that would eclipse Alibaba's 2014 record as the largest US listing by a foreign company; trading starts tomorrow under the ticker SKHY.4 The tension is exquisite: the book is stuffed while the Seoul shares trade ~17 per cent below their July start. Friday's open (No. 36's "actual listing" peg, delivered) is the cleanest referendum yet on whether the memory supercycle is a cycle or a belief.

When a nineteen-fold profit surge disappoints, the disappointment is the data.

Two Washington items complete the board. GPT-5.6 — OpenAI's Luna/Terra/Sol trio — goes public today after two weeks gated behind June's pre-release government review, launching first to about twenty vetted partners: the frontier now ships with a regulatory green light.5 And Apple signed its largest-ever US chip deal — more than $30bn to Broadcom through 2031 — even as it reportedly tests China's CXMT DRAM for China-market devices. Supply-chain bifurcation on one balance sheet.6

The Home Lab · Sovereignty at Desk Scale
Even the cloud has a rush hour now
No shiny new model dropped this week — and that is the story. DeepSeek will charge double at peak hours from mid-July, Ukraine is procuring AI it can run "without provider control," and the RAM that powers the alternative keeps getting dearer. The case for owning your weights is being made by everyone but the owners.

Start with the pricing signal. DeepSeek's V4 — the frontier-chasing release due mid-July — arrives with something no big lab has tried before: peak and off-peak API pricing, with peak-hour calls billed at roughly double, and the old model names retired after 24 July.1 It is an honest admission that inference is a utility with a load curve — and utilities have rush hours, brownouts and tariffs (Singapore readers need no reminder this month). The machine under your desk has no peak pricing. Its meter runs (see Connected Home, No. 36), but its marginal token at 9pm costs what it cost at 9am.

Then the procurement signal. Ukraine's digital ministry said on Tuesday it will select AI models that can be "operated without provider control" — weights it can run on infrastructure it holds, immune to a vendor's kill switch, outage or export ruling.2 A state at war has reached, at national scale, the same conclusion this desk reached after June's model-level export controls briefly took Fable 5 offline (No. 35): access that can be revoked is not access; it is a subscription to someone else's policy. The EU's new cybersecurity-and-AI action plan, presented Tuesday, walks the same direction in Brussels prose.3

And the bill. The RAMpocalypse (No. 36) has worsened on every tracker: a 32GB DDR5-6000 kit that cost under $90 in early 2025 now averages around $529; entry 32GB kits sit above $300; and the shortage is now forecast to run to late 2027 as datacentre orders eat the fabs' output.4 Framework has taken to publishing memory-pricing bulletins like a wartime ration board. The hedge remains what it was: run small (the 16GB-class Gemma 4 and Qwen 3.6 tiers, No. 30), buy memory soldered into unified-memory machines priced before the squeeze, and treat VRAM as the scarce currency it is.

Access that can be revoked is not access; it is a subscription to someone else's policy.

The week's quieter tooling: Hugging Face shipped transformers v5 point-13 with a unified exporter across PyTorch, ONNX and ExecuTorch — the unglamorous plumbing that moves a model from the hub to the device in your hand — and Nvidia pushed its GR00T 1.7 open robotics model into LeRobot, putting a vision-language-action stack within a hobbyist's reach.5 For the self-hosting reader, one housekeeping note: reports circulated this week of a critical authentication bypass in Gitea being exploited against self-hosted instances. We could not independently verify the advisory by press time — but if a Git forge faces your WAN, patching it this weekend costs nothing either way.6

The Chatter

r/LocalLLaMA's mood this week was thrifty rather than giddy: threads on stretching 24GB cards through the shortage, on Strix Halo boxes as "the last cheap unified memory," and a running joke that the hottest local model of July is whatever you already downloaded. Zhipu's founder, meanwhile, claims GLM-5.2's successor will match Fable 5 by year-end — noted, unverified, and priced accordingly by the thread.7

The Kit · Value, With a Memory
The $99 earbuds that remember what you heard
Nothing's Ear (3a) lands at a hundred dollars with a party trick the flagships don't have: pinch, and the last sixty seconds of your life are saved. The reviews call it the best bargain in audio. The feature deserves a harder look than the price.

The spec sheet reads like a mid-tier flagship mugged in an alley: 12mm dynamic drivers, 45dB adaptive ANC, LDAC, IP54, ten hours a charge (42 with the case), a new XS tip size for small ears, and a little LED status matrix on a rounder case — for US$99.1 Gizmodo's verdict — "still Nothing's value king" — came with the detail that matters: the reviewer couldn't reliably hear the difference against the $179 Ear 3.2 In a week when the memory in everything got dearer (p8), a $99 product that embarrasses its own $179 sibling is the counter-cyclical story.

The genuinely new thing is 32MB of onboard flash put to novel use: "Audio Snapshot" — pinch a stem and the buds save the last sixty seconds of audio — and call recording up to two hours, synced through the Nothing X app. No mainstream earbud has shipped this. It is, depending on the light, a brilliant answer to "what did she just say to remember for Friday?" — or a wearable voice recorder that everyone around you doesn't know you're wearing. The etiquette (and in some places, the law) of one-party recording just moved into your ears; see the sceptic's box before pinching anything in a meeting.

Elsewhere on the bench: Samsung has dated its next Unpacked — 22 July, London, an invitation animated with a wider, shorter silhouette and the line "A New Shape Unfolds," which the rumour mill reads as a changed fold format; everything else is guesswork until the keynote, and we'll treat it that way.3 And the GoPro Mission 1 Pro's review wave has crested warmly — Engadget's 8.7/10 called it the best action-cam video on the market (1-inch 50MP sensor, 8K/60, three-plus hours at 4K/30), with the caveats of weight and a $699 sticker, $270 above the rivals it out-shoots.4

The Family Desk · Sport & the Sideline
Who really ends a childhood sport
The average child quits organised sport just before thirteen — and in a large national survey published this spring, girls who walked away were roughly twice as likely as boys to name a parent's sideline behaviour among the reasons. The ending, in other words, is often adult-authored.

The Aspen Institute's Project Play, with Utah State and Louisiana Tech universities, surveyed about 4,000 young people aged 10–17 and asked the former players why they stopped. The gender gap sits in the parental rows: girls who had quit were far likelier than boys to say a parent compared them to other players (25 vs 9 per cent), pressured them to play (24 vs 16), or argued with coaches and referees (13 vs 6).1 The average quitting age: a little under thirteen — squarely the age in this house.

The honest caveats first: this is self-report, published this spring rather than this week, and "cited a reason" is not "caused the outcome" — a child rationalising a decision may reach for the loudest available memory, and the loudest memory on many sidelines is a parent. But the asymmetry is the finding. Boys and girls play under the same referees; what differs, in this data, is how the parental voice lands — and the consistent read across youth-sport work is that girls carry evaluative pressure (being compared) harder than encouragement-pressure. A daughter does not hear "Mia scored twice" as information.

What makes this desk-worthy rather than guilt-column material is the leverage. Most levers in parenting research are weak and slow; the sideline is a rare strong, fast one — fully under adult control, changeable this Saturday, at zero cost. The prescription from Project Play's coaching partners is almost embarrassingly simple: cheer effort generically; save analysis for never; let the coach coach; and treat the car ride home as the weekend's highest-risk fixture — the script is six words: I love watching you play.2

The sideline audit — five minutes, once
Before this page induces a single pang of retrospective guilt: nearly every sporting parent has compared, coached from the rail, or debriefed in the car. The evidence says the pattern matters, not the incident — and the pattern is the easiest thing in this section to change.

A proportionate reading of the sports data starts here: one overheated touchline afternoon does not end a childhood sport, any more than one 3am World Cup quarter-final ends a sleep schedule (No. 36). Children are robust to incidents; what wears them is weather — the reliable knowledge that every match comes with an evaluation attached. The audit, then, is not "have I ever?" (yes; everyone) but "is it the default?" Three questions cover it. Does she hear a comparison after most games? Does the car ride reliably contain analysis? Does she look at the sideline mid-game? If the answers are no, no, rarely — this page is done with you; enjoy Saturday.

If some answers are yes, the swap is mechanical, not spiritual. Replace the debrief with logistics and food ("great effort — hungry?"); replace comparison with specificity about her ("that press in the second half"); and move genuine coaching input — if you truly have it — to a scheduled, invited conversation, never the drive home. Behaviour-change research is unanimous that removing a cue beats resisting one: sit, or stand, where you cannot easily be seen from the pitch. The one-line job description from page 13 bears repeating because it is the entire syllabus: I love watching you play.1

Keep the base rates in view, too. Most children quit sport for structural reasons — cost, scheduling, the activity simply losing to better competition for finite hours — and a quit is not a failure event. The physical-literacy goal is a movement practice through adolescence, not this one: a girl who trades netball for climbing, or team sport for a running habit with a friend, has not left the path. The alarm threshold sits elsewhere: if she stops all movement and withdraws from the friendships that came with it, that is a pattern worth a gentle conversation — and if mood or sleep change with it, worth a professional one. That is a small minority of quits.2

And the eleven-year-old's version of agency applies here as everywhere in this desk's recent run — the empty afternoon (No. 34), the analog summer (No. 35), the algorithm she'd like a say over (p13): the strongest predictor of a sport surviving is that it is hers. Let her pick the sport, the intensity, and — hardest of all — the ambition. A recreational player for a decade banks more than a burned-out competitor by fourteen. The Aspen data cuts both ways: parental presence is not the problem — former players rarely cited "my parents came to games" as a negative. Attendance without evaluation is the sweet spot. You are allowed to watch; you are allowed to be delighted. You are merely excused from punditry.

Survey answers about why we quit anything are reconstructions — adults are no better at them. Treat the 25-vs-9 asymmetry as a flag on a fixable behaviour, not a diagnosis of your household. The null hypothesis about your daughter's season remains: she's fine.

Thirty-one lamps from the dawn
Euclid — the telescope Europe built to weigh dark energy — has stumbled into a different record: thirty-one newly confirmed quasars from the Universe's first billion years, two of them the most ancient ever seen. The side quest is becoming the mission.

The paper, led by Daming Yang of Leiden University and published Monday in Astronomy & Astrophysics, confirms 31 quasars at redshifts between 6.6 and 7.8 — luminous discs of matter spiralling into supermassive black holes when the Universe was younger than about 670 million years, roughly five per cent of its current age. The most distant, catalogued as EUCL J172902.75+641018.1, sits at redshift 7.77; one further candidate awaits spectroscopic confirmation before it can claim a place on the list.1

The finding's charm is that Euclid wasn't built for this. Its mandate is the two dark things — energy and matter — surveyed by imaging a third of the sky. But a survey that wide is a net that catches whatever shines, and the earliest quasars are needles no targeted telescope could efficiently find: too rare, too faint, too red. The dark-energy machine is accidentally the best ancient-lamp hunter astronomy has fielded — a pleasing rhyme with Vera Rubin's decade-long movie of the southern sky, which began two weeks ago (No. 36). The pointed discovery is giving way to the drift-net.

The physics puzzle these lamps sharpen is the one that has shadowed every early-quasar find since JWST's "little red dots" (No. 24): a billion-solar-mass black hole at redshift 7.7 has implausibly little time to grow from an ordinary seed. Each new object tightens the vice — either the seeds were enormous, the growth broke the tidy limits, or the model wants renovating. Thirty-one more data points is how that argument gets settled — slowly, and exactly.2

When she earns more, the old explanation fails
Couples where the woman out-earns her partner do separate more often — that pattern replicates. But a new study in the Journal of Marriage and Family finds the standard explanation, wounded traditional gender norms, doesn't hold up. The ending is real; the story we tell about it may be wrong.

The finding that launched a thousand think-pieces — relationships strain when women become the higher earner — has always come bundled with its explanation pre-installed: bruised male pride, the "gender-deviance" penalty, tradition avenging itself on the modern household. New work reported this week re-tests that bundle and finds the second half wanting: the elevated break-up risk appears in the data, but the markers that should accompany a norms story — stronger effects among couples with traditional attitudes, weaker among egalitarians — largely fail to show.1 Something ends the relationships; the inherited story about why explains less than we assumed.

The candidate mechanisms left standing are less cinematic and more logistical. Households where she out-earns are disproportionately households renegotiating everything at once — hours, childcare, relocation for whose career, whose exhaustion counts. Income reversals often arrive via shocks (his job loss, her promotion) that are stressful independent of any ideology. And a measurement homily worth keeping: when a correlation survives but its explanation doesn't, the honest position is "we know less than the think-pieces did." This desk has been here before — the market-snapshot fallacy in dating (No. 31), the solitude split (No. 36).

For the couple in this readership — established, mid-career, one portfolio between them — the practical translation is plain. The risk factor, insofar as the data can see one, is not her income; it is the unrenegotiated household: the division of labour and unspoken scorekeeping that made sense under one earnings map and quietly didn't survive the new one. The brake on ageing muscle — and the pedal that releases it
A Singapore-led study names a molecular reason muscle declines with age: a regulator called DEAF1 rises, jams the cell's self-cleaning, and stalls repair. Exercise, it turns out, is the switch that turns DEAF1 back down. It is a lab result, not a prescription — but it explains why "use it or lose it" is literally true.

Work from Duke-NUS Medical School here in Singapore, published in Autophagy and refreshed on ScienceDaily this week, traces a specific circuit. With age, a regulatory protein called DEAF1 accumulates in muscle; DEAF1 pushes the growth sensor mTORC1 into overdrive, which suppresses autophagy — the cell's housekeeping process that clears damaged components. Housekeeping off, damage accumulates, repair stalls, muscle declines. Exercise activates a counter-regulator, FOXO, which turns DEAF1 back down and restores the clean-up.1 In plainer terms: age installs a brake on muscle repair, and movement is one of the few things that releases it.

The caveats are load-bearing and this desk will not bury them. This is chiefly a mouse-and-cell-model study of mechanism, not a human outcomes trial — it explains a pathway, it does not prescribe a program. And it carries a sobering nuance for the mid-40s reader tempted to procrastinate: when DEAF1 is already very high and FOXO very low, as in older muscle, the paper suggests exercise alone may not fully restore repair. The brake gets harder to release the longer it's left on — the molecular argument for starting at forty-five rather than sixty. A complementary UCLA finding this month, on a different brake (a protein called NDRG1 in ageing muscle stem cells), points the same way.2

What the mechanism does not do is change the prescription — and that is reassuring, because the prescription is by now boringly well-established. It does not vindicate any supplement, any gadget's VO₂max readout, or any single "longevity" protocol. It vindicates the dullest finding in the field: combine resistance training with aerobic work, keep it consistent, and the payoff compounds. The 90–120-minutes-a-week strength "sweet spot" and the ~45 per cent lower all-cause mortality from strength-plus-cardio (Nos. 28/32/34) are the human-outcome bedrock under this week's cell biology. DEAF1 is the why; the dumbbells are unchanged.3

The temptation the study invites — and this desk resists — is to imagine a "DEAF1 pill" that spares you the gym. Maybe someday; for now, the only intervention shown to move this circuit is the one already free, already known, and already in your calendar. The Sense of an Ending
This was a week of endings — one proclaimed from a podium, one closed by a train. Between the two lies the whole difference the ancients meant when they said the end of a thing is better than its beginning: that finishing is a discipline, not an announcement, and that the endings we declare are usually the ones that don't hold.

On Wednesday, from a summit in Ankara, the President of the United States declared a war's ending over. "It's over," he said of the sixty-day framework signed barely three weeks earlier — and to make the point, eighty targets inside Iran had burned overnight, and Brent crude jumped five per cent as the market un-priced a peace it had spent June learning to believe.1 On Sunday, without a podium or a phrase, a different ending will simply occur: three stations open on the southern edge of Singapore, and a railway line that has been a horseshoe for seventeen years quietly becomes a circle.2

Hold those two next to each other, because they are the same object seen from opposite sides. One ending was declared; the other was built. The first was a sentence; the second was a decade of tunnelling. And the pattern this issue keeps stumbling into — across a Fed that can't decide whether the inflation fight is finished, a television academy sorting durable shows from loud ones, a pop star closing a chapter she opened twenty-one years ago, a molecular brake that only releases if you started early enough — is that we badly overrate the declared ending and badly underrate the built one. We announce conclusions; we rarely finish things. The Preacher of Ecclesiastes saw it first: "Better is the end of a thing than the beginning thereof: and the patient in spirit is better than the proud in spirit."3 Note that the two halves are one thought. The end is better than the beginning because it belongs to the patient rather than the proud — and the proud are precisely the people who prefer to declare endings than to build them.

The twentieth century is a museum of declared endings that didn't hold. The most famous was signed, fittingly, on a train. At 5:10 in the morning on 11 November 1918, in a railway carriage in the forest of Compiègne, the Armistice ending the First World War was put on paper; the guns fell silent at eleven. It was the ending to end all endings — "the war to end war," in H. G. Wells's phrase, already curdling into irony.4 But an armistice is a declaration, not a settlement, and the thing it declared over was not. The peace that followed was so badly built that the historian David Fromkin could title his account of the region it redrew A Peace to End All Peace — and, in one of history's grimmer rhymes, the same railway carriage at Compiègne was hauled out of its museum in June 1940 so that a triumphant Germany could dictate France's surrender in the exact spot where it had signed its own. The declared ending of 1918 was so unfinished that it came back for a sequel in the same room.5

We announce conclusions; we rarely finish things.

Korea offers the purer specimen, because it never even pretended. The fighting there stopped on 27 July 1953 with an armistice signed at Panmunjom — a ceasefire, explicitly not a peace treaty, meant as a temporary measure until a political settlement could be built. The settlement was never built. Seventy-three years later the armistice is still all there is: no treaty, a border that is technically a military ceasefire line, two states still formally at war across a village where the ending was declared and then simply left, unfinished, for three generations.6 When the news this week reports a "60-day framework" and an "interim memorandum" for Hormuz, it is describing a Panmunjom in the making — an ending announced in the hope that announcing it might, someday, make someone build it.

There is a book behind the title of this essay, and it is worth naming, because it explains why we are so bad at this. In 1967 the critic Frank Kermode gave a set of lectures published as The Sense of an Ending, an inquiry into why human beings cannot bear a story without a shape. His central example was the ticking clock: we hear it as "tick-tock," he observed, giving the bare sound a fictional beginning and end — tick the origin, tock the conclusion — when physically it is only ever the same undifferentiated click, click, click. We are, Kermode argued, "in the middest": born after the start, dying before the end, and forever imposing endings on a reality that offers none, because a middle we cannot see the edges of is unbearable to us.1

This is the psychological engine under every declared ending in the news. The President who says a war is "over" is performing a tock — supplying the conclusion the story seems to demand, whether or not the facts have finished arriving. The market that priced Brent back down to the low seventies in June was doing the same thing: reaching for the reassuring shape of "the crisis has ended," which is why it lurched so violently on Wednesday when the crisis declined to honour the shape (see Macro, p15). Kermode's word for the danger was "degenerate fiction" — the ending imposed so forcefully that it stops us seeing what is actually happening in the middle. An armistice mistaken for a peace is a degenerate fiction with an army attached.

We are always in the middest — imposing endings on a reality that offers none.

And yet the Preacher was not wrong that the end of a thing is genuinely better than its beginning. The resolution is that he meant a built ending, not a declared one — and the built ending has a wholly different character. It does not arrive on a date chosen for a press release; it arrives when the last piece fits. Consider the cathedral at Amiens, whose nave and choir were raised in a single astonishing push between 1220 and 1270 — but whose towers were not finished until the fifteenth century, whose fabric has been mended in every century since, and which is, in the way of all great buildings, never quite done.2 The people who laid its foundations knew they would not see its ending; they were, in the medieval phrase, planting a tree whose shade they would never sit in (No. 36's harvest-lag). That is the opposite of a podium. It is patience organised into stone.

Singapore's closing loop is a small, secular version of the same thing. No one will make a speech on Sunday about "ending" the Circle Line; the three new stations will just open, and the horseshoe will be a circle, and a project begun in 2009 will have quietly reached the state its planners drew a generation ago.3 The reason it feels satisfying — the reason rail enthusiasts turned out in force for a free-ride preview of a train — is precisely that finished infrastructure is so rare. We are surrounded by declared endings (wars "over," problems "solved," eras "ended") and starved of built ones. A loop that actually closes scratches an itch the news cannot reach.

The grandest declared ending of our own lifetimes makes the point. In 1989, as the Berlin Wall came down, Francis Fukuyama announced "the end of history" — not that events would stop, but that the argument about how to organise a society had been settled.4 It was the ultimate tock: a conclusion supplied to a story still mid-sentence. The three decades since — populism, the return of great-power war, a Fed that cannot declare inflation beaten (p15) — have been history declining, firmly, to have ended. The appetite to announce a conclusion is strongest exactly when the middle is most bewildering, which is when the announcement is least reliable.

The same distinction quietly organised the rest of this issue. The Emmy nominations rewarded, above all, shows that simply kept working — a real-time hospital drama with the most nominations, a comedy setting records in its long grind, a farewell season honoured for finishing well (p12). Madonna's Confessions II is being praised not as a beginning but as a closing — a sequel that lands an argument opened twenty-one years ago (p12). Even the science bent this way: Euclid's thirty-one ancient quasars are light that finished its journey after thirteen billion years in transit, arriving exactly, if late, at a lens built to catch it (p16); and the muscle biology from Duke-NUS carried the least comfortable version of the lesson — that a repair mechanism, left unused too long, may not release at all, so the ending you want (a strong body at seventy) has to be built from decades out, not declared at the door of the gym (p18).

There is a temptation, having drawn this line, to moralise it into "declared endings bad, built endings good," and the temptation should be resisted, because declaring an ending is sometimes exactly the right thing to do. A ceasefire, even an armistice that is only a ceasefire, stops the shooting now; the guns fell silent at Compiègne and at Panmunjom and real people did not die who otherwise would have. The declared ending buys the time in which the built ending might be constructed. The error is not in declaring; it is in mistaking the declaration for the construction — in filing the memorandum and going home, as if the word "over" were load-bearing. The 1918 armistice was a legitimate act. Treating it as a finished peace, and building nothing sturdier for twenty years, was the catastrophe.

This is not, in the end, only a lesson about statecraft, and MERIDIAN would be dishonest to pretend it read the week purely through Hormuz. Every reader carries a folder of declared endings that were never built — the argument "resolved" by one person announcing it was over, the project "finished" at the demo and abandoned before the last mile, the resolution declared each January and constructed by no one. The muscle study is the whole parable in miniature: the body you will inhabit at seventy is being built, or not built, on the ordinary Tuesdays of your forties, and no amount of declaring at sixty-five that you intend to get fit will release a brake that patience alone unlocks (p18). The patient in spirit, said the Preacher, are better than the proud — not as a moral compliment, but as an engineering fact. Only the patient actually finish things.

So the discipline the week recommends is a small, unglamorous one: distrust the endings that arrive as sentences, and honour the ones that arrive as last pieces. When something is proclaimed over — a war, a market crisis, an era, a diet — ask what was actually built to make it so, and hold the proclamation lightly until you find it. And when something quietly, undramatically finishes — a loop closes, a long grind gets its award, a body proves strong at an age its owner once dreaded — mark it, because a finished thing is the rarest object in the news, and worth more than a hundred declarations of "over."

The train on Sunday will make no announcement. It will simply run the whole way round for the first time, past the stations laid down one by one across seventeen years by people who mostly did not stay to see it close. That is what an ending looks like when it is better than its beginning. It looks like patience, finished.
