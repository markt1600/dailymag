Iran · Verification

The deal is signed. Now everyone is waiting for an inspector.

A week after the ceremony that wasn't, the same fight has narrowed to a single, unglamorous word — access. The watchdog says it is coming; Tehran says only after the sanctions go.

The interim peace held through another fragile week, and the argument moved exactly where the experts said it would: not to oil, not to missiles, but to whether anyone outside Iran is allowed to look. On Friday, from the Japan National Press Club in Tokyo, IAEA director-general Rafael Grossi gave the agency's firmest reading yet — the memorandum of understanding signed by both presidents, he said, states "explicitly" that nuclear activities will be supervised by his inspectors, and "initial conversations have taken place."1 Two days earlier, from the tsunami-scarred Fukushima Daiichi plant, he had put it more bluntly: "Obviously, to do that, we will have to inspect."2

Tehran heard it differently. Deputy foreign minister Kazem Gharibabadi said there were no plans to admit inspectors to the bombed sites or to the material itself — those matters, he wrote, would be "examined and settled" only inside a final agreement, and only after Washington's "practical action" to lift sanctions.3 The gap is not rhetorical. The agency last verified Iran's stockpile in June 2025 — about 440.9 kg enriched to 60%, close enough to weapons grade to worry every capital that can do the arithmetic — and has not seen it since.2 Without eyes on the seals, "compliance" is a word, not a fact. It is a small, technical standoff that happens to be the whole ballgame; the rest of the deal rests on it.

A peace you cannot inspect is a peace you can only hope is real.

The market read the calm, not the doubt. Brent slid under US$74 — its lowest since the conflict began — as tankers moved through a re-opening Hormuz and the war premium drained out of the barrel.4 The wider board stayed quiet: in Britain, the Labour leadership race to replace Starmer narrowed to Andy Burnham, with nominations 9–16 July and a new prime minister expected around the 17th — the seventh UK leader in a decade. The Israel–Hezbollah truce in southern Lebanon frayed again midweek; Colombia's run-off result (de la Espriella 43.7% to Cepeda 40.9%) held; Ukraine kept hitting Russian refineries. None of it headlined. On a weekend, the dispatch is shorter on purpose.

The Weekend Long View

The barrel falls, the corridor reopens, and travel quietly picks up the pieces

The clearest dividend of a fragile peace this week was logistical, not diplomatic. With the Strait of Hormuz toll-free again and vessels moving, the airspace over the Gulf began to reopen — and the first carrier to act on it was one Singaporeans actually fly. On 22 June, Scoot's flight TR796 touched down in Jeddah, the airline's first Middle East service since it grounded the route on 28 February when the war shut the skies.1 It is a small thing and an honest one: the geopolitics of a corridor read most plainly in a departures board. (The Travel Desk takes it from here — p19.)

Singapore Airlines is moving more slowly. Its daily Dubai service stays suspended until at least 2 August; the long-trailed Riyadh route — SIA's first Saudi flying in twelve years — has slipped to 1 September; Oman Air's new Muscat–Singapore link starts 2 July.1 Caution, in other words, is the house style. A ceasefire is a permission, not a guarantee, and the carriers are pricing the difference.

A Deadline With Your Name On It

The change lands on Tuesday. The move to make is this weekend.

From 1 July the retirement age rises 63 → 64. It changes less than the headlines suggest — but for one quiet account, the date you act on it can lock in a number for life.

First, the calming part. From 1 July the statutory retirement age rises from 63 to 64 and the re-employment age from 68 to 69 — but the CPF payout eligibility age stays at 65, so this does not push back when LIFE payouts begin.1 The retirement age is not a "stop working" age; it is the minimum age at which an employer may require you to retire. The change adds employment protection. It does not move your pension.

The wrinkle worth a Saturday's attention sits in the Supplementary Retirement Scheme. Under SRS rules, penalty-free withdrawals can generally be made from the statutory retirement age that applied when you made your first contribution. Make that first contribution before 1 July, and your prescribed age may be locked at 63; make it on or after, and it may be set at the higher 64.1 A dollar does it. For anyone who has been meaning to open and seed an SRS account, the difference between "this weekend" and "next week" is, potentially, a year of earlier penalty-free access — the rare personal-finance deadline that is both real and trivially cheap to meet.

The Weekend Move

If SRS is on your list and you want the earlier age: open the account and make a first contribution — even a nominal one — before 1 July. The tax relief and the investing come later; the age is set by the first dollar in. Idle SRS cash earns just 0.05% p.a., so don't leave it un-invested.1

Beyond the household ledger, the tape stayed buoyant. The STI printed a fresh record near 5,210 this week — up roughly a third on the year, the three local banks doing the heavy lifting2 — even as global tech wobbled. And May's inflation print came in soft, the gauge easing back toward the middle of MAS's band; whether June marks the genuine ebb is a question for the Macro Desk (p15), where the same number reads as a portfolio signal rather than a household one. For a Saturday, that is the whole local picture: a record index, a cooling price level, and one small account with a Tuesday deadline.

The Market That's Holding Its Breath

Frozen near the high mark, waiting on one tender and one launch

A weekend read on the market for someone who owns one: it has gone quiet, and the quiet is informative. URA's Q1 index rose a slim +0.9% on thin volumes; developer sales slumped to a three-month low in May (447 units, against 1,548 in April).1 Prices have not broken — but buyers and sellers are circling a stand-off, each waiting for the other to flinch, and for two specific events to set the next benchmark.

The first is land. The mixed-use Bayshore Drive GLS on the East Coast — now served by Thomson-East Coast Line Stage 4 — closes its tender at noon on 15 July, the price-discovery a paused market is waiting on.1 The second is a launch: Vela Bay (SingHaiyi), the first private project in the new Bayshore precinct in over two decades, will test whether an OCR address can clear toward the ~S$2,600–2,800 psf zone that build costs now demand. With construction floors near S$2,400 psf, the market's support is structural, not sentimental.

For an Owner

June's BTO put ~6,900 flats across five towns, roughly half of them Plus/Prime with the longer 10-year MOP and subsidy clawback — a deliberate thinning of the future resale pool. The read-through for existing resale owners is unchanged and quietly supportive: scarcer subsidised competition downstream.1

Rates? One line, by editorial standing: SORA has drifted, refinancing windows remain open, and none of it is news — we stopped leading on it months ago. The story that matters is supply and the price it clears at, and both answers arrive in mid-July. Until then, the most honest thing to say about Singapore property is that it is waiting — for a tender result on the 15th and a launch price soon after — and that waiting, here, is a position, not a failure to have one.

The Backdrop

The memory cycle just answered its own stress-test

A weekend doesn't need the whole earnings call, just the verdict. After Tuesday's $1.3-trillion memory-and-AI selloff rattled Asia, Micron walked in on Wednesday and answered it: fiscal-Q3 revenue of $41.46B (+74% q/q, +346% y/y, a ~$5.5B beat), record gross margin near 84.9%, and HBM3E/HBM4 sold out through CY2027 into 2028. The stock jumped ~14.6% after hours to about $1,199.1

The bull case reads it as proof the AI memory demand is real, not a story. The bear case — held even-handed — notes how much of that demand traces back through a circular loop of Nvidia-and-OpenAI financing, where the same dollars appear to fund both the buyer and the seller. Both can be true at once: a genuine boom and a fragile capital structure underneath it. The H200-into-China licence question stays a one-liner; nothing changed this week.

There are two read-throughs for a reader of this magazine, and they point in opposite directions. The first is the household one: the same HBM shortage that prints Micron's record margin is the reason consumer memory and GPU prices stay stubbornly high — the "memory tax" (No. 24) that sets the ceiling on every home-lab build. Sold-out HBM into 2028 means the card you want for a local model won't get cheap soon. The second is the portfolio one: VWRA, the all-world anchor on the Macro Desk (p15), is heavily weighted toward exactly these names, which means you already own this cycle — its records and its fragility both — whether or not you ever run a model yourself. The chart on this page is, quietly, a holding in your own book.

It is also the reason this desk is called Silicon & Sovereignty. A cycle this concentrated turns a memory shortage into a geopolitical fact. The high-bandwidth memory that AI now runs on comes from essentially three firms — Micron, SK Hynix, Samsung; the logic from one, TSMC; the systems from one, Nvidia. When a resource that narrow becomes that essential, it starts to behave like oil: priced by who can pay, rationed by who is allowed to buy. The H200 export licence to China is not a footnote to this story. It is the story — compute as a strategic commodity, allocated by states as much as by markets.

Which loops back, at household scale, to the page after this one. You cannot onshore a fab over a weekend, and you cannot un-concentrate an industry by wishing. But the small, answerable version of the sovereignty question is the one you can act on: a model that runs on a card you own, in a room you control, answering to no one's allocation policy. It will never match the rented frontier. It will, however, be yours — which, this issue keeps arguing, is a different and underrated kind of good.

The point for the reader sits on the next page, where the cycle stops being a stock chart and becomes a question about your own machine: rent the intelligence, or own it?

A Saturday Project

Own the intelligence in your house, don't rent it

The case for a quiet weekend build: a capable open model that fits on a card you already own, wired into Home Assistant, answering to no one's server but yours.

While the memory cycle prints records on the page before this one, the more interesting development for a home lab is how good the models you can run yourself have become. June's open-weight wave brought the consumer sweet spot into focus: Qwen 3.6 27B posts ~77% on SWE-bench and fits in roughly 22 GB at 4-bit on a 24 GB card; Gemma 4 12B runs encoder-free multimodal inside 16 GB; Ollama 0.30.8 (12 Jun) ships GGUF plus Apple-Silicon MLX.1 The frontier datacentre models — MiniMax, Nemotron, the rest — stay on the shelf, where they belong for this purpose. The question isn't the leaderboard. It's tokens-you-actually-get.

The Saturday build is small and satisfying. Point Ollama at a model that fits your card; wire it into Home Assistant as a local conversation agent; and now the voice that runs your lights, your blinds and your morning scene is talking to a model on a box in your own home — not to a ~1.2-trillion-parameter system rented on someone else's cloud at a dollar a year per user. "Run the model that fits, and measure the throughput" is the whole discipline: benchmark prints are marketing; the token rate on your hardware is the truth.1

A benchmark is a brochure. Throughput on your own card is the receipt.

There's a sovereignty argument buried in the convenience one, and it rhymes with this issue's spine. The networked house that listens is wonderful right up until the listening leaves the building. Keeping the brain local is not paranoia; it is the same instinct that makes a person prefer a garden they can stand in to a postcard of one — the difference between a thing you own and a thing you're shown. The Camera For The Lanes

A slow year for cameras, a perfect one for the camera you already love

2026 has been, by the trade's own admission, a very slow year for new cameras outside the gimbal aisle.1 Which is the right cue for a weekend that's about a soft-yen trip to Japan (p21): the best travel camera this summer is almost certainly the small, fixed-lens compact you can already carry down a lantern-lit lane without it announcing you — the Fujifilm X100VI, the Leica Q3, a Ricoh GR, a Sony RX1R III. The renaissance of the pocketable "proper" camera is the quiet story the spec wars keep missing.

Two pieces of genuine news, both forward-looking. Leica has confirmed it is developing its own image sensor — chairman Andreas Kaufmann said as much on a January podcast — a striking move for a marque that has long bought Sony silicon, and the strongest signal yet that the next M is a 2027 event.1 Separately, the rumour mill (flagged as exactly that) has Sony readying a ~180-megapixel medium-format sensor destined for Fujifilm and Hasselblad, whose shared 100 MP chip is now seven years old.1 And October brings the 30th anniversary of the Ricoh GR1, with commemorative items teased. None of it ships this weekend. All of it says the same thing: the interesting cameras are about craft and restraint, not megapixels.

The Contrarian

Forum heretics will remind you that you can now buy a film-recipe preset that fakes "the Leica look" for thousands less than a Leica — and that in a blind test, plenty of people couldn't tell.1 True, and beside the point. You don't buy the rendering. You buy the way the thing makes you slow down and look. On a Kanazawa morning, that's the whole feature.

So the buying advice for the trip is almost anti-consumerist: don't wait for a 2027 sensor or a rumoured 180 MP back. Take the small camera you trust, a single fast prime, and the discipline to shoot less. The Travel Desk and the Good Life desk are about the same instinct from two angles — the object that earns the journey, and the journey that earns the object.

The House That Knows It's Saturday

Automate the room, not the sensor — and keep the keys at home

A weekend scene, the local-first way: the blinds lift a notch when the living room actually brightens, the coffee machine warms when the kitchen senses someone up, and nothing in the chain phones a server in another country to do it. The update that makes this gentler arrived in beta this week. Home Assistant 2026.7 ("A betta title," 24 Jun) introduces intent-based triggers and conditions that target a room rather than a named entity — "when the study gets warm," not "when sensor_4 crosses 26°."1 You describe the place and the state; the system finds the devices. It is a small abstraction that makes a home automation read like a sentence.

The standards churn underneath stays a one-liner, as it should. Matter 1.6 (17 Jun) added setup, multi-ecosystem and NFC-commissioning polish but no new device categories; HA's engine sits on the matter.js / Matter 1.5.1 base with 1.6 "coming soon."2 The rule hasn't changed since No. 31: buy the certified device, not the version number. And a quiet proof of the local-first thesis landed in the release notes — Home Assistant's Microsoft Teams integration was removed after Microsoft retired its Office 365 connectors on 22 May. When a cloud dependency vanishes, the automation that lived on your own network simply… keeps working.1

The Migrator's Note

Room-level intents are the friendliest on-ramp yet for the Lutron-to-Home-Assistant crowd: you can express a scene in plain language and let HA resolve the hardware. Pair it with a local LLM (p8) and the voice layer never leaves the house either.

The sceptic's footnote is fair: an abstraction layer is still the same back-end with a nicer label, and "automate the room" can hide which sensor actually fired when something misbehaves. True. But for a weekend morning the trade is worth it — fewer brittle entity names, more scenes that survive a device swap. The deeper point is the one this whole issue keeps circling: a home that listens is a privilege only while the listening stays inside the walls.

The Soft-Yen Window

A weak yen, a quiet craft, and the watch you buy where it's made

The yen is near a 39-year low. The temptation is the obvious flex bought cheap. The better object is the one a Japanese artisan finished by hand — and the window for buying it tax-free is closing in November.

The number first, because it sets everything else. USD/JPY closed Friday near ¥161.6 — territory not seen since 1986 — with SGD/JPY around ¥125, despite repeated verbal intervention and a Bank of Japan that meets again on 31 July.1 For a Singapore traveller, Japan is the rare luxury that has quietly gone on sale and stayed there. The lazy move is to spend the discount on a Swiss flex bought below home prices. The interesting one is to spend it on something you can only properly buy there: a watch finished by a Japanese hand.

This is a genuine moment for the craft. Independents like Kurono Tokyo — Hajime Asaoka's more attainable line — turn out limited lacquer-dialled pieces that sell out in minutes; Naoya Hida's 2026 collection runs to roughly 87 examples across seven references; brands like Knot offer "Japan Concept" dials worked in urushi lacquer, gold dust and mother-of-pearl by a single local craftsman.2 That last detail is the through-line to this weekend's Grand Tour: the gold leaf on a Knot dial and the gold leaf on a Kanazawa teahouse wall come from the same 99%-of-Japan source (p21). The object and the journey share a material.

Spend a weak yen on a thing a hand made, not a thing a logo sells.
The Object

A Japanese lacquer dial. Not one model but a category having a moment — Kurono, Naoya Hida, Credor's urushi work, Knot's gold-dust dials. The value isn't the discount; it's the maker. Buy the craft, not the currency.2

The Fine Print

The tax-free clock is ticking. Until 1 November 2026, Japan applies the 10% consumption-tax exemption instantly at checkout (purchases over ¥5,000, passport required). After that it switches to a refund-on-departure system — same saving, more friction.3 If a craft watch is the plan, the pre-November trip is the cleaner one.

A closing caution against the obvious: a weak yen is a tailwind, not a thesis. Buy what you'd want at ¥140, not what only tempts you at ¥161 — currencies turn, and a watch bought for the discount becomes a watch you don't wear. The craft pieces survive that test because the reason to own them was never the exchange rate. Which is, come to think of it, the same advice the Macro Desk gives about everything else this weekend (p15).

The Weekend the Cosmos Showed Up

Two records named for the deep, and a galaxy drawn like a bow

Press play this weekend. Friday delivered two of the year's most-anticipated rock records at once. Muse's The Wow! Signal — titled for the 1977 SETI burst that astronomers still can't explain — arrived to the band's strongest reviews in two decades; The Strokes' Reality Awaits landed the same day, their first in five years.1 First-week chart numbers are the honest next peg and won't print until midweek; we're holding them rather than guessing. But the timing is the story: a record named for a signal from the deep drops in the same 48 hours that a real telescope ships for launch and a genuinely strange galaxy makes the wires (Curiosities, p16). The culture and the cosmos rhymed.

On screens, the verdict is almost in but not quite. Supergirl (Milly Alcock, dir. Craig Gillespie) opened Friday to a soft start — $7.8M in Thursday previews, ~$13M globally on day one, a 57% Rotten Tomatoes critics' score against a 77% audience mark — and is tracking to a $40–51M domestic weekend, well shy of the ~$315M it reportedly needs to break even, and certain to lose its own opening frame to Toy Story 5's second weekend.2 The reviews are united on one thing: Alcock outshines the script. The Case For The Adventure

As safe as necessary — not as safe as possible

A new evidence review makes the unfashionable case that a little risk, a lot of agency, and time in wild places aren't hazards to be managed out of childhood. They're the mechanism through which children grow.

It is the perfect weekend to make this argument, because the Grand Tour two pages on is a trip you'd take an eleven-year-old on. A December scoping review in Behavioural Sciences — Tonia Gray and colleagues, PRISMA-ScR, 40 empirical studies from 2015–2025 — synthesised what risky outdoor play and adventure education actually do for children and adolescents. All 40 studies reported positive associations, across eight themes: resilience and confidence, wellbeing, physical skills, autonomy and agency, nature-connectedness, quality play, educator influence.1 The organising principle is a phrase worth taping to the fridge: design childhood to be "as safe as necessary, rather than as safe as possible."

The mechanism matters more than the slogan. Across early childhood and the school years, the review found that authentic agency and autonomy — the child genuinely choosing, judging, and occasionally failing — were the critical channel through which the benefits arrived.1 A climbing frame supervised into total safety teaches less than a slightly-too-high branch the child decides to trust. "How high can I go? Is this branch strong enough?" are not risks to be eliminated; they are the curriculum.2 This is not, to be clear, a licence for carelessness. It is a recalibration of the default from prevent everything to permit the useful difficulty.

The slightly-too-high branch is not the hazard. It's the lesson.
Don't Be Alarmist

"Acceptable risk" is the opposite of recklessness — it is risk a child can assess and manage, with an adult nearby and a soft-enough landing. The benefit–risk frame asks one question of any activity: does removing the risk also remove the point? If it does, keep the risk and lower the stakes, don't cancel the climb.

The Small Self

Why feeling tiny is one of the best things you can do for yourself

For two decades, psychology has been quietly assembling the case that awe — the goosebump emotion you get from vastness — is among the most therapeutic states a human can occupy. This is its field guide.

Awe spent a long time being ignored by science — too rare, too vague, too close to the religious to feel like a serious subject. Then, in 2003, Dacher Keltner and Jonathan Haidt gave it a definition crisp enough to study: the emotion elicited by perceived vastness that exceeds your existing mental structures — something so large, physically or conceptually or morally, that your mind has to rearrange itself to take it in.1 Twenty years of research later, the goosebumps turn out to do real work. Awe shrinks the ruminating self, expands felt time, increases generosity, quiets the brain's self-referential default-mode network, and — in some studies — even lowers markers of inflammation.2

The flagship finding is the one this whole issue is named for. Across five studies (N = 2,078), Paul Piff and colleagues showed that awe produces a "small self" — a diminished sense of one's own importance and concerns — and that this smallness reliably makes people more generous and more ethical. In one study, participants who simply stood for a minute in a grove of towering eucalyptus behaved more prosocially afterward than those who'd looked at an ordinary building.3 Feeling small, it turns out, doesn't diminish you. It connects you — to other people, to a longer clock, to something past the edge of your own week.

Feeling small doesn't diminish you. It connects you.
How To Dose It

The awe walk. In one UCSF study, older adults sent on weekly "awe walks" — same route, instruction to attend to the vast and unexpected — showed greater joy, more prosocial feeling, and literally bigger smiles than ordinary walkers.4

The night sky. Free, reliable, overhead. Conceptual vastness — deep time, the scale of a galaxy (p16) — produces the full response too.

Human goodness. Keltner's data ranks witnessing moral beauty — courage, kindness, skill — as everyday awe's single largest source. A counter-feed to the one optimised for outrage.

Much of this is short-term and lab-based; a 2025 randomised trial found awe reduced depressive symptoms, but awe is a nudge, not a cure, and the research rewards frequency over intensity — small, repeatable doses beat the once-a-decade canyon.2 Still, of all the wellbeing prescriptions this magazine has reported, this is the cheapest and least demanding: step outside this weekend, find something larger than your own concerns — a garden, a coastline, the sky, a stranger being good — and let it make you small for a minute. An Idea To Try This Weekend

The long slow effort, and the four-minute one — the two ends of the same week

Here is a weekend programming idea that asks for no gym and no gadget: bookend your week with the two opposite ends of the cardio spectrum. On the weekend, the long, slow effort — a Zone-2 walk, ride or hike long enough to be boring, easy enough to hold a conversation. It builds the aerobic base that underwrites everything else, and (per the Love & Life desk opposite) if you take it somewhere vast and leave the phone in your pocket, it doubles as an awe walk.1 On weekdays, the opposite: the "exercise snack" — short, vigorous, incidental bursts. The snack idea has real science behind the viral framing. Vigorous intermittent lifestyle physical activity — VILPA — in bouts as short as a minute or two, accumulated across a day, tracks with meaningfully lower mortality in large observational studies; the headline "four minutes can punch above a longer moderate walk" captures the intensity effect, even if the exact ratio is marketing.1 The mechanism is the one worth caring about: brief hard efforts are the most time-efficient way to nudge cardiorespiratory fitness, and fitness is among the strongest predictors of how long and how well you live.2

Long and slow on Saturday. Short and hard on Tuesday. That's the whole program.

Kanazawa: the Kyoto you get to keep to yourself

Two and a half hours from Tokyo on the wrong coast, a samurai city kept its gardens, its geisha quarters and its gold leaf intact — and somehow stayed off most itineraries. Go for the calm, the seafood, and the rain.

Every traveller eventually graduates from the Golden Route — Tokyo, Kyoto, Osaka — and starts asking the better question: where is the Kyoto without the crush? The most convincing answer sits on the Sea of Japan side, two and a half hours from Tokyo on the Hokuriku Shinkansen. Kanazawa was the seat of the Maeda lords, the second-wealthiest domain in feudal Japan, and it spent that wealth on culture rather than war. The result survived the centuries — and the bombs of the 1940s — almost whole: a great garden, preserved geisha and samurai districts, a market that still feels like a working town's larder, and a craft tradition that supplies 99% of Japan's gold leaf.1

Start at Kenrokuen, ranked among Japan's three great gardens. Its name means "the garden of six attributes" — spaciousness, seclusion, artifice, antiquity, water, and a commanding view — the six qualities a classical Chinese text said could never coexist, here arranged so they do.1 Its emblem is the two-legged Kotoji stone lantern, shaped like the bridge of a koto; in winter the gardeners rig the pines with yukitsuri, conical rope cages that hold the branches against the snow — one of the most photographed images in all of Japan. Then walk the lattice-fronted lanes of Higashi Chaya, the largest of the city's geisha quarters and, with Kyoto's Gion and Kanazawa's own Kazue-machi, one of only three such districts designated a national Cultural Asset.2

Then let the city slow you down. Nagamachi is the samurai quarter — earthen walls, narrow lanes, the texture of old Kanazawa with no single headline attraction and all the better for it; it's the place to put the phone away and just walk. Omicho Market, 200-plus stalls of Sea-of-Japan seafood, is the city's briny heart — less "local" than it was, the regulars admit, but still worlds better than the tourist-trap markets of Kyoto, Tokyo and Osaka.2 And for a change of register, two museums sit at opposite poles: the 21st Century Museum of Contemporary Art, with Leandro Erlich's optical-illusion swimming pool (a child can stand "underwater" and wave up at you), and the D.T. Suzuki Museum, a spare, water-mirrored building built for sitting still and thinking about Zen. Spectacle and silence, ten minutes apart.

Planning It Honestly

When to go, where to sleep, and what the forums actually say

When. Two windows reward you. Autumn (late October to mid-November) gives Kyoto-grade foliage with a fraction of the crowds — Kenrokuen, Nagamachi and Oyama Shrine all turn. Winter (December–February) is the foodie's secret: snow crab and yellowtail at their peak, the yukitsuri ropes lit at night, and hotel rates 20–40% lower.1 Spring sakura runs about a week behind Kyoto's — so you can catch blossoms there, then ride the Thunderbird and catch them again here. One local proverb governs all seasons: "You can forget your lunch, but never your umbrella." It rains. Pack for it.

Where to base. Three good options, all on the ¥800 Loop Bus: the Station area (most convenient, under that extraordinary Tsuzumi-mon gate); Korinbo–Katamachi (central, best for dining and nightlife); or a machiya townhouse in Higashi Chaya itself, for atmosphere over convenience.1 The city is small and genuinely walkable — Kenrokuen to Higashi Chaya to Omicho Market is a comfortable half-day on foot, which after Tokyo feels like a holiday from the holiday.

Beyond The City

Day trips stack up: Shirakawa-go's thatched gassho-zukuri villages (direct bus ~1h15 from the East Gate), the quieter Gokayama, the castle town of Takayama, and Toyama just 20 minutes on by Shinkansen. A rail pass makes these effortless; without one, think twice.2

The veterans split, usefully. One camp: "One full day is enough" for the core sights. One thing they agree on: don't day-trip it from Tokyo without a rail pass — it's too dear for the time, and the Shinkansen back isn't frequent. And Omicho Market, while "not so local anymore," still beats the tourist-trap markets of Kyoto, Tokyo and Osaka by a wide margin.2

If the Noto Peninsula tempts you north, go with eyes open. The M7.6 quake of 1 January 2024 killed more than 500 people; two years on, most major sites and roads have reopened, but roughly half the onsen inns remain closed, and Wajima's famous morning market now runs from a relocated plaza with ~30–35 stalls.3 Locals frame visiting as "hope tourism" — your spending helps. A Note On Timing

The matsuri are the argument against the Grand Tour's calm in July — Kyoto in Gion season is glorious and packed. The reconciliation: base in Kanazawa, day-trip the festivals, sleep where the crowds aren't. Or save Kanazawa itself for the quiet autumn-foliage window the Travel Desk recommends (p22) and do July purely for the festivals.

The Small Self

On awe, the uses of feeling tiny, and a quiet rebellion against the machinery that wants you to feel enormous.

There is a particular kind of relief that has no good name in English. It arrives when you stand at the foot of something vast — a mountain, a cathedral, a clear night sky far from any city — and feel yourself, pleasantly, shrink. The petty griefs of the week lose their grip. Time seems to slow and widen. You become, for a moment, a small thing in a large world, and instead of frightening you, it steadies you. Every culture has noticed this. Ours has nearly automated it away. This essay is about the emotion the psychologists now call awe, the long tradition of thinkers who took it seriously, and the strange fact that a feeling defined by smallness might be the most useful thing a self can do.

The modern science begins, as the Love & Life desk noted earlier (p17), with a definition. In 2003 two psychologists, Dacher Keltner and Jonathan Haidt, proposed that awe is the emotion you feel in the presence of perceived vastness that exceeds your existing mental structures — something so big that your mind has to accommodate, to rebuild itself slightly, to fit the new fact in.1 Two ingredients, then: vastness, and the need to update. A skyscraper is large but not awesome; you already have a category for it. A galaxy ploughing a 1.8-million-light-year wake through intergalactic space, found by a student on a Himalayan hillside (p16), is large in a way your categories cannot hold — and so it produces the goosebumps, the dropped jaw, the involuntary oh.

What the research then found is that this small, ancient feeling has outsized effects. Awe diminishes the sense of self-importance — Paul Piff's studies call it the "small self" — and that diminishment makes people measurably more generous, more ethical, more patient, more connected to those around them.2 It expands the felt experience of time, so that an awestruck afternoon seems longer and more spacious than a busy one. Neuroscience offers a mechanism: awe quiets the brain's default-mode network, the circuitry of self-referential thought, the part that narrates your worries back to you on a loop. For a moment, the narrator goes quiet. The self gets smaller, and the world gets larger, and the trade is overwhelmingly in your favour.

There is even an effect on the most modern of scarcities: time. In a set of experiments, Melanie Rudd and colleagues found that awe makes the present moment feel more abundant — people who had just felt it reported having more time available, were more willing to volunteer it to others, and judged their lives more satisfying.3 The feeling, in other words, does the opposite of the rushed Saturday it interrupts: it slows the clock down and loosens the grip of the to-do list. And the "small self" appears to be close to universal — researchers have documented it from California to Beijing, with cultural variations in how the diminishment is felt but a common core: in the presence of vastness, the loud, defended, comparing self goes quiet, and something more connected takes its place.3

None of this would have surprised the philosophers. The feeling we now measure with questionnaires and fMRI was, for centuries, the central concern of an entire aesthetic tradition — the tradition of the sublime. And to understand why feeling small is good for you, it helps to go back to the people who first tried to explain why we seek out the very things that make us feel that way.

A feeling defined by smallness may be the most useful thing a self can do.

That tradition runs from a young Irishman's first book, through a German philosopher's tombstone, to a photograph taken six billion kilometres from Earth — and it arrives, eventually, at a Japanese garden engineered to make infinity fit inside a few hectares. It is, in its way, the most practical lineage in the history of ideas: a 250-year argument that the cure for taking yourself too seriously is to go and stand near something that doesn't.

II · The Sublime

Burke's terror, Kant's tombstone, and the man with his back to us

In 1757 a young Edmund Burke — better known later as a statesman — published A Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful. Its central move was to split apart two things we tend to lump together. The beautiful, Burke argued, is what we love — the small, the smooth, the harmonious, the safely pleasing. The sublime is something else entirely: the vast, the obscure, the powerful, the things that brush against terror. A still lake is beautiful; the ocean in a storm is sublime. And crucially, Burke insisted, we are drawn to the sublime precisely because it overwhelms us — provided we are safe enough to feel the overwhelming as awe rather than fear. Vastness, viewed from a place of safety, becomes a pleasure of a deeper kind than mere prettiness.1

A generation later, Immanuel Kant gave the idea its most enduring sentence — the one this issue borrowed for its epigraph (p2). At the close of his Critique of Practical Reason (1788) he wrote that two things filled him with "ever new and increasing admiration and awe": the starry heavens above, and the moral law within.2 The pairing is the whole point. For Kant, the sublime was not really out there in the storm or the stars; it was the mind's own response to its smallness before them, and the surprising discovery, in that smallness, of its own dignity. To stand under the night sky and feel tiny was, paradoxically, to be reminded that you are the kind of creature who can comprehend that sky at all. He had the sentence carved on his tombstone in Königsberg. It is, as far as last words go, an argument that feeling small and feeling significant are not opposites but partners.

The Romantics painted it. Around 1818 Caspar David Friedrich finished Wanderer above the Sea of Fog — a lone figure, seen from behind, standing on a dark crag above a churning ocean of cloud, the peaks rising beyond. We never see his face; we are placed with him, looking out. It is the most famous image of the sublime ever made, and it works because of its arithmetic: one small human, an immensity of world, and the strange exaltation of being the former in the presence of the latter.3 Friedrich's wanderer is doing, in oil, exactly what Piff's subjects did in a grove of eucalyptus and what a UCSF study's pensioners did on their awe walks (p17) — standing before vastness and being remade, briefly, smaller and better.

To feel tiny under the stars is to remember you are the creature who can comprehend them.

For most of human history, the sublime was rare and required a journey — a mountain pass, a cathedral, an ocean crossing. You had to go to it. What changed everything was a camera carried further from home than anything humans had ever sent, and a scientist who understood that the most overwhelming vastness of all might be the view back toward ourselves.

III · The Mote & The Garden

A pale blue dot, an overview, and a garden that fakes the infinite

On 14 February 1990, at Carl Sagan's urging, the Voyager 1 spacecraft turned its camera back toward home from beyond Neptune and photographed the Earth as a single pale blue pixel, suspended in a band of scattered sunlight. Sagan's words about that image have outlived almost everything else written in the era: that everyone you love, everyone who ever was, every triumph and atrocity of history, lived out their lives on "a mote of dust suspended in a sunbeam."1 It is the sublime inverted — not a vastness we look up at, but a smallness we are forced to look down upon. And its effect is identical to Friedrich's wanderer and Kant's starry sky: it shrinks the self, widens the perspective, and makes the ordinary cruelties of the species look as small as they actually are.

The astronauts gave the experience a name. Frank White, interviewing those who had seen Earth whole from space, called it the Overview Effect: a reliable cognitive shift in which the planet's borders vanish, its fragility becomes viscerally obvious, and the observer returns changed — more protective, less tribal, permanently re-scaled.2 It is awe delivered at the maximum dose, and it is the reason a 300-megapixel telescope shipping for an August launch (p16) is not merely an instrument but a kind of civic medicine: every deep-field image is an invitation to the small self, mass-produced.

But here is the quiet twist, and the reason this issue's Grand Tour went to a garden town. You do not need a spacecraft. Four centuries ago, Japanese garden masters worked out how to manufacture the sublime at human scale — to make a few hectares feel infinite. The technique called shakkei, "borrowed scenery," pulls a distant mountain into a garden's composition so that the bounded space seems to extend forever. The aesthetic of yūgen — a profound, dimly-felt grace, more suggested than shown — does in a raked courtyard what the Overview Effect does from orbit. Kenrokuen, which the Travel Desk walked you through (p21), is precisely this: an engineered vastness, a designed awe, proof that the small self can be summoned in a garden as surely as under the stars.3

The feed wants you enormous. The garden, the sky, and the sea want you small. Trust the older technology.

Which leaves the rebellion. We have built, in the feed, the most powerful self-magnifying machine in history — an apparatus whose entire business model is to keep you at the centre of the frame, aggrieved, comparing, enormous. Awe is its precise opposite, and its cheapest antidote. It asks nothing but attention and something large to point it at: a night sky, a coastline, a garden, a stranger being unexpectedly good (Keltner's data says human goodness is the most common everyday source of all4). John Muir said he only went out for a walk and concluded to stay out till sundown, "for going out, I found, was really going in" (No. 27). Lao Tzu prized the emptiness that makes a vessel useful (No. 22). Annie Dillard reminded us that how we spend our days is how we spend our lives (No. 21). They were all describing the same manoeuvre. This weekend, perform it. Find something vast. Let it make you small. It is free, it is good for you, and it is the oldest technology we have for being briefly, gratefully, sane.
