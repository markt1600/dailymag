From the Desk

"Where is the wisdom we have lost in knowledge? Where is the knowledge we have lost in information?"

— T. S. Eliot, choruses from The Rock (1934)

Eliot wrote that lament a year after the BBC went to a national audience and a decade before the first programmable computers. We read it ninety-two years on, drowning in feeds, models and channels — and the question the whole book keeps circling is older and simpler than any of them: where did this come from, and can I trust it?

This issue, the answer is rarely on the label. A wave of anti-Indian posts that surfaced in Singapore traced back to a server in China. Apple's new assistant thinks with a model it rents from Google. The Matter camera you mount this month speaks a universal language — but stores its footage wherever its maker likes. A particle billed for years as the most powerful proton ever caught may not be a proton at all. The thread is provenance: not what a thing says it is, but where it actually comes from, and who holds the keys once it's through your gate.

Hormuz · The Hundred-Day War

Oil leaks back through the strait. The peace stays unsigned.

A weekend exchange of missiles ended as fast as it flared. Tankers are moving again — and yet the only thing nobody will put a name to is the deal that is supposed to end it all.

The first direct fire between Israel and Iran since April's truce lasted barely a night. Iran loosed missiles at Israel on 7–8 June over Israel's continued strikes in southern Lebanon; Israel hit back, then both halted at Washington's urging.1 By Tuesday the airspace had reopened, schools were back, and the barrage that briefly pushed Brent above US$98 had drained away. What it left behind is the same paradox the reader has watched harden for four months: a war fought in spasms around a deal "described in every capital and signed in none."

The market's tell this week was oil going down on a war scare. Brent settled near US$91 and US crude near US$88 — off about 3% on the day — after US Energy Secretary Chris Wright said traffic through the Strait of Hormuz was "rising very meaningfully," even as President Trump accused Iran of downing a US Apache patrolling the chokepoint.2 The dual blockade still chokes the world's busiest oil artery, but more crude is slipping through than the satellites show. JPMorgan told clients on 4 June that real flows likely exceed visible ones.

"A ceasefire is not a refund. Ceasefires end wars; they do not undo them."

That is the provenance problem in geopolitical form. Every party agrees a piece of paper exists; none will vouch for what is on it, or whose signature counts. Tehran's parliamentary speaker, Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf, says Iran will "defeat" the naval blockade and lists three breaches it blames on Washington. The White House says the blockade lifts only when a "final deal" is reached. Both statements describe the same sixty-day framework. Neither will sign it first.3

The forecasters, meanwhile, refuse to relax. The US Energy Information Administration still pencils Brent near US$106 for May–June before Gulf supply pulls it toward US$89 by the fourth quarter — a reminder that the buffer of global stockpiles is finite and summer demand is climbing into it.

The Chatter · Energy Desks

Traders on the commodities forums read Wright's "rising meaningfully" as a nudge, not a number — he gave no figures. The consensus: prices are "moderate relative to the disruption" only because inventories are doing the heavy lifting, and that cushion thins every week.

— Compiled from energy-trading commentary, 9 Jun

The Sceptic

A contrarian note doing the rounds: the Apache claim and the "traffic rising" claim can't both be load-bearing. If Hormuz were genuinely normalising, you wouldn't need to keep announcing it. Treat both as messaging until tanker-tracking confirms.

— Paraphrased market scepticism

Foreign Interference

A foreign hand finds a local nerve

Fourteen posts, geo-blocked in a day. The content was crude; the lesson underneath it is the one worth keeping: the anger was ours, the amplifier was not.

On 6 June the government invoked the Online Criminal Harms Act and ordered YouTube, Facebook and X to block local access to fourteen posts that cast Singapore as "overrun by Indians." The Home Affairs ministry said the narratives surfaced in May inside a China-based information space before being amplified abroad; Second Minister Edwin Tong put it plainly — "the content originated from overseas."1 The platforms complied within the day.

It is tempting to file this under housekeeping. The more useful reading is the one that runs through this whole issue: consider the source. The resentment the posts tried to harvest — over immigration, jobs, the texture of daily integration — is real and homegrown. What was imported was the megaphone. An external actor took a genuine domestic tension and tried to turn the gain up, the same play used against the 2024 leadership transition and again at last year's election.

The anger was ours. The amplifier was not. Telling the two apart is now a civic skill.

For a reader who watches the region's wiring, this lands alongside a quieter strategic anxiety: the sense, voiced by analysts this month, that Washington's tariffs, forced-labour probes and pressure to "pick a side" are narrowing Singapore's room to balance. The danger isn't a dramatic pivot to Beijing; it's a slow diversification of ties that erodes everyone's leverage. A small state's only real defence against being moved is to know exactly who is doing the moving.2

The Chatter · r/singapore

The thread split predictably: relief that it was acted on fast, unease that "block 14 posts" barely dents a firehose, and a sharper minority asking the honest question — why did it land at all? The most-upvoted reply: because the grievance was real before anyone weaponised it.

— Paraphrased forum consensus

The Sceptic

"Foreign origin" is doing heavy lifting in the official account. Attribution of information operations is genuinely hard; treat "likely from a China-based platform" as a careful claim, not a closed case — which is exactly the standard the takedown asks citizens to apply.

The Safe-Haven Reflex

War in the Gulf, launches in the suburbs

A hundred days into an oil war, Singapore's developers are not flinching. History gives the reader a read-through worth filing — and a caution worth filing beside it.

The instinct, when missiles fly and Brent jumps, is to assume property freezes. The local record says otherwise. Huttons notes that Singapore's private market has historically shrugged off Middle East conflict — prices rose about 2.8% in 2004, the year after the Iraq war — and analysts now expect some 8,900 units across roughly twenty projects to launch between now and year-end.1 Developers are pressing on, on the wager that has paid out for two decades: in a nervous world, a politically stable city with rule of law reads as a vault.

The flash data is co-operative. The URA's first-quarter index was revised up to +0.6% from a 0.3% estimate — soft, but positive, with the Rest-of-Central and Outside-Central regions doing the work while landed prices slipped. Recent launches confirm the pulse: Hudson Place Residences moved 201 of 327 units on debut at about S$2,458 psf, and a CapitaLand–UOL consortium paid S$1.5 billion for the Hougang Central site, a vote of confidence priced in nine figures.2

A vault is only as safe as the assumption that nothing changes. That is the assumption to interrogate.

For an owner, the read-through is steadiness, not euphoria: a market grinding higher on supply and stability rather than speculation, with the cooling measures still load-bearing. The caution is the flip side of the safe-haven story — the same energy shock lifting tariffs is squeezing households, and a market that leans on "Singapore is different" is, in the end, leaning on a belief about provenance: that the stability priced in is real and durable. It usually has been. "Usually" is the word doing the work.

The Chatter · Agents & Buyers

Showflat chatter is unbothered — "wars don't move Singapore property" is repeated like a mantra. The more interesting agent line: foreign enquiries tick up precisely because the region looks unsettled, ABSD notwithstanding. Safe-haven demand is its own small tailwind.

— Paraphrased market commentary

The Sceptic

2004 is a thin sample. The mantra works until a shock arrives through a channel property isn't insulated from — a sustained rates regime, a genuine demand recession, or a policy turn. "It's always held" is a track record, not a guarantee.

— Contrarian read

Still on the shelf. June's Prime-heavy BTO and the URA 2H2026 GLS Confirmed List — both covered last issue — advance by a line: applications for the BTO open shortly, and the supply pipeline ticks toward ~61,000 units. The SORA refinancing window stays a one-line footnote, as flagged: rates are not the story this issue, and won't be again without a genuinely new turn.

The Edge Comes Home

A 120-billion-parameter mind, in a backpack

Nvidia spent a decade owning the data centre. At Computex it pointed the same architecture at your lap — and quietly answered a question the home-lab reader has been asking for two years: how far can local actually go?

On 1 June in Taipei, Jensen Huang unveiled the RTX Spark Superchip and said the quiet part loudly: Nvidia, with Microsoft, intends to "reinvent the PC." The part numbers do the arguing. Up to twenty Arm CPU cores and a Blackwell GPU with 6,144 CUDA cores, lashed to 128GB of LPDDR5X over NVLink at up to 300 GB/s — enough, Nvidia says, to run 120-billion-parameter models with context windows stretching to a million tokens, locally, on a laptop.1 Systems ship this autumn from Dell, HP, Lenovo, Asus and MSI, plus a Microsoft Surface Ultra. AMD, Intel and Qualcomm shares dipped on the news.

For the reader running a home lab, this is the sibling to the DGX Spark desktop we flagged in No. 16 — only now the frontier-class local box detaches from the wall. The significance isn't raw speed; it's residence. A model that lives on a device you own, fed by memory you own, answering from silicon you control, is the cleanest possible answer to the provenance question this issue keeps asking. Rent nothing; own the keys.

The question was never "is the cloud faster?" It was "whose machine is thinking?"

The catch is the one binding constraint we've tracked all month: memory. 128GB of LPDDR5X is generous precisely because DRAM has gone scarce and dear — the memory tax that pushed contract prices up sharply this quarter is exactly why a unified-memory laptop is both a marvel and a luxury. The chip is the easy part now. The RAM is the moat.

Four places a model can live. The RTX Spark drags the frontier one full notch left — toward the end of the line where the keys are yours.
The Chatter · Local-LLM Forums

The home-lab boards greeted the Spark with the usual split: half see the end of the noisy GPU tower, half are already doing the arithmetic on what 128GB of soldered memory costs when DRAM is this dear. The recurring caution — soldered means no upgrade path, so you buy the ceiling you can afford on day one and live with it for the life of the machine.

Wearables · Back to Basics

Garmin remembers what a watch is for

While every other wrist gadget races to bolt on AI, Garmin's new entry-level runners do something quietly radical: less.

Garmin's Forerunner 70 and 170 land as the new floor of its running line, replacing the well-loved 55 and 165. The two share a 43mm case, a 1.2-inch AMOLED touchscreen, five physical buttons and support for 80-plus activities; the 170 sits a notch higher on sensors.1 No chatbot, no "assistant," no subscription — just a watch that measures the run and gets out of the way. For a reader already wearing an Oura ring and a Whoop band, that restraint is the appeal: a focused tool whose data lives on a device built to do one job honestly.

It's the counterweight to the page before. Where the laptop story is about owning a vast, general mind, the watch story is about owning a small, specific one — a sensor you can trust precisely because it isn't trying to be clever. In an issue about knowing where your data comes from, a buttoned device that simply reports what it measured is its own small statement.

Matter · The Camera Finally Arrives

A universal remote — but whose box?

Matter cameras are finally on shelves. The buttons now work everywhere. What you can't standardise is the part that matters most to a local-first home: where the footage goes.

After the long wait we logged in No. 14 and a deliberate one-line demotion in No. 15, there's a real peg at last: the first wave of certified Matter 1.5 cameras has reached shelves, with Samsung's SmartThings the only major controller shipping support so far — Aqara, Eve and Ulticam supplying the hardware. The CSA's inaugural Unify event, Matter's first dedicated trade show, runs 16–18 June in Austin.1 Apple, Google and Amazon are "expected to follow," which in Matter-camera terms has meant "eventually."

Here is the line that matters for the migrator. Matter 1.5 standardises the buttons — live video, motion, person- and package-detection, two-way audio, the privacy shutter — so any controller can read any camera the same way. It pointedly does not standardise the back end: where footage is stored, where the AI runs, what hides behind a subscription. As one apt summary put it, it's a universal remote where the buttons work everywhere but the channels still depend on whose box you bought.2

Interoperable at the glass, proprietary at the vault. Read the spec for what it standardises — and for what it leaves to the vendor.

Which is the whole issue in a doorbell. A camera can speak perfect Matter and still phone its footage home to a cloud you don't own and can't audit. For the reader keeping the brain local on Home Assistant, the move is unchanged and now better-supported: adopt Matter for the interoperability, but insist the recording and the inference stay inside the house. Standard plumbing, your own reservoir.

The Migrator's Read

Home Assistant has, as ever, been quickest on the new spec. Pair a Matter cam to a local HA instance, route events to local automations, keep clips on your own NVR. The Matter layer buys you escape-hatch portability between ecosystems without surrendering the footage. That's the dream the standard was sold on — finally testable.

— Meridian Connected Home desk

The Sceptic

"On shelves" with one controller is a soft launch, not an ecosystem. Until Apple/Google/Amazon ship and the WebRTC streams prove robust across hubs, treat Matter cameras as early-adopter territory. The standard's history is patience.

— Contrarian note

Continuity. HA 2026.6 "Pick a card" (shipped 3 Jun) is not re-led — it's the stable base under all of the above. Apple's homeOS and the HomePad previewed at WWDC remain a delta-only watch: a voice surface to treat as a peripheral, not a brain.

Media Literacy · The New Basic

Raising a "consider-the-source" kid

The week's biggest local story — posts about Singapore, written somewhere else — is also the week's best teaching moment. The skill an eleven-year-old needs most now isn't typing. It's asking where a thing came from.

A child today swims in information whose origin is deliberately hidden: a meme amplified from overseas, a "fact" a chatbot stated with total confidence, a video that may have been made by no one. The grown-up version of that problem fills this whole issue. The eleven-year-old version is simpler and more urgent: she will form beliefs from a feed engineered to be persuasive, not true. The antidote isn't a ban. It's a habit — who made this, why, and how would I check?

There's fresh reason to teach it deliberately. Research this spring found that conversational AI doesn't merely repeat misinformation; because it validates and builds on whatever a user says, it can actively strengthen a false belief — a feedback loop a credulous reader of any age can fall into.1 Pair that with this week's reminder that a coordinated narrative can be seeded from a server abroad, and the lesson writes itself: the most important question is no longer "what does it say" but "where did it come from."

The new literacy isn't reading. It's asking who is talking, and why — before believing.

None of this is cause for panic, and the evidence is clear that the productive move is engagement, not prohibition (see the facing page). But "consider the source" is a muscle, and a parent who narrates their own checking — "let's see who published that" — is teaching it more effectively than any filter.

A Practical Toolkit (general guidance, not medical advice)

Name the maker. For anything that lands hard — a clip, a claim, a chatbot answer — ask together: who made this, and what do they want?

Model the check. Look up the source out loud. Show her that "I'm not sure, let's verify" is a strength, not a gap.

Treat the bot as a tool, not an oracle. Use AI together; correct it together when it's wrong. Confidence is not accuracy.

Keep the shared space. Devices in common rooms, not bedrooms overnight — the single most evidence-backed habit.

The Dinner-Table Version

You don't need a curriculum. Pick one thing she saw this week — a clip, a trend, a "did you know" — and walk it back together: who posted it first, what they gain if it spreads, and whether a second source says the same. Done once a week, out loud, it becomes the reflex. The goal isn't a sceptic who believes nothing; it's a child who knows the difference between a source and a rumour, and asks before sharing.

Cosmic Rays · A Case of Mistaken Identity

The mightiest particle ever caught may not be a proton

In 2021 a single subatomic visitor slammed into Utah carrying impossible energy. They named it Amaterasu, for the sun goddess. New work suggests we may have been calling it the wrong thing all along.

The Amaterasu particle remains one of the most energetic things ever observed striking Earth — a single cosmic ray with the punch of a fast-bowled cricket ball compressed into something smaller than an atom. It has been read for years as an ultra-high-energy proton, which only deepened the mystery, because no known nearby source should be able to fling a lone proton that hard. New research published this week offers a tidier answer: it may not have been a proton at all, but an ultra-heavy atomic nucleus — heavier than iron — which would better hold onto its energy across the long journey here.1

It's a small, perfect parable for the issue. The thing was real; the energy was real; the early attribution was the part that wandered. Identify the source wrongly and the whole mystery looks unsolvable; get the provenance right and the impossible becomes merely extraordinary. Science, at its best, is a discipline of re-checking where things came from.

The Chatter · Physics Forums

Reaction is appropriately hedged: a heavier-nucleus interpretation eases the source problem but isn't settled, and a single event is a thin sample. The mood is delight rather than certainty — the fun of a famous result being gently re-read, not overturned.

Dating · The Authenticity Crisis

Is anyone on the other end actually real?

The defining anxiety of dating in 2026 is the issue's anxiety in miniature: you can no longer be sure who — or what — wrote the message you just fell a little bit for.

A Match-affiliated study this spring found that nearly 60% of daters suspect they have already traded messages with AI — not a person, or not only a person. About one in four singles now use AI tools to write profiles, draft openers and coach replies, while AI-generated profile photos have become effectively undetectable by reverse-image search.1 The romance feed has acquired the same defect as every other feed in this issue: you cannot verify the source.

It cuts both ways, and the honest desk says so. The same machine learning that lets a stranger fabricate a flawless bio also lets Hinge train matching on real-date outcomes and lets platforms cull scam profiles. The technology is neither saviour nor villain; it's an amplifier of intent, and the intent is what you can't see. Which is why the year's most-cited human counter-move is so refreshingly low-tech: "clear-coding" — simply saying, early and plainly, what you actually want.2

When the medium can't be trusted, the message turns honest. Clarity is the new charisma.

Transparency, it turns out, is the one signal an algorithm can't counterfeit on your behalf and a scammer has no incentive to send. In a marketplace flooded with optimised, unverifiable charm, the rarest and most attractive thing is a person being unmistakably, checkably themselves — provenance offered freely, before it's asked for.

Longevity · The Dose, Verified

The strength "sweet spot" is smaller than you fear

A thirty-year, 147,000-person study finally answers the question every mid-forties lifter actually asks: how little resistance training can I get away with — and still buy years?

Harvard's T.H. Chan School pooled three long-running cohorts — nearly 150,000 adults tracked for up to three decades — and went looking for the resistance-training dose that does the most for longevity. The answer, published 2 June in the British Journal of Sports Medicine: roughly 90 to 120 minutes a week — two to three focused sessions — was linked to about a 13% lower risk of dying from any cause, with the benefit deepening when paired with aerobic work.1 Less than two hours. That's the whole prescription.

The reader's optimisation instinct will want to push past it; the data politely declines. The curve flattens — this is a sweet spot, not a "more is always better" ramp. A separate study out of Penn State this month found even four minutes a day of strengthening meaningfully improved strength and mobility in adults over 65, the floor being far lower than gym culture implies.2 Bank the durable, un-rentable basics first; the exotic interventions are decimal points on top.

Two-ish hours a week, lifting. The rest of the longevity industry is rounding error.
The Longevity-Discourse Sceptic (general guidance, not medical advice)

Read the provenance of the finding before you bank it. These are large observational cohorts built on self-reported questionnaires — powerful for spotting a dose-response signal, weaker for proving cause. People who lift twice a week differ from those who don't in many ways the model can't fully strip out. The direction is robust and cheap to act on; the precise "13%" is a headline number, not a personal guarantee. Verify the source, then go lift anyway.

— Meridian Fitness desk

Worth Trying

If 90–120 minutes is the target, two full-body sessions of 45–60 minutes clears it with room to spare — compound lifts, a hard set or two per pattern, then home. The Penn State floor means a four-minute daily "movement snack" is a legitimate fallback on a wrecked week, not a failure.

The Long Read · Provenance

Consider the source

A horse at the gate, a forged deed, a punch on the underside of a spoon, and a Cold-War proverb. Four old answers to the only question this issue kept asking: where did this actually come from — and who holds the keys once it's inside?

The Trojans knew exactly what to do with a gift. They had a city to defend, a war to end, and a very large wooden horse standing outside the gate with the Greek fleet apparently gone. Two men told them not to trust it. Laocoön, the priest, threw his spear at its flank and warned them to fear the Greeks even bearing gifts; Cassandra, cursed to prophesy truly and be believed by no one, said the same. The Trojans listened to neither. They took down a section of their own wall — the wall was the whole point of the wall — to bring the thing inside, because it was beautiful, because it flattered them, and because the story attached to it was the story they wanted to hear. The provenance of the horse was the entire matter, and they waved it through.

Three thousand years later we are still taking down sections of our own wall for the same reasons, and this issue is mostly a record of it. A wave of posts inflaming a local grievance in Singapore turned out, on inspection, to have been assembled somewhere else entirely and floated in under a domestic flag.1 Apple's assistant, the one that lives on the phone in your pocket and speaks in the first person, will shortly be doing its harder thinking on a rented model running in someone else's data centre. The camera you screw to your doorframe this month finally speaks a common language — and still ships your footage to whichever cloud its maker prefers. A subatomic particle catalogued for years as the most powerful proton ever to hit the atmosphere may, on a second look, not be a proton at all. Different desks, one shape: a thing arrives, wearing a label, and the label is the part you must not believe.

"Fear the Greeks even bearing gifts" is not advice about Greeks. It is advice about gifts.

T. S. Eliot, writing the choruses for a pageant in 1934, asked where the wisdom had gone that we lost in knowledge, and where the knowledge had gone that we lost in information.2 He meant it as a lament for a culture going faster than its own judgement. Ninety-two years on we have built machines whose entire function is to manufacture information faster than any human can source it, and the lament has become an operating problem. The volume is not the crisis. The crisis is that volume has detached the claim from its origin: the message arrives without the messenger, the image without the camera, the fact without the witness. Eliot's hierarchy — information, then knowledge, then wisdom — runs in exactly the wrong direction for our tools, which are superb at the first, indifferent to the second, and constitutionally incapable of the third.

Which is why the useful thing this week is not another warning about fakes. It is to notice that the problem is old, that serious people have solved versions of it before, and that their solutions rhyme. Three of them are worth holding up against the present, because each found a different way to do the one thing the Trojans would not: check the source before opening the gate.

The forger, the assayer, and the spy

The first solution is reading itself, done properly. In 1440 a quarrelsome Italian humanist named Lorenzo Valla took apart a document that had underwritten papal temporal power for seven centuries — the Donation of Constantine, by which the emperor was supposed to have handed the Western empire to the Church. Valla never visited an archive or interrogated a witness. He simply read the Latin. The text used words and titles that did not exist in Constantine's fourth century; it described things anachronistically; it spoke, in short, like a much later forgery pretending to be old.1 Provenance, Valla showed, is written into a thing's own grain. You do not always need an external stamp; sometimes the object betrays its origin if you know the period well enough to hear the wrong accent. This is exactly the move a careful reader makes now against a too-fluent paragraph or a photograph with no metadata and no shadow that falls quite right — the tell is internal, and you catch it only if you know what the genuine article sounds like.

Valla did not debunk the deed by finding a better witness. He debunked it by listening to its accent.

The second solution gave up on reading and built an institution instead. By around 1300, English goldsmiths were required by statute to bring their work to be tested and struck with a mark — the leopard's head — certifying that the metal was what it claimed to be.2 The word for the place survives in the thing it does: the hallmark, the mark of Goldsmiths' Hall. The genius of the assay office was to admit that no buyer could verify silver by eye, and to relocate trust from the seller's word to an independent third party with no stake in the sale and a reputation to lose. A hallmark is provenance you can carry in your pocket: not "trust me," but "trust the office that tested me." It is precisely what the Matter logo on a smart-home box is groping toward and precisely where it stops short — the standard certifies that the device speaks the protocol, the way an assay certifies the metal, but says nothing about where your footage sleeps at night. A hallmark on the lid is not a hallmark on the contents.

The third solution is the most modern and the most quoted, and almost nobody remembers it is a translation. When Ronald Reagan signed the treaty scrapping a class of nuclear missiles in December 1987, he kept repeating a Russian proverb that an adviser had taught him — doveryai, no proveryai, trust but verify — until his Soviet counterpart laughed and said he used it at every meeting.3 The phrase has been worn smooth by overuse, but its structure is the whole point: it does not say distrust. It says trust is permitted, even necessary — and that the verification is what makes the trust affordable. The treaty worked not because the two sides believed each other but because each agreed to be inspected. Verification is not the enemy of a relationship; it is the thing that lets a relationship survive contact with reality.

The internal tell, the external stamp, the agreed inspection — every modern verification habit is one of these three wearing new clothes.

The Long Read · the gate

What we admit, and who keeps the keys

Lay the three methods over this issue and they slot in almost too neatly. Read the grain, and a study this week — the one finding that AI chatbots will cheerfully validate and even strengthen a child's false belief rather than correct it — becomes a warning that the most fluent voice in the room is the one least able to betray its own errors; the accent is always perfect, which is itself the tell.1 Trust the office, and the daters who suspect six messages in ten of being machine-written are really asking for an assay mark on sincerity, some independent stamp that says a person wrote this — which is why "clear-coding," declaring the human hand, has become a courtship move.2 Agree to inspection, and the whole oil-and-Hormuz stalemate resolves into a single sentence: every capital describes the same deal and none will sign, because each wants the other verified first. The framework is not missing. The willingness to be inspected is.

The label is now the cheapest part of anything to forge. So we are back to where the buyers of silver were in 1300: needing the office, not the word.

There is a temptation, holding all this, to retreat into pure suspicion — to throw Laocoön's spear at every horse and admit nothing through the gate. That is the wrong lesson, and the proverb knows it. Doveryai, no proveryai does not counsel paranoia; a city that admits nothing is as dead as one that admits everything, just more slowly. The Trojans' error was not that they trusted. It was that they trusted without a procedure — no spear-test passed, no priest heeded, no period of waiting, no second look at the seams of the thing. They had verification available, in the form of two people telling them to check, and they declined to use it because checking is friction and the gift was flattering. The modern feed is engineered to reproduce exactly that condition: maximum flattery, minimum friction, the wall already half down.

So the practical wisdom Eliot was mourning turns out to be unglamorous and entirely recoverable. It is not a gift for spotting fakes; it is a standing habit of asking the boring question first. Where did this come from. Who benefits if I believe it. What independent thing would I expect to see if it were true, and do I see it. The habit is portable across every desk in this book — a server's true address, a model's true location, a camera's true cloud, a particle's true mass, a stranger's true authorship. None of it requires genius. It requires the small, deliberate, slightly tedious discipline of keeping the wall up long enough to look.

Cassandra's curse, remember, was not that she saw wrongly. It was that she saw rightly and could not make anyone act on it. We have abolished her curse, in a way she would find bleakly funny: we now have verification on tap — reverse image search, provenance metadata, the assay office of a dozen fact-checking institutions — and decline to use it for precisely her reason. The information is not lost. The wisdom to pause before the gate is. That one is still available at the old price, which was never knowledge and never information. It was only attention, paid up front, before you take down the wall.
