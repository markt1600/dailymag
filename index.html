Houthis Blockade Saudi Arabia as the Gulf War Widens Further
A third American service member is dead and Iranian fire has now reached six Gulf states in 72 hours, as Yemen's Houthis open a new front against Riyadh in the Red Sea.

Two days after Tehran declared its Islamabad ceasefire framework dead, the war it was meant to end has widened rather than narrowed. Yemen's Houthi movement announced Monday that it is imposing a maritime "blockade" on Saudi Arabia through the Bab el-Mandeb strait — the corridor carrying roughly a tenth of world trade — retaliating, it said, for a Saudi-enforced blockade of Yemen and a strike on Sanaa's airport.1 The declaration lands as the United States confirmed a third American service member killed in the war's spillover in three days, taking the U.S. combat toll to 17.2

The geography keeps stretching. Iranian missiles and drones have now struck or been intercepted over Bahrain, Qatar, Oman and the UAE, joining Jordan, Kuwait and Saudi Arabia itself, as the U.S. flew its ninth consecutive night of strikes into Monday.3 Kuwait's state oil company reported "significant material damage and some injuries" after repeated hits on an offshore platform and a desalination plant near Shuwaikh — the second Gulf energy facility, after last week's terminal strike, pulled directly into the fighting.4

Markets are pricing the widening in real time. Brent closed Monday near $89 after President Trump warned Iran "will pay" for the American deaths — oil is up roughly a fifth this month, with traders watching whether slower Hormuz traffic plus the new Bab el-Mandeb threat push it past $100.5 The backdrop is a policy Washington half-reversed: Trump's proposed 20% "Guardian of the Strait" toll on Hormuz cargo lasted barely a day before being swapped for Gulf-state investment pledges, though the naval blockade on Iranian-flagged shipping remains.6 See p15.

Kyiv Hands Its War Ministry to a Strike Commander

Ukraine's defence ministry now has a war-fighter in the acting chair. Yevhen Khmara — until last week acting head of the SBU security service, and before that commander of its Alpha special-operations unit and long-range strike programme — took over as acting defence minister on 20 July, President Zelensky citing his "unprecedented experience in technology-driven strike operations" as exactly what wartime procurement and battlefield coordination need.1 Formal Rada confirmation, delayed by the chamber's summer recess, is now expected in mid-August rather than this week, First Deputy Speaker Oleksandr Kornienko says2 — leaving Khmara running the ministry through Russia's heaviest barrage of the summer: 41 ballistic and cruise missiles, including ten Zircon hypersonics, plus 125 attack drones against Kyiv overnight on 19 July, killing at least one person and wounding sixteen.3

Congo's Ebola Crosses 900 Deaths as WHO Calls It "a Fire"

The Bundibugyo-strain outbreak declared in eastern Congo on 15 May is now, by the WHO's own description, the fastest-spreading Ebola epidemic on record. Monday's health-ministry count put deaths at 930 and confirmed cases at 2,344 — up from 828 dead a week earlier — with 37 new deaths logged in a single 24-hour span over the weekend and most new infections now tracing to unknown chains of transmission.4 WHO emergencies chief Chikwe Ihekweazu's assessment: "This is a fire."5 The caveat worth holding onto alongside the alarm: researchers reported this week that the Bundibugyo species may respond better to existing therapeutics than the more familiar Zaire strain, though no vaccine or treatment is yet approved for it specifically.6

Gaza's "Ceasefire" Logs Its Bloodiest Week Since October

Nine months into the truce that ended the Gaza war, enforcement is fraying rather than holding. Gaza's health ministry says Israeli strikes have killed 1,127 Palestinians and wounded 3,643 since the October 2025 ceasefire took effect — including 14 killed in a single day last week, among them eight in a drone strike on a funeral procession at the Nuseirat refugee camp that the Israeli military said targeted Palestinian Islamic Jihad fighters.7 Neither side has declared the truce void; both continue to describe the strikes as enforcement, not a resumption of the war.

Faishal Ibrahim resigns as minister and MP over conduct lapse
A month-old email about "inappropriate interactions" ends a front-bench career within a day of reaching the Prime Minister's Office — the Home Affairs and Muslim Affairs portfolios reassigned same-day, no criminal charges brought against either party.

Associate Professor Muhammad Faishal Ibrahim, 58, resigned Monday as Senior Minister of State for Home Affairs, Acting Minister-in-Charge of Muslim Affairs, Member of Parliament for Marine Parade–Braddell Heights GRC and a member of the People's Action Party.1 The Prime Minister's Office says it received an email roughly a month earlier from a member of the public describing online exchanges with Faishal; both were interviewed separately, each subsequently alleged harassment against the other, and police investigated before concluding no criminal charges would be filed against either.2 Faishal accepted that his conduct "fell short of the standards expected," thanking his wife for standing by him and declining to say more out of respect for his family's privacy.2

Analysts read the same-day resolution as a deliberate signal rather than a reflex. Prime Minister Lawrence Wong had limited internal levers — the complainant was a private citizen, not a party member to be counselled through PAP's own machinery — leaving public resolution as the fastest way to close off leak risk and protect the "integrity and reputation of the institution."3 Senior Minister of State Zaqy Mohamad was named Acting Minister-in-Charge of Muslim Affairs within hours; some analysts now expect the episode to pull forward a wider Cabinet reshuffle.3

Jurong's second-CBD anchor site returns — smaller, and state-subsidised
URA relaunches the Town Hall Link white site at roughly half its old size, with the government now absorbing the district infrastructure bill — an unusually generous fix for a 2023 mega-parcel that drew one bid, deemed too low, and no award.

URA opened tender on 3 July for a 3.72ha white site at Town Hall Link in Jurong Lake District, the anchor plot of Singapore's long-stalled "second CBD" ambition, with results due at noon on 17 November.1 Capped at a 5.0 plot ratio, the site can yield up to 1,200 private homes, a minimum 40,000 sqm of office space, and roughly 44,000 sqm more for retail, serviced apartments, a hotel, clinics and community uses — directly linked by underground concourse to the Jurong East interchange (East-West, North-South and Jurong Region lines) and the future Cross Island Line station opening 2032.1

The site is a consolation prize with a purpose. It was carved from a much larger master-developer parcel that URA put up in 2023; that tender closed in 2024 with a single bid, judged too low, and no award followed — the failure that has kept Jurong's second-CBD ambition on paper since.2 Town Hall Link is roughly half that parcel's floor area, and this time the state is absorbing more of the risk directly: it will fund and build a district cooling plant and a pneumatic waste conveyance central station on-site to serve the wider precinct, infrastructure a developer would otherwise have had to underwrite itself.2 Analysts also note the presale quota effectively doubles versus the failed 2023 design, easing the upfront capital a winning developer must carry.3

Technology · Silicon & Sovereignty
The chokepoint moved from the chip to the gas cylinder
China's tungsten export curbs just took a quarter of the world's high-purity chipmaking gas offline — permanently. It's a smaller, quieter lever than an export ban, and it lands at exactly the manufacturing step a GPU's own memory stack depends on.

Two Japanese chemical makers, Kanto Denka and Central Glass, permanently shut down their tungsten hexafluoride (WF6) production lines from 1 July, after Beijing's tightened tungsten-export licensing left them unable to secure the raw feedstock; final delivery notices went out to Samsung, SK hynix and TSMC in mid-June, with last shipments on 30 June.1 WF6 is the unglamorous gas used to lay tungsten interconnects and contacts in advanced logic and memory chips — and the two firms supplied roughly a quarter of global high-purity capacity, against annual demand of 8,000–9,000 tonnes, leaving a shortfall industry trackers put near 2,000 tonnes for the second half of the year.1

It is a narrower, quieter lever than the blanket rare-earth curbs Beijing tested earlier this year — and it compounds a shortage this desk has been tracking from the other end for a fortnight now. Micron's own chief executive, Sanjay Mehrotra, told analysts on the company's June earnings call that tight memory supply will persist "beyond calendar 2027," with 2026 DRAM contract pricing potentially rising as much as 355% and NAND as much as 510% — hard numbers behind the RTX 5090's now-familiar sticker shock (see p8).2

The Chatter

Nvidia's own market barely blinked: shares pushed the company past a $5 trillion market cap on 13 July, even as a SemiAnalysis report of a year-plus delay to its next-generation "Kyber" rack architecture briefly rattled the stock. Nvidia calls the roadmap "intact," targeting a second-half-2027 launch — p15 has the fuller market read.3

Sovereignty, the DIY version

Away from the chokepoints, the frontier labs are hedging their own Nvidia dependence directly. Anthropic is in early, preliminary talks with Meta to lease roughly $10bn of AI compute capacity, CNBC reported on 17 July, while separately exploring a custom AI accelerator tuned to Claude with Samsung — the same playbook Google, Amazon and Meta are already running, and that OpenAI is pursuing through its own $10bn Broadcom collaboration.4

The Home Lab
The shortage just got an admission and a deadline attached
Micron's own CEO now says tight memory supply won't ease before 2028. Apple's next Mac Studio has slipped for the same reason. The pragmatic home-lab build hasn't changed — the runway to a cheaper one just got longer.

Micron's chief executive, Sanjay Mehrotra, told analysts on the company's fiscal Q3 earnings call that tight memory supply will persist "beyond calendar 2027" — the plainest admission yet, from the industry's own vendor, that this isn't a hiccup correcting itself on a normal foundry timeline. DRAM spot prices are up 52% since January; 2026 DRAM contract pricing could rise as much as 355%, NAND as much as 510%, on Micron's own numbers.1

The RTX 5090 is still listing around $4,329 on Amazon, largely unchanged from last fortnight, but the squeeze is spreading sideways. AMD has told AIB partners to expect roughly a 10% increase on GPU-and-GDDR memory bundle kits from this month — its second such hike in six months, so Radeon pricing is now moving with the same memory market as Nvidia's.2 Even Nvidia's own DGX Spark hasn't been spared: the Founders Edition MSRP was raised from its $3,999 launch price to $4,699 this year, Nvidia citing the same DRAM/NAND shortage.2 Apple has felt it too — the next Mac Studio (M5 Max/Ultra) has slipped to October at the earliest, first reported in April and still holding as of this month's supply-chain coverage, reportedly because Apple can't yet secure memory at the configurations it wants.3

None of which changes the pragmatic answer for this reader's own bench: a secondhand RTX 3090 (24GB VRAM) paired with Ollama and ComfyUI remains the rational build, and there's genuinely little new to report on either tool this fortnight — Ollama sits at v0.32.1 (16 Jul) and ComfyUI at v0.28.0 (15 Jul), both already covered here, with no successor release found as this issue closed.4

What The Forums Say

The DGX Spark's 128GB unified memory keeps drawing interest from builders chasing 200-billion-parameter models that a 3090, or even a 5090, simply can't hold — but the recurring pushback across Nvidia's own developer forums and local-LLM communities is that an RTX 5090-plus-Threadripper build at a similar price delivers higher raw throughput, and that AMD's Ryzen AI Max+ 395 offers a comparable unified-memory approach at roughly half the cost. The rough consensus: Spark buys capacity, not speed, and stays a niche pick even among Nvidia loyalists.5

The weights actually worth waiting for

For a lab that self-hosts rather than rents, the two dates worth calendaring are both open-weight releases: Alibaba's Qwen3.8-Max (2.4 trillion parameters), previewed 19 July with weights "coming soon," and Moonshot's Kimi K3 (2.8 trillion, mixture-of-experts), API-only since 16 July with its weight release on track for 27 July.6 Neither is confirmed to fit on a single consumer card even quantised — worth waiting for the community's first GGUF conversions first.

A child-safety group's stress test of Google's AI search features found real problems — using a methodology built to find them. General guidance, not medical advice.

Common Sense Media's Youth AI Safety Institute published a risk assessment of Google's AI Overview and AI Mode search features on 15 July, testing them through Google accounts configured for 11- and 15-year-olds with SafeSearch enabled. Across more than 2,600 test searches and over 2,100 audited AI-generated answers, the features failed all five of the report's defined "red line" severe-harm tests and scored poorly on seven of its eight safety principles. The most concrete findings: AI Overview missed 29% of explicit statements referring to suicide and roughly half of more indirect ones; both features gave step-by-step instructions for creating deepfakes usable for bullying; and both answered homework questions outright rather than prompting a student to work through them.1

Google's on-the-record response was that the test set used "a narrow set of ambiguous and contrived queries" that don't reflect how people actually use Search — a fair question to sit with, since the report's authors deliberately designed adversarial prompts to probe for failure, rather than sampling how an average 11-year-old actually searches on an ordinary afternoon.2 That doesn't make the findings false. It does mean "unacceptable risk" is a rating from one advocacy organisation's own rubric applied to a stress test, not an independent clinical or regulatory finding — worth knowing before treating the headline as a verdict rather than an audit.

A stress test that finds real cracks is not the same thing as an independent verdict that the product is unsafe for every child, every time it's used.
The Sceptic's Take

Read together with p13's Roblox settlements, the honest pattern across this week's child-safety stories is the same one: real, specific, documented problems — a missed suicide-risk cue, a deepfake how-to, a five-state settlement bill — sitting inside reporting that reaches for the most alarming possible framing of them. Both things can be true at once: the underlying problem is real, and the framing is doing some of the emotional work the evidence alone doesn't quite support.

Your handshake might be buffering your brain
General guidance, not medical advice. A new grip-strength finding is genuinely interesting — and easy to over-read.

A study out of Washington University, published this year in GeroScience, finds that grip strength changes how blood-based Alzheimer's biomarkers relate to actual cognitive performance. Researchers cross-sectionally analysed 348 people from the Aging Adult Brain Connectome cohort, measuring plasma markers of Alzheimer's pathology — phosphorylated tau-217, GFAP, neurofilament light chain — against a cognitive-composite score. Grip strength, treated as a marker of frailty and functional reserve, modified how strongly biomarker burden tracked with cognition, the effect more pronounced in women.1

Read this carefully, because it's easy to over-claim: the study is cross-sectional, capturing everyone at a single point rather than following them over time. It cannot yet say a stronger grip protects the brain, only that the two are statistically entangled in this cohort, more so in women. Whether training grip would shift anyone's risk is a question this dataset wasn't built to answer.

The Longevity-Discourse Warning Label

Grip strength has been a fixture of biohacker and longevity-podcast content for years, usually cited via a much older, much broader literature — a roughly 11-pound (5kg) drop in grip strength associated with a 16% jump in all-cause mortality risk, drawn from population studies like PURE and UK Biobank dating back a decade or more. That's a real and separately well-replicated finding about grip and general mortality. It is not the same claim as this week's narrower, brand-new one about grip strength and Alzheimer's-biomarker interpretation specifically — the online discourse tends to blur the two into one big "grip strength predicts everything" headline, which neither study actually supports on its own.

Idea To Try: Train The Grip On Purpose

Grip strength isn't a separate program to add — it's a byproduct of loaded carries, dead hangs, mixed-grip pulls and anything that forces the hand to hold on under real tension, all of which fit inside the resistance-training window this desk has already covered at length this year. Farmer's carries for distance or time, a 30-to-60-second dead hang between sets, and simply not using lifting straps on pulling movements are the low-effort additions; none require restructuring a program that's already hitting a reasonable weekly resistance-training dose.2

A stronger handshake correlating with a healthier brain doesn't yet mean training the handshake changes the brain. It's a genuinely new finding — worth watching, not worth over-interpreting.

Creatine's Evidence Base Keeps Widening

Coverage this month reinforced a trend building through 2026: creatine's evidence extending past muscle into brain function and bone health, with a research focus on post-menopausal women. Paired with resistance training, 3–5g daily has shown lean-mass gains in older adults, though effects in women run more modest than in men (~0.5–0.6kg vs 1.1–1.5kg) — a real but smaller benefit.3

The Narrowest Point
A strait, a manufacturing gas, a single desalination plant, one email: this week's news kept turning on parts too small or too boring to have been defended — the places a system is actually weakest. Three episodes, two thousand years apart, about what happens when someone finds the narrow point first.

There is an old proverb, popular enough by Benjamin Franklin's day that he printed a version of it in Poor Richard's Almanack, that runs downhill from something tiny to something enormous: for want of a nail the shoe was lost, for want of a shoe the horse was lost, for want of a horse the rider, the message, the battle, the kingdom — "and all for the want of a horseshoe nail."1 It is a children's rhyme and a logistics doctrine at the same time. Its real subject is not nails. Its subject is the uncomfortable fact that a system's fate is usually decided not at its strongest or most defended point but at its narrowest one — the part small enough, cheap enough, or dull enough that nobody thought it worth protecting until it failed.

This issue is, almost accidentally, a catalogue of narrow points. On p3, an entire regional war keeps concentrating itself onto two thin bands of water — the Strait of Hormuz and now the Bab el-Mandeb — through which a tenth or more of the world's trade has to physically pass, and which a single determined actor can threaten with mines, drones and a declaration. On p7, the semiconductor industry discovered that a quarter of the planet's supply of one obscure manufacturing gas, tungsten hexafluoride, ran through two Japanese factories that could be switched off not by bombing them but by quietly denying them a Chinese raw material. On p15, the same Gulf war reached past oil terminals to a single desalination plant supplying a Gulf state's drinking water. And on p5, a front-bench career that took decades to build ended inside a day, undone by one email from one member of the public.

A system's fate is decided not at its strongest point but at its narrowest — the part too cheap or too dull to have been defended.

Put like that, the week looks less like a run of unrelated stories and more like the same story told at four scales. Each involves something load-bearing that turned out to rest on a part far smaller than the thing it held up. And in each, the danger was not that the narrow point was fragile — narrow points usually work perfectly, for years — but that its very reliability made it invisible. You do not stockpile a gas that has never run short. You do not build a second strait.

The oldest and clearest illustration of the opposite instinct — of a power that saw its narrow point and guarded it obsessively — belongs to Augustus. When Rome absorbed Egypt in 30 BC, after Actium and Cleopatra's death, Augustus did something he did with no other province: he kept it as a personal possession, governed by a knight answerable directly to him, and forbade any Roman senator from so much as setting foot in it without his permission.2

The reason was grain. Egypt fed something like a third of the city of Rome through the annona, and Augustus understood that whoever controlled the grain controlled the mob, and whoever controlled the mob could unmake an emperor. Egypt was not Rome's biggest province or its most defended frontier. It was its narrowest point — and Augustus treated the man who might stand on it as more dangerous than any army.2

Augustus had the easy version of the problem. His narrow point sat inside his own empire; he could simply garrison it and bar the door. The harder version — the one this week's technology pages actually describe — is when your narrow point sits inside someone else's territory, held by a rival who can close it at will. You cannot garrison that. You can only try to make it matter less.

The classic case is a century old and worth retelling because it rhymes so exactly. Before 1914, the entire industrial world's supply of fixed nitrogen — for fertiliser, and for the explosives every army needed — came overwhelmingly from one place: the saltpetre deposits of the Atacama Desert in Chile, mined largely by British firms and shipped across sea lanes the British navy controlled. Germany imported hundreds of thousands of tonnes of Chilean nitrate a year.1 The moment war came, the British blockade did to that supply exactly what you would expect: it closed the strait, metaphorically speaking, and left Germany's munitions industry staring at a chokepoint held entirely by its enemy.

Germany's answer to a chokepoint it could not seize was to make the chokepoint irrelevant — to synthesise the thing itself.

What happened next is the part that matters. Germany did not defeat the blockade; it never controlled the sea. Instead, a chemist named Fritz Haber had, in the years just before the war, worked out how to pull nitrogen out of the air itself and fix it into ammonia, and Carl Bosch and BASF had industrialised it at Oppau by 1913. The Haber–Bosch process let Germany manufacture, at home, from atmosphere, the very thing the blockade was meant to deny it. Historians generally agree that without it Germany would have run out of both bread and shells within a year.1 The narrow point had not been captured. It had been engineered out of existence.

This is precisely the manoeuvre the frontier AI labs are now attempting, and it is why the otherwise dry procurement items on p7 belong in an essay about war and grain. Every serious lab has spent 2026 discovering that its own narrow point is a single supplier — Nvidia — whose hardware it cannot manufacture and whose allocation it does not control. The response is not to seize Nvidia. It is to build a domestic substitute: Anthropic exploring a custom accelerator with Samsung and leasing compute from Meta, OpenAI designing its own silicon with Broadcom, Google and Amazon already years into their own chips.2 Each is looking for its Haber–Bosch — a way to make the chokepoint matter less by no longer depending on it.

Redundancy always looks wasteful right up until the day it isn't. That is exactly why chokepoints form: nobody funds a second supply against a first that has never failed.

There is a reason this is hard, and it is not stupidity. Redundancy is expensive, and against a supply line that has worked flawlessly for years it looks like pure waste — a second factory nobody needs, a strategic reserve of a gas that has never run short, a home-grown chip slower and dearer than the one you can simply buy. Every quarter you do not build it, you are rewarded for the saving. The bill comes due only once, all at once, on the day the narrow point is finally squeezed — which is precisely why so few institutions pay it in advance, and why the ones that do (Augustus garrisoning Egypt, Haber working before the war) look in hindsight less lucky than disciplined.

The difference between the labs and Germany, and it is a large one, is time. Haber had his process ready before the blockade closed; Germany's redundancy existed the day it was needed. The labs are building theirs while the dependence is already total and the shortage, as Micron's own chief executive conceded this week, is now forecast past 2027 (p8). A substitute you are still designing when the chokepoint tightens is not a substitute. It is a hope.

And some narrow points have no Haber waiting. The tungsten-hexafluoride story is the genuinely frightening one precisely because nobody was working on a bypass — the gas was too boring, too reliable, too cheap to have earned a contingency plan. It was the horseshoe nail: unnoticed until the moment it was gone.

There is a third way a narrow point can decide events, and it is the one the belligerents in the Gulf should study most carefully, because it is the trap of the party that thinks it holds the chokepoint. The Chinese commentary quoted on p3 reached, unprompted, for the analogy — the United States in the Strait of Hormuz, a Global Times writer suggested, resembles Britain at Suez in 1956.1 It is worth taking the comparison seriously rather than dismissing it as propaganda, because Suez is the definitive modern lesson in mistaking a chokepoint for power.

Britain in 1956 believed the Suez Canal was a thing it controlled, and that controlling it conferred strength. When Nasser nationalised the canal and Britain, France and Israel moved to take it back by force, the operation was a military success and a strategic catastrophe: American financial pressure — a run on the pound Washington declined to stop — forced a humiliating withdrawal within weeks, and the episode is generally marked as the moment the world learned Britain was no longer a power that could act alone.1 The chokepoint Britain thought it held turned out to be holding Britain, exposing exactly how narrow its own real margin had become.

The party that assumes it holds the chokepoint may discover, at the worst moment, that the chokepoint has been holding it.

That is the warning under the whole Hormuz confrontation. Every party to it — Iran threatening the strait, Washington proposing tolls to "guard" it (p4), the Gulf states caught in the blast radius — is behaving as though the narrow point is an asset to be owned. Suez suggests it is more often a liability that owns you: the harder you grip a chokepoint, the more of your own strength you reveal to be riding on it, and the more you invite the discovery that your margin was always thinner than the map implied.

There is an asymmetry here worth making explicit, because it decides who wins these confrontations. The party that merely threatens a chokepoint and the party that depends on it are not playing the same game. The threatener risks little — a mine, a drone, a declaration — and needs only to make passage uncertain, not to close it. The dependent party must keep the point open every single day, and is judged by its worst day, not its average one. That is why a comparatively weak actor can hold a strait against a far stronger one, and why the stronger party's instinct — to grip harder, to escalate, to prove control — is so often the move that exposes exactly how little slack it had. Britain discovered this in eleven days in 1956. The lesson has not aged.

Set the three episodes side by side and a rough decision tree emerges, the one every actor in this issue is somewhere inside. First: can you see your narrow point? Augustus could, and guarded it; the semiconductor industry could not, and lost a gas. Second: if the point sits in a rival's hands, can you build redundancy — your own Haber–Bosch — before it is used against you? Germany could; the AI labs are racing to, and may not finish in time. Third, and subtlest: are you the one who thinks he holds the chokepoint? Because that is Suez, and Suez is where confidence in a narrow point becomes the very thing that breaks you.

Thomas Reid, writing in 1786, gave the whole idea its cleanest form: "In every chain of reasoning, the evidence of the last conclusion can be no greater than that of the weakest link of the chain, whatever may be the strength of the rest."2 He was talking about arguments, not empires or supply chains, but the geometry is identical. What holds a thing up is never its average strength; it is its minimum. The rest of the chain can be magnificent and it will not matter.

Which leaves the genuinely hard part, the one Reid's tidy sentence quietly omits. You usually cannot tell in advance which link is weakest. The nail looks like every other nail right up until the shoe comes off. So the real discipline — for a war cabinet, a chip industry, a lab, or a government that just lost a minister to a single email — is not the heroic one of strengthening the whole chain, which is impossible, but the humbler, more boring one of going looking for the narrowest point on purpose, and paying it the attention it has done nothing yet to earn. This week, that narrow point was a strait, a gas cylinder, a desalination plant and an inbox. Next week it will be something else that has been working flawlessly, unremarked, for years — right up until it isn't.
