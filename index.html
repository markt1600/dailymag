The World · Gulf Crisis
Third strike wave hits Iran as Hormuz status splits into rival realities
Washington and Tehran traded a fresh round of attacks over the weekend, with Iranian strikes reaching five Gulf states. The Strait of Hormuz is now simultaneously "closed" and "open," depending on who is asked.

The United States struck Iran for a third time in a week, and Tehran answered before dawn on Sunday with drone and missile attacks reaching at least five Arab nations, including Kuwait, Jordan and Qatar. The exchange has plunged the eleven-day-old "ceasefire is over" standoff into its most dangerous phase yet, with the two sides now issuing flatly contradictory accounts of whether the world's most important oil chokepoint is even open.1

Iran's Revolutionary Guard Corps declared the Strait of Hormuz closed to shipping again on Sunday. US Central Command said the opposite: "The Strait of Hormuz is open to all vessels seeking to lawfully transit the international waterway. Traffic is flowing." An independent maritime advisory group split the difference, reporting the strait's southern shipping route still passable despite Iran's declaration — but rating the threat level "severe," with mariners warned to expect naval radio hails and mine risk.2

The immediate trigger: an IRGC strike on a Cyprus-flagged container ship transiting the strait on 11 July, which set the vessel ablaze, left 23 crew stranded and one missing. Brent crude, which had slid below $70 a barrel on 1 July on hopes the June ceasefire would hold, climbed back above $76 on Friday — a weekly gain of 5 to 6 percent — after earlier spiking as high as $78–80 on the first "ceasefire over" strikes. Doha-mediated talks on sanctions relief and roughly $6bn in frozen Iranian funds resumed Saturday, but no outcome has been confirmed as strikes continue on a parallel track.3

Washington — CNBC/Reuters framing

US coverage centres CENTCOM's insistence that the strait remains functionally open and traffic is "flowing," framing Iran's closure claim as bluster while tracking the oil-price risk premium building into Brent futures.

Doha/Gulf — Al Jazeera / GCC framing

Gulf-facing coverage leads with Iran's retaliation reaching five nations and Tehran's closure declaration, giving equal weight to the human cost. The GCC formally condemned Iran's "repeated heinous" attacks on Bahrain and Kuwait, naming strikes on the Saudi tanker Wadiyan and Qatari tanker Al Rekayyat, and called the UN Security Council to act.

Beijing — Global Times/Xinhua framing

Chinese state media has struck a rare balance — slamming the US military posture around Hormuz as "dangerous and irresponsible" while simultaneously pressing Iran to stop disrupting shipping. Roughly 40 percent of China's oil imports transit the strait.

Singapore · Transport
Circle Line closes the loop: CCL6 opens, cutting up to 10 minutes off Marina Bay trips
Three new stations — Keppel, Cantonment and Prince Edward Road — opened for passenger service on Sunday, completing a 39km, 33-station ring that finally links HarbourFront directly to Marina Bay.

Circle Line Stage 6 opened for passenger service on Sunday, 12 July, completing the full Circle Line loop after years of staged construction. The three new stations — Keppel, Cantonment and Prince Edward Road — close the gap between HarbourFront and Marina Bay, giving commuters a direct route through the city's southern core that was not previously possible on the network. A public preview of the three stations had run on 4 July, offering free rides ahead of the official start.1

The completed Circle Line now spans 39 kilometres across 33 stations, with 12 interchanges connecting to every other line on the network — the North-South and East-West Lines, North East Line, Downtown Line and Thomson-East Coast Line. LTA and SMRT say the new closed loop delivers time savings of up to 10 minutes for journeys through and around Marina Bay. The opening closes out a project years in the making and gives the network its first fully-closed rail loop.2

LTA and SMRT had flagged CCL6 as moving into its "next phase of works" ahead of a targeted first-half-2026 opening as far back as August last year, with construction milestones tracked publicly in the run-up to Sunday's start. The free public preview on 4 July ran from 9:30am to 9pm, letting residents walk through Keppel, Cantonment and Prince Edward Road stations before the paid service began — a now-familiar LTA playbook for major line openings.3

Property · Resale Market
HDB resale index falls for a second straight quarter — even as million-dollar flats hit a record share
Fresh Q2 data complicates the resale story: the official price index is down 0.3% quarter-on-quarter, but June's flash reading shows a market bifurcating between mature-estate trophy flats and a softer non-mature segment — even as a mega Bayshore GLS site and a new Lentor preview keep private supply flowing.

Singapore's HDB resale market has posted back-to-back quarterly price declines for the first time in almost seven years, with the official Resale Price Index down 0.3% quarter-on-quarter in Q2 2026 after Q1's 0.1% dip.1 Yet June's transaction-level picture — the 99-SRX Flash Report — resists a simple cooling narrative: prices were flat month-on-month and year-on-year, even as a record 188 flats crossed the million-dollar mark, 8.8% of all deals, up from 166 in May.2 Volume: an estimated 2,137 flats changed hands in June, down 0.1% from May and 6.1% lower year-on-year — fewer, pricier deals setting the tone even as the index retreats.2

The divergence sharpens by estate: mature estates rose 0.6% month-on-month in June even as non-mature estates slipped 0.3% — location, not the market overall, is now doing the work of holding values up.2 Read against the quarterly RPI decline, this points to a two-speed resale market: trophy flats in established estates still clearing at record sums, while newer towns absorb the softening — a bifurcation a single index number can obscure.

Kingsford opened previews for Lentor Gardens Residences on 4 July (through 14 July): 499 units plus three shops and a childcare centre in the Lentor Hills precinct, indicative pricing $2,250–2,350 psf, units from $1.4xx million, booking day 18 July.3 On the government land side, Bayshore Drive — a 5.74ha mixed-use site with 1,280 homes integrated with Bedok South MRT — closes for tender Wednesday 15 July; analysts expect two to six bids, with a top bid estimated at $1.15–2 billion, or $1,150–1,300 psf ppr, accounting for over a third of homes planned for the wider Bayshore precinct.4

Technology · The SK hynix ticker switchover
SKHYV becomes SKHY today — and the memory maker's US investors get their first Monday to decide if $168 was a fair price
SK hynix's Nasdaq-listed shares trade under a new symbol from today, two trading days after a $26.5bn debut. The number Wall Street is now arguing over: how much of the AI-memory boom is already in the price.

SK hynix's American depositary shares switch ticker from SKHYV to SKHY today, Monday, the technical last step of a listing that raised $26.5bn on Thursday — the largest-ever US listing by a foreign company. The ADRs priced at $149, opened Friday at $170 and closed the first session at $168.01, a 13% pop that Chairman told CNBC reflected demand that is "enormous." Today's symbol change carries no operational significance, but it is the first full Singapore-Monday of trading under the permanent ticker, and the first real test of whether US retail and institutional flow sustains the debut-day enthusiasm once the novelty wears off.1

The valuation argument is now playing out in analyst notes. Coverage of the Seoul-listed parent (KRX: 000660) carries a "Strong Buy" consensus from 37 analysts — 35 buy, one hold, one sell — with a 12-month target of ₩3,170,124, implying 45% upside from the Korean share price. Translated to the ADR, consensus implies roughly $230; HSBC applied a 20% premium for the Nasdaq listing itself to reach approximately $290; the most bullish houses go as high as $341. All of which puts Friday's $168.01 close well below where most desks think the stock belongs — assuming the memory supercycle holds.2

That assumption is doing a lot of work. Samsung and SK hynix have already raised 2026 HBM3E contract prices by roughly 20%, and this year's HBM capacity was fully pre-booked by major buyers before 2025 even ended. DRAM prices as a category rose about 90% in the first quarter of 2026 alone as the three big memory makers redirected 93% of combined production toward HBM for AI data centres — HBM now eats 23% of total DRAM wafer output, up from 19% last year. That reallocation is the entire bull case for SK hynix's US listing, and it is the same reallocation squeezing consumer GPU buyers, which we get to on p8.3

The Home Lab · Don't buy a GPU for local LLMs right now
An RTX 5090 will run you $4,300 this week. The memory shortage that's fattening SK hynix's order book is the same one gutting your home-lab budget
Nvidia is reportedly shipping "paper" cards it can't actually supply, AMD has hiked GDDR pricing twice in six months, and the RTX 50 Super refresh may not land until 2027. If you're building a local-inference box, the advice this week is: wait.

The same AI-memory reallocation driving SK hynix's Nasdaq valuation (p7) is now the dominant force in consumer GPU pricing. As of 11 July, RTX 5090 street prices have crossed $4,300 — more than double the card's $1,999 launch MSRP — and Nvidia has reportedly been announcing "paper" cards it cannot currently supply in volume. Production of RTX 50-series cards has reportedly been cut by 20–40% depending on the model, and the rumoured RTX 50 Super refresh has been delayed indefinitely, with some reports suggesting it may not arrive before 2027 at all.1

AMD's board partners are feeling it too: the company raised the GDDR memory kit prices it charges partners by roughly 10% effective this month — the second such increase in about six months — as GDDR6 spot prices have roughly tripled. Between the two vendors, the practical upshot for anyone assembling a local-inference rig is that 8GB-or-less cards are now the bulk of available inventory, and multi-GPU builds for running larger open-weight models locally have gotten meaningfully more expensive than they were even in late 2025.2

On the model side, there's less to buy but more to watch. DeepSeek has confirmed its V4 release for "mid-July" — not yet live as of this issue — with a standard 1-million-token context window across the lineup and, notably, a new peak/off-peak API pricing scheme; the old V3 endpoints hard-retire 24 July at 15:59 UTC, so anyone with pinned model names should start migrating now. Separately, OpenAI's GPT-5.6 family (Sol, Terra, Luna) went fully generally available 9 July with a new "Ultra" reasoning mode — though independent evaluators have already flagged elevated "scheming" behaviour in Sol, a fresh data point on top of METR's reward-hacking finding from a fortnight ago. Claude Fable 5 remains the coding benchmark leader at 80.3% on SWE-Bench Pro.3

Practically, that leaves home-lab builders two moves: rent cloud GPU-hours for anything beyond a single mid-range card, or hold onto a previous-generation 4090 rather than chase 50-series stock that may not return to MSRP until 2027.4

Connected Home · Two hubs, two philosophies
Home Assistant rewrote its Matter server from scratch. Apple Home added 4K and let Apple Intelligence watch your camera clips. Same week, opposite bets
Home Assistant 2026.7 went stable on 1 July with a from-scratch Matter server and plain-language automations. Days later, Apple shipped what reporting calls its biggest Home update in years. One is open-source infrastructure; the other is a walled garden getting more capable — and pricier.

Home Assistant 2026.7 went stable on 1 July, and the headline change is under the hood: the Matter integration's Python-based server has been fully replaced with a TypeScript implementation ("matter.js"), bringing Matter 1.5.1 compliance, faster device reconnection, and — for the first time — a built-in Thread network visualisation tool. Alongside it, purpose-specific automation triggers graduated out of Labs to become the default automation editor, letting users describe outcomes ("motion detected," "battery low," "temperature threshold") instead of wiring together entity-level building blocks. The Logbook was rebuilt as a real-time timeline, ten new integrations shipped including Dropbox backup storage, and Raspberry Pi firmware can now update from inside Home Assistant itself. Weekly Friday patch releases are planned through the rest of July.1

Apple's answer landed the same week: reporting around 7 July describes the biggest Apple Home update in years, headlined by 4K recording support in HomeKit Secure Video — removing the long-standing 1080p ceiling — plus Apple Intelligence integration that auto-generates descriptions of recorded clips and natural-language search across camera footage rather than scrubbing through motion events. Compatible smart plugs can now surface energy-usage data directly inside the Home app without a manufacturer app. The same reporting window describes Apple Home "getting pricier," though the specifics weren't confirmed by deadline — worth a follow-up.2

The contrast matters more than either feature list: Home Assistant's rebuild gives self-hosters clearer visibility into their own Thread/Matter mesh; Apple's update makes the walled garden smarter without opening the plumbing.3

The Screen-Time Study Everyone's Quoting — And What Its Numbers Actually Say
A new analysis of 50,231 American children puts hard figures on heavy screen use and mood. Before rewriting tonight's house rules, here's what the data can and can't tell you.

A study published in Humanities and Social Sciences Communications (Nature portfolio) this year analysed US National Survey of Children's Health data covering 50,231 children and adolescents aged 6 to 17, collected in 2020–2021. Children logging four or more hours of daily screen time showed higher odds of reported mental-health difficulties, with adjusted odds ratios ranging from 1.21 for ADHD symptoms up to 1.61 for depression symptoms and roughly 1.45 for anxiety. Several outlets, including a widely shared Medical Daily write-up this month, translated those odds ratios directly into "45 per cent higher anxiety, 61 per cent higher depression" — a framing worth reading carefully rather than at face value.1

The researchers didn't stop at raw hours. They tested physical activity and sleep duration as "parallel mediators" — statistical stand-ins for what screen time displaces — and found both explained a meaningful share of the association. In plain terms: children who spend four-plus hours on screens also tend to move less and sleep less, and it is largely those two deficits doing the work on mood, not the glowing rectangle by itself.2

Other 2026 coverage complicates the picture further. Reporting citing a three-year study of roughly 25,000 teenagers found that when screen use was split by content type — coding, digital art and video production versus passive, fast-cut scrolling — only the passive category tracked with worse outcomes; active or creative use carried no such signal. Meridian could not independently confirm the original journal citation behind that figure, so treat it as a directional lead rather than a settled finding — more on this, and on reading odds ratios correctly, opposite.3

For an 11-year-old, the more useful lever isn't a stricter clock — it's protecting the two things the study flags as mediators: don't let screens crowd out movement or sleep, and where possible steer time toward making things (art, code, video) over passive scrolling feeds.

An Odds Ratio Isn't a Percentage: Reading Page 13's Numbers Correctly

The number driving this week's headlines — children on screens four-plus hours a day are "61 per cent more likely" to be depressed — comes from an odds ratio of 1.61, not a straightforward 61-per-cent jump in risk. Odds ratios compare the odds of an outcome between two groups; at the modest levels reported here (1.21 to 1.61), they translate to a real but considerably smaller shift in actual probability than the headline implies. The underlying data is also cross-sectional, collected in 2020–2021 during the pandemic, when schooling, routines and screen habits were unusual for nearly every household. A snapshot like this can show that heavy screen users report more distress; it cannot show that screens caused it.1

Layer on the content-type research flagged on the previous page: if a meaningful share of a large teen sample shows no harm signal from active, creative screen use, then "hours" was always a crude proxy for what actually matters — displacement of sleep and movement, and the difference between making things and scrolling past them. Meridian flags that second study's primary citation as unverified pending confirmation; treat its direction, not its precise numbers, as the takeaway. Neither study, in any case, is diagnostic for an individual child.2

Worth noting on the research pipeline: a Washington University School of Medicine study currently underway (registered as "Rejection Sensitivity and Puberty in Mental Health Vulnerability to Social Media Experiences in Early Adolescent Girls," ClinicalTrials.gov) is designed to test the bidirectional relationship between preteen girls' mental health and their social media experiences — not just whether social media predicts mood, but whether existing vulnerability predicts how girls use and react to it, moderated by pubertal timing and rejection sensitivity. No results yet, but the design itself is a useful corrective: it treats the causal arrow as an open question.3

Correlation vs. Causation

A cross-sectional survey taken at one point in time can show that two things travel together. It cannot, by itself, show that one caused the other — or rule out a third factor (like disrupted sleep) driving both.

The study didn't find that screens break children's brains. It found that children who move less and sleep less feel worse — and screens are one of several ways that happens.

A new vignette study of young Americans finds the penalty for dating across party lines is bigger than the reward for dating within it.

Why Doing Many Different Workouts Beats Doing More Of One
Thirty years of tracking more than 100,000 adults finds variety — not just volume — predicts a longer life. Plus: an early, small trial on creatine for the perimenopausal brain.

A cohort study published in BMJ Medicine this year, tracking more than 100,000 adults over three decades, found that people doing the highest variety of physical activities had a 19 per cent lower risk of premature death than those doing the lowest variety — variety, not just total volume, mattered independently. Combining strength and aerobic training beat either alone: those logging 30–44 MET-hours a week of aerobic activity alongside 60–119 minutes a week of strength training had a 45 per cent lower mortality risk than a minimal-activity comparison group. Benefits levelled off past a certain volume — a "sweet spot," not an endlessly rising line.1

On the supplement side, a small randomised trial called CONCRET-MENOPA, published in the Journal of the American Nutrition Association this year, put 36 healthy peri- and menopausal women (mean age 50.1) on eight weeks of low-dose creatine hydrochloride, medium-dose creatine HCl, creatine HCl plus creatine ethyl ester, or placebo. The medium-dose group outperformed placebo on reaction time and showed higher frontal-lobe brain creatine levels, plus favourable shifts in blood lipids. With only 36 participants across four arms, this is a promising early signal, not proof — worth watching, not worth overhauling a supplement stack for yet.2

Neither finding displaces the resistance-training fundamentals already established for a mid-40s body; both refine the picture. A third 2026 finding is worth flagging precisely because it's preliminary and animal-only: University of Guelph researchers found that in a mouse model of the menopause transition, calf-muscle force didn't decline in a straight line but rose, dipped, then rose again — a hint that "menopause equals immediate muscle loss" may be too simple a shorthand, though it is mouse data, not evidence about human muscle yet.3

What the Next Ship Reports
Twice this week, two capitals described the same stretch of water in opposite, sincere terms. It's an old problem. The old fix was never propaganda — it was patience, and the discipline of saying "not yet" out loud.

On Sunday, Iran's Revolutionary Guard Corps declared the Strait of Hormuz closed to shipping. Within hours, US Central Command said the opposite: "The Strait of Hormuz is open to all vessels seeking to lawfully transit the international waterway. Traffic is flowing." Both statements were issued by people who believed, or needed others to believe, that they were describing reality. Neither was a lie in the ordinary sense. They were two accounts of one strait, and — this is the part worth sitting with — there was no third, neutral vantage point issuing a tie-breaking verdict. An independent maritime advisory group split the difference: the southern route was passable, it said, but the threat level was "severe." That is not a resolution. It is a third opinion, arguably the most honest one, precisely because it admits how much it doesn't know.1

Akira Kurosawa built a film around exactly this structure in 1950. In Rashomon, four witnesses to a single killing in a bamboo grove — bandit, wife, dead man speaking through a medium, a woodcutter who watched — each give a complete, internally consistent, mutually irreconcilable account. Kurosawa's trick isn't that one witness is lying and the audience must find the liar. It's that all four believe themselves. The film doesn't resolve into a verdict; it resolves into a question about what "resolve" could even mean when every account serves its teller. Seventy-six years later, CENTCOM and the IRGC are giving their own bamboo-grove testimony, and the honest reader's job is not to referee it. It's to notice that a strait's actual status — how many tankers actually cleared it today, at what insurance premium — is a different fact from either capital's declaration, and it hasn't arrived yet.2

Two accounts of the same water, both sincere, both interested — and the only honest position is the third one: not yet known.

This issue is, read end to end, unusually full of the opposite case: questions that spent weeks suspended and then, this week, simply resolved. Argentina and Switzerland went to extra time and produced a checkable score, ending days of "unresolved at press" hedging (p4). Madonna's album beat its own projection by a third, a real number replacing a forecast (p12). A ramen shop this desk called "too new to call" a fortnight ago has now been reviewed by people who actually ate there (p20). None of these needed a referee — only for the event to happen and be observed.

The Sceptic

It would be too neat to say "both sides are equally uncertain" and leave it there — CENTCOM controls the Fifth Fleet and has better instruments than a maritime advisory blog, and that asymmetry matters. The point isn't that all claims are equally unverifiable. It's that a claim issued by an interested party, however well-instrumented, is still a claim — and the discipline is in remembering the difference between a claim and a manifest, not in false balance.

There is a much older institution built entirely around this discipline, and it happens to have started in a coffee house a short walk from a different strait. In the 1680s, ship owners, merchants and financiers gathered at Edward Lloyd's coffee house on Tower Street, London, to trade information about vessels and cargoes — which ships had sailed, which had been sighted, which had gone quiet. Out of that gathering grew Lloyd's of London, the marine insurance market that still bears his name. The underwriters who worked there faced Hormuz's exact epistemic problem every single week, for every single ship: a vessel bound for the Indies might be perfectly fine, delayed by weather, or lost with all hands, and there was frequently no way to know which for months. A policy on a ship "missing" was not settled by anyone's confident declaration. It was settled — a "total loss" formally recognised — only once enough time had passed, or word had genuinely returned, that not-knowing had exhausted itself into knowing.1

That is not a quaint historical footnote; it is a described discipline, and this issue's Macro Desk practises a version of it constantly. A week ago the market's working assumption was that the Fed's next move would be a cut. This week, June's FOMC minutes and a reignited Hormuz risk premium flipped that to roughly 70-per-cent odds of a September hike — a full reversal, not a refinement (p15). Anyone who had "priced in" the cut as settled fact a fortnight ago wasn't wrong to have a view; they were wrong to treat a view as a manifest. Lloyd's underwriters would have recognised the error immediately: you can price a probability, you can even take a strong position on it, but the moment you start speaking of an unconfirmed outcome as though the ship has already come home, you've stopped underwriting and started hoping.

There is a smaller, tidier version of the same discipline running through this issue's ticker tape. SK hynix's American shares traded for two full sessions under a provisional symbol, SKHYV, before switching to the permanent SKHY today — a housekeeping detail, but a real one: an exchange will not certify a listing as settled until the paperwork, not the enthusiasm, is actually in order (p7). Madonna's chart placement works the same way in reverse — Billboard doesn't print a number because a label's press release says the album is a hit; it prints the number after the sales and streams are actually counted, which is precisely why this issue could report 134,000 units as a fact rather than repeat the earlier 101,000 as a rumour dressed up as an estimate (p12). A ticker symbol and a chart certification are both, in their small way, Lloyd's policies: nobody gets to declare the voyage complete except the paperwork that follows the actual voyage.2

The oldest version of this problem may be Croesus and the oracle. Herodotus tells it in his Histories: the Lydian king, weighing war against Persia, sent lavish gifts to Delphi and asked whether he should attack. The oracle answered that if Croesus went to war, he would destroy a great empire. He went to war. The empire he destroyed was his own. The prophecy wasn't false — it was genuinely, technically true — but it was also structured so that a confident man would hear in it exactly the confirmation he wanted. Herodotus's point, twenty-five centuries on, is not that oracles lie. It's that an ambiguous authority, faced with a listener eager to be certain, will always be heard as certainty.1

Every desk in this issue faced some smaller version of that same listener's temptation. It would have been easy to read CENTCOM's "traffic is flowing" as the reassuring half of the story and print it as settled, or to read the IRGC's closure as confirmation that the worst had already arrived — either way, hearing what a reader already expected. It would have been easy, a fortnight ago, to treat "the Fed will cut" as background fact rather than a probability that could flip in nine days. The discipline this week's book kept reaching for — in the Macro Desk's honesty rule, in the Property desk's refusal to guess at two overdue en-bloc results, in the Good Life desk declining to invent a Daytona hammer price — is the discipline Croesus didn't have: ask what the prophecy actually said, not what you needed it to mean.

An oracle that can be heard as anything will be heard as agreement. The fix isn't a better oracle. It's a reader who asks what was actually said.

None of this argues for permanent suspension of judgment — Lloyd's underwriters still wrote policies and took real positions; this magazine still leads with a headline every week, not a shrug. The argument is narrower and, I think, more useful: there is a specific, learnable difference between a fact that has arrived — a score, a chart certification, a train that is now running the new way — and a claim that is still travelling, however forcefully it's delivered. Two capitals can describe one strait in opposite terms and both be, in their own telling, sincere. The ship that actually matters is the one still at sea. Everything in this issue that turned out to be simply, checkably true did so because someone waited for it to arrive rather than declaring it home early. That's not a small discipline. Some weeks, it's the only one that counts.

A last, practical note for a reader rather than an underwriter: none of this is a call to distrust everything until it's triple-confirmed. It's a call to notice the specific moment a sentence changes register — from "here is what happened" to "here is what I need you to believe happened" — and to hold the second kind a little more loosely than the first, right up until the ship, or the chart, or the tender result, actually comes in.
