The Hormuz war · the delta

Described in every capital. This weekend, perhaps, signed.

For sixty issues' worth of weeks the US–Iran framework has been a thing everyone talks about and no one inks. On Friday the oil market began, cautiously, to believe.

The pattern of the past fortnight was violence then retreat — the 7–8 June direct Israel–Iran exchange over Lebanon, fresh US and Iranian strikes mid-week, Tehran firing at Gulf bases. Then, on Thursday, President Trump suspended a planned attack and said a deal "including the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz" could be signed "as early as this weekend, likely in Europe."1 By Friday the market had moved: Brent fell more than 4% to below US$86.50, its lowest since early March, as Iran's Mehr news agency reported a fourteen-point draft that would lift oil sanctions and commit Tehran to reopen the strait within thirty days.2

The caution is the story. The draft still needs Iranian sign-off; tanker traffic, though rising, remains a fraction of 2025's twenty million barrels a day; and even a signed page leaves mines to clear, idled fields to restart and damaged terminals to repair. A peace announced is not a peace in effect — a distinction this desk has made, in one form or another, since the strait first closed in February. What is new is that, for the first time, the gap between the words and the world looks as if it might actually close.

A peace announced is not a peace in effect. What is new is that the gap looks, this weekend, like it might close.

The read-through for a Singapore reader is mostly arithmetic. Every dollar off Brent is a dollar off the imported gas that makes 95% of the island's electricity, and a softer line under the July tariff (see Singapore, p.5). For a traveller, it is a steadier yen and a calmer long-haul map. For an investor, it is the removal of a risk premium that has sat on everything from freight to airline fuel hedges since late winter.

The framing is momentum: a deal is "close," reachable this weekend in Europe, contingent on Hormuz reopening "in both directions without tolls." The suspended strike is offered as leverage rewarded, not pressure abandoned — Mr Trump still threatens Kharg Island "in the not-too-distant future" should the page go unsigned.

The slow news of a summer Saturday

What else moved while the strait held its breath

Colombia votes a week from Sunday. The 21 June run-off pits the right's Abelardo de la Espriella (43.7% in the first round) against the left's Iván Cepeda (40.9%) — a contest being read across Latin America as a referendum on the post-Petro direction, with Valencia's endorsement now in play.1

Ukraine keeps hitting the refineries. Long-range drones again struck Russian oil infrastructure this week, with Volgograd reported offline — the slow, deliberate campaign against the revenue base that has quietly done more to the war's economics than any front line.2

The bill, globally. The World Bank cut its 2026 global-growth forecast to 2.5% — the weakest since the pandemic, from 2.9% — citing the energy shock and trade friction, with a downside scenario of 1.3%.3 The US Federal Reserve holds its first meeting under new chair Kevin Warsh next week; an early hint of where the rate path goes now that the oil scare may be receding.

Reuters' Friday wrap caught the mood in one line: equities up, oil down, "Trump says deal is close, but Tehran denies anything is approved." The market is buying the relief and pricing the doubt at the same time — which is roughly the correct posture for a draft no one has signed.

Cost of living · the weekend read

The oil retreat reaches the breakfast table

If the draft in Europe holds, the household squeeze that has defined the year may, quietly, have peaked. A Saturday is a good morning to do the small sums.

Nothing about a Singapore household budget is decided in the Strait of Hormuz, and yet almost everything passes through it. Ninety-five per cent of the island's electricity is made from imported natural gas, and the second-quarter tariff of 29.72 cents a kilowatt-hour was set on January-to-mid-March prices — before the war's worst.1 The Energy Market Authority has flagged a sharper third-quarter hike. But Friday's slide in Brent is the first genuinely hopeful input that picture has had in months: if the peace draft is signed and the strait reopens, the feared July spike may arrive softer than the spring math implied.

That is the right register for a weekend — not a briefing, but a kitchen-table calculation. The relief calendar is already pre-positioned: the S$500 CDC vouchers were pulled forward into June, with U-Save rebates due in July, GST Vouchers in August and the larger Cost-of-Living payout in September.2 The government's framing has been that this is the worst energy shock since the 1973 embargo; the counter-framing, available since Friday, is that the embargo eventually ended too.

The forum mood is cautiously practical: people comparing fixed-price retailer contracts against the regulated tariff before the Q3 revision lands, and a recurring reminder that an aircon set to 25°C rather than 18°C does more for the bill than any rebate. Sensible, unglamorous, correct.

The owner's weekend question

The shrinking gap between the suburbs and the centre

Land scarcity and a 40%-a-decade rise in build costs are pushing suburban launch prices toward levels once reserved for the core. For a multiple-property owner, that is the question worth a coffee.

The number that should make an owner sit up is not a rate — this desk retired the rates story by editorial instruction — but a convergence. New launches in the Outside Central Region are now routinely guided toward and past S$2,000 a square foot, with prime-district projects pushing beyond S$2.5 million a unit, narrowing the historic premium the core commanded over the suburbs.1 When a Bukit or Bayshore launch prices within hailing distance of a fringe-central one, the old mental map — central equals expensive, suburban equals value — stops being a reliable guide to where the value actually is.

Two fresh pegs frame it. At Turf City, Dunearn House — the first private development to break ground in a district the Draft Master Plan 2025 has earmarked for 15,000–20,000 homes — drew nine bids, the strongest developer interest in a Core Central plot since 2018, before going to a Frasers / Sekisui House / CSC consortium at S$1,410 psf ppr.2 Out east, Vela Bay opens the new Bayshore precinct at the doorstep of its MRT station. Both are benchmark-setters; both say the same thing — the developers are paying up for land, and that floor flows straight into launch prices.

The home lab · as a Saturday afternoon

Give the house a brain you actually own

A weekend ago Apple announced it would rent Siri's intelligence from Google. This weekend, on the hardware already on your desk, is a good time to build the opposite: a private model that never leaves the building.

The WWDC keynote eight days ago drew the line cleanly. Apple's rebuilt Siri will run a custom, roughly 1.2-trillion-parameter Google Gemini model on Google's cloud, for a reported billion dollars a year — "rent the model, own the routing," as we put it in No. 17.1 It is a defensible trade for a company shipping to a billion phones. It is also the precise inverse of what a home lab is for. The interesting weekend project is not waiting for the autumn betas; it is wiring a model you control into the house you live in, so that the lights, the scenes and the questions never touch someone else's server.

The good news, this June, is that the bar has dropped. You no longer need a frontier rig to do something genuinely useful. A capable small model — a Phi-class instruct model, a Gemma 3 27B, a quantised Qwen — runs on a 16–24GB consumer GPU or any unified-memory Apple Silicon machine, fast enough to act as a local voice assistant.2 Point Home Assistant's conversation agent at it through Ollama or a local OpenAI-compatible endpoint, and the brain of the home becomes a box on your own shelf.

Why bother, when the cloud model is cleverer? Because the home lab was never about winning a benchmark. It is about a brain that answers in the dark, keeps your routines off someone else's server, and costs nothing per query once the box is paid for. The frontier rents by the token; the shelf is a one-time purchase — and the gap in everyday usefulness, for a house, is smaller than the leaderboard suggests.

The small-model renaissance

The interesting frontier got smaller

The headline race — Kimi K2.6, DeepSeek V4-Pro, GLM-5.1, Qwen 3.6 — is unchanged since No. 14, and stays on the shelf below. The story that moved this spring is at the other end: models small enough to run on a laptop that are finally good enough to trust. Microsoft's Phi-4-mini packs a 128K context into 3.8 billion parameters and needs no GPU; Gemma 3 27B and Qwen variants give a single consumer card frontier-adjacent reasoning.1 For the self-hoster, that is the whole game: the question is no longer "can I run something useful at home" but "which small one fits my box."

The sceptic's box

The binding constraint hasn't moved: it's memory, not compute. DDR5 has roughly quadrupled off its 2025 trough, and the rally is projected to run past 2028 — so the RAM-heavy upgrade you're eyeing for a bigger local model is the one purchase to time, not rush (No. 16–17).

— the standing memory-tax caveat

The practical upshot for a weekend tinkerer: don't chase the biggest number. A 27-billion-parameter model that fits your card and answers in a second beats a 200-billion one that swaps to disk and answers in a minute. Fit, latency and licence decide a home build — not the top of the chart.

Travel gear · the grab-and-go

Canon brings a steady hand to the pocket

For years DJI owned the pocket-gimbal market by default. Canon has now confirmed a compact three-axis challenger — and for the traveller who already carries a "serious" camera, it's the right kind of second body.

DJI built the pocket gimbal-camera category almost single-handed; the Osmo Pocket became the default recommendation for anyone who wanted stabilised footage without strapping a rig to their chest. Canon's newly confirmed entry — a compact three-axis unit with a fixed lens and an auto-folding mechanism that keeps it jacket-pocket small — is the first credible answer in years.1 The pitch is simple: Canon's optical pedigree the moment it ships, in a body that handles the walking and handheld wobble that makes phone footage feel amateur.

For this reader, the appeal isn't replacing the Leica or the Sony. It is the companion piece. On a soft-yen wander through Tokyo (see The Good Life, p.11) or a school-holiday day out, the big camera stays for the frames that matter and the gimbal catches the moving ones — a child running ahead, a market street, a train pulling in — without a gimbal-rig's bulk. The category has matured to the point where "stabilised, pocketable, just works" is finally true.

The weekend house

A house that knows it's Saturday

The smart home earns its keep not on the spectacular automation but on the quiet one — the morning that unfolds without a single tap. This weekend, the case for keeping that brain local has rarely been stronger.

There is a particular pleasure in a house that handles the morning by itself: blinds easing open with the sun, the kettle's circuit live, a single "good morning" that nudges the lights warm and reads back the day — no commute alarm, because it knows it's the weekend. None of that requires the cloud. With Home Assistant's local-first stack — the 2026.6 release shipped on 3 June, with two-way infrared and local Z-Wave lock PINs — the scene lives entirely on a box in the study, paired to the local model from p.7.1 The reward is a home that keeps working when the internet doesn't, and keeps its mornings to itself.

The hardware tailwind is real. Thread-native, standards-first kit is getting cheap enough that local control is becoming the default rather than the enthusiast's project — which is exactly the migrator's dream: keep the brain local, treat any voice surface as a dumb terminal. The connoisseur's move remains the same as it has been all year: own the logic, rent nothing you don't have to.

The migrator's discipline this weekend is small and concrete: pick one scene, build it locally, and prove it survives the router being unplugged. A home that needs someone else's uptime to switch on a lamp is not a smart home; it's a rented one — and the rent comes due the day the manufacturer sunsets the app.

Travel · the window

The soft-yen window is still open. Watch the door.

At ¥160 to the dollar, Japan remains the great bargain of the connoisseur's year. But the Bank of Japan meets next week, and the peace whiplash is already tugging at the rate — go on the delta, not the dread.

The yen sat near ¥160.2 to the dollar on Friday, a hair off its weakest since mid-2024, having briefly strengthened on Thursday when the dollar slid on Mr Trump's "deal this weekend" remark.1 For the traveller, that is the headline: a Tokyo that is still, in hard-currency terms, on sale. A kaiseki dinner, a night at a ryokan, a vintage-watch crawl through Nakano — all of it costs a Singapore wallet roughly a third less than it did three years ago. The connoisseur's instinct this June should be to book the trip, not model the bottom.

The wrinkle worth knowing: the BOJ meets next week and is widely expected to raise rates, with Japan's producer prices up 6.1% in May on energy — the fastest in three years.2 A hawkish surprise, or a signed Hormuz peace that lifts the dollar's risk premium, could nudge the yen off its lows. None of that closes the window this summer; it simply means the cheapest yen of the cycle may already be behind us. The move is the one this desk keeps recommending: act on the measured delta, not the imagined tail.

What does the discount actually buy? A ryokan night that was a splurge three years ago is now merely a treat; a Ginza boutique's grey-market gap narrows; a kaiseki counter that priced out the casual visitor is back in reach. The connoisseur's move is to spend the saving on the experiences that don't travel — the meal, the room, the maker's workshop — rather than the goods you could buy at home anyway.

Music · the arrival

Rodrigo's third arrives — and it's a grown-up heartbreak

After four years, You Seem Pretty Sad for a Girl So in Love landed on Friday. The verdict, unusually unanimous for a Friday: she's levelled up.

Olivia Rodrigo's third album — thirteen tracks, again produced by Dan Nigro, out Friday on Geffen — broke from the punchy one-word titles of Sour and Guts, and the music broke too.1 The early reviews are warm and broadly aligned: Rolling Stone reads the opener "Drop Dead" as a "pure dopamine rush," all the giddiness of a new love willing to risk everything; Stereogum singles out the penultimate "expectations" as the album's banger, an icy, new-wave kiss-off that shows a register listeners hadn't heard from her before.2 The through-line critics keep naming is maturity — sharper storytelling, more visceral feeling, the title itself turning every platitude about being "in love" on its head. Robert Smith of the Cure features on "The Cure," a wink only Rodrigo could land.

The Chatter · the fans

Listener boards split the usual way — a vocal camp still rates Guts the peak, while converts call this "an instant summer 2026 classic." The recurring praise: the way she "paints entire scenes with words." A career commercial run that, on this evidence, isn't slowing.

— aggregated, Album of the Year boards

The wider pattern this desk keeps flagging: the album, like the film, increasingly arrives all at once and everywhere — the slow build of a single replaced by a Friday drop engineered to own a weekend. "Drop Dead" and "The Cure" trailed the record by weeks for exactly that reason; the arrival is now a campaign, not a moment. Which makes the unhurried act of simply listening, start to finish, the quietly radical one.

The June holidays · the cheapest line item

The case for sending them outside

In a city where roughly four in five young adults are short-sighted, the single best thing a Singapore parent can do these school holidays costs nothing, needs no screen, and has the evidence squarely behind it: two hours a day outdoors.

Singapore has one of the highest childhood-myopia rates on earth — by adulthood, close to 80% are short-sighted — and the science on what slows it is, for once, refreshingly unambiguous.1 It isn't a gadget or a drop; it's daylight. Time spent outdoors is the safest, best-evidenced intervention going. A landmark Guangzhou trial added forty minutes of outdoor school time a day and cut new myopia cases by roughly a quarter; Taiwan's island-wide "Daily 120" pushed two hours a day and saw rates turn.2 One large study estimated that lifting a child from one to three hours outdoors daily could halve their risk. The mechanism is bright natural light regulating eye growth — and indoor light, even a well-lit room, is a fraction as strong.

For an eleven-year-old mid-June-holiday, the prescription writes itself: aim for two hours a day outside, however you get there — a cycle on the park connectors, a swim, a long museum-to-hawker amble, a badminton court. It is not screen-time policing; it's screen-time displacement by something better. And the bonus dividends — sleep, mood, movement — arrive whether or not the eyes ever needed them.

There's a second, softer argument for the outdoor afternoon: it manufactures the boredom that self-directed play needs. A child handed ten unstructured weeks and no screen will, after the initial protest, invent — and invention is where resilience, curiosity and attention are built. The parent's job over the June holidays isn't to fill every hour; it's to protect the empty ones, ideally in daylight, and then get out of the way.

The sky · two faces of a day

Two completely different twilights on one alien world

On a planet 880 light-years off, dawn and dusk are not the same place. The James Webb telescope has just read the difference — a fitting find for an issue about how a single day is spent.

WASP-121 b is an ultra-hot gas giant so close to its star that one side bakes in permanent day and the other freezes in permanent night. The interesting real estate is the seam between them — the terminators, the planet's dawn and dusk. Using starlight filtered through the atmosphere as the planet crossed its star, a Max Planck-led team found the clearest evidence yet that the morning and evening edges have genuinely different temperatures and chemistry, confirming a pattern that had lived only in models.1 A world, in other words, where the difference between dawn and dusk is not a mood but a measurable fact.

And, closer to home: scientists re-examining amber from Goethe's own collection — yes, the poet's — used advanced 3-D imaging to find three fossil insects hidden inside, including an extinct ant preserved in exquisite detail.2 A two-century-old object, re-read, gives up a secret it kept the whole time. The week's quieter Curiosity, meanwhile, is a "cold blob" in the North Atlantic that stubbornly cools while the planet warms — a reminder that even the obvious story has an asterisk.

The amber is the better parable for this issue. A specimen sat in a poet's drawer for two centuries, its hidden ants invisible to everyone who handled it — until someone pointed a new instrument at the old object and asked again. Nothing about the amber changed; the attention did. Which is, more or less, the whole argument of the weekend: the ordinary thing in your hand rewards a second, better look.

The examined evening

What the happiest couples do instead of the screen

The default modern evening is dinner, dishes, then collapse onto the couch for the television. There's nothing wrong with it — but a psychologist who studies couples says the happiest ones spend at least some evenings differently.

Mark Travers, a relationships psychologist, put it plainly in a CNBC piece this week: the happiest couples use their evenings in ways that "invite a little bit of novelty" rather than running the same autopilot — home, logistics, dinner, screen, sleep.1 Not every night; comfort shows are comforting for a reason. But the resilient couples, he argues, deliberately protect one shared, undistracted moment — a walk, a quick game, a few honest minutes of "how are you, really" — and treat it as something to defend rather than something that happens if there's time left over.

It pairs with a smaller, sturdier finding: couples who simply go to bed at the same time tend to report feeling closer — and sleep better for it.2 None of it is grand. It is the relationship equivalent of the issue's larger argument — that the ordinary evening, spent with a little attention, is not the leftover of a life but a fair share of it.

Why does the evening carry so much weight? Because it's the one stretch of the day most couples actually share, and the one most easily surrendered to the path of least resistance. Travers' list isn't romantic in the grand sense — a walk, a question, a synced bedtime — but it's where a relationship is quietly maintained or quietly neglected, one ordinary night at a time.

An idea to try

You already live in a sauna. Now use it on purpose.

For a Singapore reader, heat isn't a training tool you have to find — it's the ambient condition. The science on deliberate post-exercise heat is intriguing, if not airtight, and worth a weekend experiment.

Heat training has quietly become one of the more interesting corners of endurance science. The logic: a body that adapts to heat builds more blood-plasma volume and better thermoregulation, and several small studies suggest measurable gains. In one, three weeks of post-exercise sauna sessions — around thirty minutes, a few times a week — improved runners' VO₂-adjacent markers and their speed at lactate threshold versus a control group.1 A systematic review found post-exercise hot-water immersion improved time-trial performance specifically in hot conditions. For a mid-40s reader chasing the long game, it's a low-cost lever: the engine, trained in the heat.

The Singapore twist is that you don't need a Finnish cabin. The island is the heat chamber — which makes a deliberate Zone-2 session outdoors, or a sauna after the gym, an experiment that costs nothing but discipline. Hydrate hard, start short, and treat it as an addition to the basics, never a replacement.

A sane way to test it: after a couple of weeks of consistent training, add a short post-session sauna — start at ten minutes, build toward twenty to thirty, two or three times a week — and hydrate as if it were a workout, because metabolically it is. Or simply run your easy Zone-2 outdoors at the hottest hour rather than the coolest. Track how a familiar pace feels after a month; if nothing changes, you've lost nothing but sweat.

The sceptic's box

Hold the enthusiasm at room temperature. Heat is a garnish on a meal of resistance work and Zone 2 — not the meal. And if you're unwell or pregnant, skip it.

The Ordinary Saturday

A week that kept promising arrivals — a peace, an album, a verdict, a wedding — is a useful week to remember that a life is not made of arrivals. It is made of Saturdays. On the gap between the announced and the lived, and why the gap is where we actually live.

Consider the kind of week this was. In Europe, a peace described in every capital for four months looked, on Friday, as though it might finally be signed — and the oil price fell on the hope of it. A pop record four years awaited arrived at midnight. A literary prize, long handicapped for one favourite, went instead to a debut about a woman writing letters. And an entire internet held its breath for a celebrity wedding it had decided would happen today, on the strength of a date no one with standing ever confirmed. Arrivals, arrivals, arrivals — each one trailed for weeks by anticipation, each one finally cashing out (or not) on a single day. It is enough to make you believe that life is the sum of its announced events, that the meaningful part is the arrival and everything before it merely the waiting room. Annie Dillard, whose line opens this issue, spent a career arguing the opposite. How we spend our days, she wrote, is, of course, how we spend our lives.1 Not how we spend our arrivals. Our days. The treaty that gets signed, the album that drops, the wedding that finally happens — these are punctuation. The sentence is the ordinary Saturday, and most of us read straight past it, waiting for the next full stop.

The treaty, the album, the wedding — these are punctuation. The sentence is the ordinary Saturday.

The Romans had a word for the posture the week demands, and it was not the bumper-sticker one. Carpe diem, from Horace's eleventh ode, is usually mistranslated as a licence to indulge — seize the day, party like it ends. Read the whole line and it is sterner and sadder: carpe diem, quam minimum credula postero — pluck the day, trusting as little as possible in the next.2 Horace wrote it in 23 BC to a woman consulting astrologers about her future, and his point was precisely the one the oil market made on Friday: do not stake your living on a tomorrow that has not arrived and may not. The Iranian draft is unsigned. The wedding date was a guess. The future you are waiting to live in is a forecast, and forecasts are the one commodity the desks in this issue agree never to fully trust. What you have, indisputably, is the day in your hands — which Horace, gardener's verb and all, tells you to pluck, the way you would a ripe thing that will not keep.

If the day is the unit, the weekend is the deliberate frame we built around it — and it is worth remembering that the frame is an invention, not a law of nature. For most of human history there was no protected, recurring stretch of non-productive time; the Jewish Sabbath, that radical insistence on one day in seven set aside, was among the first technologies for carving the ordinary day out of the relentless economy of work. The two-day weekend is younger still. It is barely a century old, and it was, of all people, Henry Ford who made it standard: in 1926 the Ford Motor Company moved its factories to a five-day, forty-hour week, partly out of welfare and partly out of the dawning industrialist's realisation that workers who had time off would buy the cars they built.1 The Saturday you are reading this on was, in other words, manufactured — argued for, legislated, fought over — precisely because someone understood that a life is not only the work that gets announced and shipped. It is the time around it.

And what do you do with carved-out time? One nineteenth-century answer became an art form. The flâneur — Baudelaire's idler, later Walter Benjamin's emblem of modern attention — was a person who walked the city with no destination and full awareness, treating the ordinary street as worth seeing.2 It sounds like doing nothing. It is, in fact, the discipline this whole issue keeps circling: attention as the thing that converts a passed day into a lived one. The Good Life desk's soft-yen Tokyo wander, the Kit's pocket camera for the moving moment, the Family desk's two unhurried hours of daylight, Love & Life's evening walk with the phone left behind — these are flânerie by other names. The instruction underneath all of them is identical: be where you are, on purpose.

The arrivals are few and loud; the days are many and quiet — and the days are the life. Meridian original.

There is a Japanese phrase the soft-yen traveller this week might carry home along with the watches and the matsuri photographs: ichigo ichie, 一期一会, "one time, one meeting." It comes from the tea ceremony — codified in the lineage of Sen no Rikyū — and it means that this gathering, this exact cup, these specific people in this light, will never recur, and so deserve to be met as the unrepeatable thing they are.1 It is the opposite of the arrivals mindset. The arrivals mindset treats the present as a corridor to somewhere better — once the treaty is signed, once the album is heard, once the wedding happens, then life resumes. Ichigo ichie says: there is no corridor. This ordinary Saturday, with its small disappointments and its unsigned futures, is not the waiting room for your life. It is the appointment. You will not get this one back, and you do not need to, because you are in it now.

There is no corridor. This ordinary Saturday is not the waiting room for your life. It is the appointment.

Thread the issue back through that idea and it coheres. Seneca warned us last week (No. 20) that we suffer more in imagination than in reality — that the dust cloud is rarely the army; the corollary is that the imagined future steals attention the present has a better claim on. Browning, in No. 15, gave us the reach that should exceed the grasp; the answer to a reach that never closes is not despair but presence — to live well in the reaching. And Thoreau, away back in No. 10, defined the cost of a thing as "the amount of life which is required to be exchanged for it." A day spent entirely in anticipation of an arrival that may not come is a day exchanged for a forecast. The desks this week quietly argue for a better trade: own the model rather than rent it, choose the watch for the maker's hand rather than the queue, send the child into the daylight, take the evening walk, book the Kyoto weekend while the yen is kind. None of these are arrivals. All of them are days, spent on purpose.

So let the wire do what it does. The strait may open this weekend or it may not; the BOJ will move or hold; the wedding will happen on some date that was never today. You, meanwhile, have a Saturday in your hands — ripe, and disinclined to keep. Dillard's whole argument fits in the gap between two words: not how we spend our arrivals, but how we spend our days. Pluck this one. It is, after all, the only part of the future that has actually arrived.

The Last Word

On not waiting

A confession to close a weekend book: we spent this whole issue watching for arrivals — refreshing the oil screen for a signed peace, the charts for an album's verdict, a venue's statement for a wedding that turned out to be a rumour about today. It is a strange way to read a Saturday. Annie Dillard's line is the cure, and it is almost rude in its simplicity: the days are the life. There is no version you are waiting for. The strait will open or it won't; the yen will hold or it won't; the future will, as ever, arrive as a series of perfectly ordinary mornings. Horace told a woman fretting over her horoscope to pluck the day and trust the next as little as possible. Two thousand years on, with better forecasts and no more certainty, it remains the only sound advice anyone has ever given about a weekend. So close the magazine. Go outside.
