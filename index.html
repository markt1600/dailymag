The signed world

It has a date now

For twenty-two editions the peace was described in every capital and signed in none. On Sunday a mediator in Islamabad used the word "reached" — and a Friday in Switzerland acquired a meaning.

Pakistan's prime minister, Shehbaz Sharif, did not hedge. "The Peace Deal between the United States of America and the Islamic Republic of Iran has been reached," he posted on Sunday, declaring "the immediate and permanent termination of military operations on all fronts, including in Lebanon."1 Within the hour Donald Trump answered on his own platform: "The Deal with the Islamic Republic of Iran is now complete." He authorised the toll-free reopening of the Strait of Hormuz and the removal of the US naval blockade with four words that will outlive the prose around them — "Let the oil flow!"2

The mechanics are deliberately staged. Each side signs electronically; a ceremony follows on Friday, 19 June, in Switzerland, with "pre-implementation" meetings filling the week between.3 The document itself — the Islamabad Memorandum of Understanding, fourteen points and, by the Iranian foreign minister's own account, fewer than two pages after two months of drafting — defers the hardest matter. Terms governing Iran's nuclear programme are to be finalised in the sixty days after the initial signing, a window the parties may extend.4

What is not deferred is the strait. The blockade lifts; Hormuz reopens; frozen Iranian assets are to be released and sanctions phased down; a reconstruction plan is written into the text. Oil read the news the only way it knows how. Brent, which had touched its lowest since early March on Friday, settled near US$86 — still some 23 per cent above the roughly US$70 it fetched the day before the war began in late February, but a world away from the US$110 peaks of the spring.5

And here the briefing must be honest, because the market is. A reopened strait is not a flowing one. Mines must be cleared, idled Gulf fields restarted, drone-scarred terminals repaired; Morgan Stanley warned weeks ago that supply chains take months to normalise even after a reopening is achieved.6 The signature is Friday's. The tankers are a question for the summer.

TehranIRNA / Fars

The win is framed as relief delivered: blockade lifted, frozen assets returned, a reconstruction clause for war damage. Araghchi's caution — that the document's contents be left to official release, not leaked drafts — doubles as a reminder that the sixty-day nuclear clock starts, not stops, at the signing. The negotiator's old line still hangs over it: the winner is whoever is better prepared the day after.

WashingtonWhite House / CNBC

"Complete," says the President; an official puts the odds of an actual signature in the coming days at eighty per cent — "not 100." The deal, as Washington describes it, reopens Hormuz, dismantles enrichment and removes uranium, with financial incentives for compliance. The leaked Tehran version reads more favourably to Iran; the gap between the two is the week's real work.

"Let the oil flow!" — four words that reopened a strait, and started a clock.

Grossi's people know the lesson of every arms deal since 1987: the signature is cheap, the inspections expensive, and trust is only as good as the access that backs it.

Still on the wire

The scar the peace inherits. Even as the guns quiet, the bill arrives. The World Bank this month cut its 2026 global growth forecast to 2.5 per cent — down from 2.9, the weakest since the pandemic — citing the energy shock and trade disruption the war leaves behind.7 A peace signed on Friday does not un-write a half-year of US$100 oil; it only stops adding to the total.

Colombia decides. The presidential run-off lands on 21 June, the hard-right Abelardo de la Espriella (43.7 per cent in the first round) against the leftist Iván Cepeda (40.9), with Sergio Fajardo's centrist endorsement the swing weight.8 A continent that watched Washington spend the spring on the Gulf gets its own verdict on the populist tide.

Kyiv keeps the pressure on. Ukraine's long-range drones again reached deep into Russian refining — Volgograd's plant offline once more — a reminder that the year's other war has no Islamabad waiting in the wings, only the slow attrition of the things that turn crude into power.9

For the reader who has followed the oil line down this column for four months: the war premium is unwinding, but the structural costs it exposed — strained grids, re-priced shipping, a memory of how thin the buffers ran — do not unwind with it. That thread runs through Technology, Property and the Long Read.

The little red dot

Relief built for a war that may be ending

The cheques were calibrated for a conflict at full roar. The peace, if it holds, will arrive faster than the meter — and the July bill will still sting.

Singapore runs on imported gas — about 95 per cent of the island's power — so every dollar of Brent eventually shows up on a utility bill, with a lag.1 That lag is the whole story this quarter. The current household tariff of 29.72 cents per kilowatt-hour was set on natural-gas prices from January to mid-March, before the worst of the spike; the Energy Market Authority warned of a "sharper" hike from July, when the bill finally catches up to the war.2

Now the war may be ending — and the meter does not care. The July tariff reflects gas bought through a window that includes the spring's US$100-plus oil; the relief of Sunday's deal will take months to reach the kettle. So the government's pre-positioned calendar of cash matters more, not less. The S$500 Community Development Council vouchers were pulled forward to June; a second, enlarged tranche of U-Save rebates — up to S$570 for eligible HDB households — lands in July precisely to blunt that sharper bill; GST Vouchers follow in August and a Cost-of-Living top-up in September.3

The read-through for a comfortable household is undramatic and worth saying plainly: the squeeze peaks roughly now and eases through the autumn, assuming the strait stays open. The cheques are a bridge across the lag, not a windfall. The first quarter's growth held at 6 per cent on AI-driven demand and April inflation sat at 1.8 per cent — a economy absorbing the shock rather than buckling under it.4

Bricks & mortar

Fewer launches, firmer floor

The thinnest new-launch pipeline in years is meeting a land-and-build cost that refuses to fall. For an owner, scarcity and a hard floor point the same way.

The paradox of 2026's private market is that it is simultaneously cooling and underpinned. Just 17 new-launch projects are expected this year — about 8,100 units, a 30 per cent fall on 2025's bumper run — with the first half yielding only some 4,575 of them.1 Yet prices have not cracked: the first quarter's private index rose 0.9 per cent (final), the slowest pace in eight quarters, with December's ABSD tweak cooling demand at the margins rather than reversing it.2

What holds the floor is cost, not froth. Government Land Sale tenders have been clearing around S$1,463 per square foot per plot ratio, and construction costs have climbed roughly 40 per cent over the decade — so developers simply lack the room to discount. Break-evens on new suburban land increasingly start north of S$2,400 psf, which is why the price gap between the city fringe and the suburbs keeps narrowing rather than widening.3

Does the peace help? At the margin. A strait reopening eases the energy line in shipping steel, cement and finishes, and an oil retreat takes some heat out of input inflation. But land and labour — the two biggest stones in the wall — are set in Singapore, not in Hormuz. The supply tap, meanwhile, stays open by policy: the second-half GLS list carries roughly 4,745 private units across nine sites, lifting the pipeline toward 61,000.4

The owner's read

A thin launch calendar plus a hard cost floor is a quietly supportive mix for existing stock: less new competition, little room for developers to undercut. The risk isn't a price crash; it's liquidity — fewer transactions, longer holds.

Silicon & sovereignty

The peace won't pay the power bill

The war premium on oil is unwinding. The structural squeeze the war exposed — that compute is now an energy problem — is not going anywhere.

It is tempting, on the morning of a peace, to file the energy story under "resolved." Don't. The oil shock was a spike; the thing it lit up is a trend. America's data-centre demand is modelled to climb from roughly 80 gigawatts toward 150 by 2028, and the Department of Energy unveiled its "Agora" grid-simulation tool this month precisely because more than 300 state bills are now wrestling with who pays for it.1 Carnegie Mellon researchers put the resulting household electricity rise at around 8 per cent by 2030 — and far higher in data-centre states such as Virginia.2

A reopened Hormuz lowers the price of a barrel. It does nothing for the binding constraint that the spring made legible: a frontier model's cost is increasingly measured in watts, not just FLOPs. That is the reframing this desk has tracked since the spike — and the peace, far from dissolving it, strips away the distraction. With the war premium gone, the grid is the story that's left.

Nvidia, reading the same map, keeps moving down the stack. Its Vera CPU is now in full production with OpenAI, Anthropic and SpaceX among early adopters, while the RTX Spark superchip (flagged in No. 18) pushes frontier-class inference to the laptop and the desk.3 Around it, Amazon, Google and Microsoft press their own silicon — a hedge against both supply and the power bill. Sovereignty, this year, is spelled in megawatts.

The home lab

Tokens per watt — the metric that survives the peace

For the self-hoster, the war only confirmed what the electricity bill was already saying: the constraint moved from the GPU to the wall and the RAM.

If the macro lesson of the spring is that compute is an energy problem, the home-lab version is smaller and more useful: judge a rig not by tokens per second but by tokens per watt. A desk-class box drawing roughly 250 watts that runs a 120-billion-parameter model overnight is, in 2026, a more sovereign machine than a cloud subscription that quietly meters your every prompt — and it does not care whether Hormuz is open.

The tax that does still bite is memory. DDR5 remains some four times off its 2025 low, with the squeeze projected to run past 2028 as high-bandwidth memory eats wafer capacity. The peace lowers the diesel that you don't burn; it does nothing for the RAM invoice that you do pay. Buy the box on merit now; do not model a memory dip that the supply curve says isn't coming.

Gear we're watching

Who owns the band on your wrist

A US court just blocked a cheaper Whoop rival from sale. For the reader wearing an Oura and a Whoop, it's a useful reminder of what a recovery score actually costs.

A Massachusetts court has granted Whoop a preliminary injunction blocking US sales of Lexqi's rival band while the underlying case proceeds — a quietly significant move in a category the reader of this desk lives inside.1 Whoop's model was never the strap; it was the subscription. The injunction protects that moat, and in doing so spells out the bargain: when you buy into a recovery score, you are renting a relationship, not owning a device.

It is worth holding that thought against the alternative philosophies on your other wrist. Garmin and Apple sell you a watch and let you keep it; Oura and Whoop sell you a membership and the data that comes with it. Neither is wrong, but they age differently — one depreciates, the other renews — and a court fight over a cheaper clone is a reminder that the membership model defends itself in ways a one-time purchase never has to. Meta, for what it's worth, is reported to be readying its first smartwatch this year, with a Meta-AI assistant built in — another walled garden, not a way out of them.2

The buying climate, with the war premium fading, is straightforward: the things with little memory exposure — wearables, headphones, cameras, watches — can be bought on merit now. The RAM-heavy gear (laptops, handhelds, NAS) is where patience still pays, for reasons the Home Lab page sets out. A cheaper oil barrel does not lower a DDR5 invoice.

The contrarian

"Own vs subscribe" is the real spec war in wearables. A S$0 strap with a S$30/month tail can cost more over three years than a S$700 watch you never pay again. Price the relationship, not the hardware.

Walls that listen

Matter's week in Austin

The Connectivity Standards Alliance holds its first "Unify" gathering this week, with Matter 1.5 cameras finally on shelves and a flood of cheap Thread hardware arriving behind them.

From 16 to 18 June the standards body convenes in Austin for "Unify," its inaugural event — and the timing is real, not ceremonial.1 Matter 1.5 cameras have at last reached shelves, SmartThings first, with Aqara, Eve and others following; the spec finally standardises the buttons that matter — video, motion, person-and-package detection, two-way audio, a privacy shutter.2 What it pointedly does not standardise is the back end: storage, the AI that reads the feed, the subscription. As we put it in No. 18, Matter gives you a universal remote — but not whose box it points at.

Behind the cameras comes the cheap stuff, which is what actually changes a house. IKEA is pushing more than twenty Thread-native products, and when standards-native hardware gets that inexpensive, local control stops being the enthusiast's choice and becomes the default one. For the migrator keeping the brain on Home Assistant, that is the quiet win of the week: more devices that simply join the mesh and answer locally.

And the energy angle the war put on this desk has not expired with the ceasefire. A home that can see its own consumption — Home Assistant's Energy dashboard, Matter energy sensors, local load-shifting — is still the cheapest power upgrade available, because the structural grid squeeze (see Technology) outlives the oil spike. The meter still matters even as the war premium fades.

The migrator's read

Buy the Matter camera for the local stream and the privacy shutter; ignore the bundled cloud tier. Keep recording and recognition on your own box. "Standardised front, proprietary back" is the trap — Unify won't fix it, but cheap Thread gear routes around it.

The map redraws — and Japan waits

A peace reopens airspace and unwinds the fuel surcharges that bent the long-haul map for a season. The obvious place to spend the relief is a yen that has rarely been this soft.

For four months the great-circle routes detoured — around closed Gulf airspace, around the Red Sea, the surcharges climbing with the crude. A signed peace begins to undo that: reroutes shorten, war-risk premiums ease, and the arithmetic of a long flight tilts back toward the traveller. It will not happen overnight — insurers and schedulers move slowly — but the direction has reversed, and the patient planner books into a falling cost rather than a rising one.

Where to point it is barely a question. The yen sits near ¥160 to the dollar, close to a three-decade low, after a year that already drew a record 42.7 million visitors to Japan. The connoisseur's move is the early-July window — after the spring crowds, before the August peak — when Mount Fuji's trails open and the matsuri season begins (see The Diary). A soft currency turns a once-splurge into a merely-expensive one, and an expensive country into a generous one.

It is the unfashionable counsel that travels best: book on the delta, not the dread. The fear that kept the map bent is unwinding; the discount it leaves behind is real for a while, then gone. Go before the surcharges fully reset.

Raising them well

Telling a child a war is ending

She has heard the word "war" in the kitchen for four months. This week the word changed. Helping an eleven-year-old process good news is its own quiet skill.

A study published this spring found that children carry the idea of war more heavily than their parents assume — and differently from earlier generations. Where Cold-War kids feared a named enemy, today's view it as an existential threat to the planet itself, a "fear of planetary destruction" that floats free of any particular border.1 Which means the end of a war is not automatically experienced as relief. A child who has absorbed months of grave grown-up faces may need help understanding that the grave faces can stop.

The most reliable lever is the least technological one. Researchers describe emotional contagion: a parent's own dysregulated nervous system transmits, over time, to the child.2 If the adults model relief — visibly letting the shoulders down, naming the good news as good — that travels too. The clinicians' advice is consistent across the American Academy of Child & Adolescent Psychiatry, UNICEF and hospital guidance: watch the news with your child, ask first what they've already heard, correct gently, and reassure in plain words.3

There is also a gift hidden in this particular week. The deal arrived through duelling accounts — an Iranian outlet's leaked draft, a presidential post insisting those terms were not the ones agreed. For an eleven-year-old, that contradiction is the perfect, low-stakes lesson in the desk's favourite habit: who is saying this, how would they know, and what would change my mind? A war ending is a safer classroom for media literacy than a war beginning.

A practical note the clinicians add: keep the routine. The bedtime, the dinner, the walk to school are not interruptions to the conversation about the news — they are the conversation, the structure that tells a child the world is still steady enough to be ordinary. Crisis or relief, the ordinary evening does the reassuring that no speech can. And let her set the depth; some children want the map and the timeline, others want only to know that the grown-ups are alright. Both are fine. The goal is not to deliver a briefing but to leave the door open for the next question, whenever it comes.

At the dinner table

"What have you heard about the news?" beats "Here's what happened." Let her lead; you'll learn what she's actually worried about, which is rarely what you'd guess.

Then: name the good news as good. Children take their emotional cue from your face before your words.

predictor of a child's screen habits: the parent's own
What not to say

"It's over, don't worry about it" closes the door. "It looks like good news — what made you think about it?" keeps it open. Reassurance that forbids the worry rarely lands; reassurance that acknowledges it does.

The tween years

The evidence keeps pointing back at us

A year of fresh data on tweens and screens lands on one stubborn finding: the strongest predictor of a child's habits is the adult holding the other phone.

If the Family Desk has a single through-line this year, it is that the interventions which work are unglamorous and the ones parents reach for first usually do not. Longitudinal work from the Adolescent Brain Cognitive Development study — the largest of its kind, following thousands of American children into adolescence — keeps surfacing the same uncomfortable result: a parent's own screen behaviour predicts the child's better than rules, contracts or app timers do.1 Modelling beats monitoring. The phone left face-down at dinner teaches more than the lecture delivered over it.

The second reliable finding is about sleep, not screens as such. Researchers tracking bedtime device use find the damage is mostly displacement — minutes that would have been sleep, lost to the scroll — and the fix is boundary, not abstinence: devices out of the bedroom overnight, a charging station in the kitchen, a wind-down that does not glow.2 Pair that with outdoor time, which keeps showing up as protective in ways that have little to do with exercise and much to do with attention recovering when the eyes look far away.

What the better clinicians stress, against the grain of the year's louder headlines, is proportion. The same datasets that link heavy use to lower wellbeing show the effect sizes are modest and the direction of causation genuinely contested — anxious children may seek screens as much as screens make children anxious.3 The advice that survives scrutiny is boring on purpose: watch what they watch, keep talking, protect sleep, model the behaviour you want, and treat the device as a tool with hours rather than a vice to be policed.

For an eleven-year-old specifically — old enough to want autonomy, young enough to still take the cue from the room — the lever is collaboration over surveillance. Co-viewing, shared rules the adults also follow, and a standing invitation to bring the strange thing she saw to the table beat a tracking app she will route around within a fortnight.

The far edges

The universe just got 161 louder

A new gravitational-wave catalogue nearly doubles the tally of black-hole collisions ever recorded — a week for counting things that finally arrive.

The LIGO–Virgo–KAGRA collaboration released its widest haul yet this week: 161 fresh gravitational-wave detections, lifting the running catalogue of confirmed cosmic collisions to roughly 390.1 Each entry is the faint ring of spacetime itself, set quivering when two black holes spiral together and merge — a signal that took a century to predict and another to hear. Doubling the sample is not a vanity number; it is the difference between anecdote and census. With this many events, astronomers can start asking population questions — how heavy these black holes run, how fast they spin, where in cosmic history they pair off — rather than marvelling at one chirp at a time.

The catalogue also sharpens the oddities. Buried in the new data is a candidate that may sit in the so-called mass gap, the awkward weight class between the heaviest neutron stars and the lightest black holes, where theory has long predicted a shortage and nature keeps declining to comply.2 Every such object is a small rebuke to a tidy model — exactly the kind of rebuke that moves a field.

Two more far-edge items worth your attention. A mathematics-heavy paper made the rounds arguing that the accelerating universe might be explained without invoking dark energy at all, if the cosmos is lumpier than the smooth-average models assume — a minority view, but a serious one, and a reminder that the field's biggest term remains its least understood.3 And spectroscopists report a stubborn shortfall of sulphur in certain molecular clouds: the element is there in the rock of planets but missing from the gas it should have come from, a small accounting error with an unsolved story behind it.4

It is, in other words, a fitting week for the desk that counts. The headlines this issue are full of a thing that finally arrived after long delay; out at the far edges, the instruments spent the same week doing what they always do — turning faint, long-awaited signals into a number you can put in a table.

The human desk

A ceasefire is not a peace

The week's diplomacy has a domestic echo. The research on repairing relationships agrees with the diplomats: stopping the fight is the easy part.

There is a useful borrowed vocabulary in the conflict literature: a ceasefire ends the shooting; a peace rebuilds the relationship so the shooting need not resume. Couples therapists describe the same gap. Most reconciliations after a serious rupture are really ceasefires — the argument stops, the silence thaws — and then quietly fail, because nothing was done in the days after to restore the thing that broke. The research on repair is consistent: it is the day-after work, not the apology itself, that predicts whether trust comes back.1

Forgiveness, the studies are careful to note, is not the same as reconciliation. One is an internal release; the other is a rebuilt relationship, and you can do the first without the second. Where forgiveness does improve satisfaction, it appears to work indirectly — through reduced negative conflict and a willingness to put in effort — rather than as a single magnanimous gesture.2 The couples who recover are not the ones who say sorry best; they are the ones who change the pattern that produced the rupture.

A necessary caveat, because the desk does not deal in fairy tales: reconciliation is not always the wise outcome. On-again, off-again couples report more uncertainty and, in some longitudinal work, higher rates of later divorce — a reminder that a relationship can reach a ceasefire repeatedly without ever reaching a peace, and that some of those should be allowed to end.3 The skill is telling the two apart: a repairable rupture from a recurring one.

What does the day-after work actually consist of? The research is less romantic than the word "repair" suggests. It is mostly maintenance: the deliberate, slightly boring re-accumulation of small positive interactions that a rupture spends down. Trust, on this view, is not restored by one cathartic conversation but rebuilt the way it was built in the first place — in ordinary attention, kept promises, the unremarkable evening that passes without incident. The diplomats would recognise the principle exactly. A signed accord that is never funded, never staffed, never felt in daily life reverts to a ceasefire by neglect. So does a marriage. The peace, in either case, is not the moment the fighting stops; it is everything you do afterwards to make sure it stays stopped.

"The apology stops the fight. The week after the apology decides the marriage."
What the work looks like

Naming the pattern, not just the incident. Small, repeated, visible changes over a single grand gesture. Re-establishing routine — the shared dinner, the ordinary evening — as the place trust is rebuilt, not in the big talk but in the days around it.

The sceptic

Not every reconciliation should succeed. Chronic on-again, off-again cycling correlates with worse outcomes; sometimes the healthiest repair is a clean ending, not another ceasefire.

Variety, not just volume

A large new analysis suggests how you spread your exercise across activities may matter as much as how much you do — good news for the mid-40s body that no longer wants to do one thing forever.

The longevity literature has spent years establishing that more movement is better, up to a generous ceiling. A new analysis pushes a subtler claim: at the same total volume, people whose activity is spread across a wider variety of types appear to live longer than those who pour the same minutes into a single mode.1 Drawing on tens of thousands of adults followed for decades, the most-varied movers showed a meaningfully lower risk of death than the least-varied, holding weekly volume constant — with everyday walking emerging as a quietly powerful staple rather than an afterthought.

For a reader in his mid-40s, the practical reading is permission, not prescription. The body that has done the same routine for a decade often resists it; a portfolio — some strength, some brisk walking, a little that raises the heart rate hard, something that asks for balance and mobility — may be both more sustainable and, on this evidence, more protective. The desk's standing caution applies: association is not proof, and people who vary their movement differ in a hundred other ways from those who don't.

It also dovetails with what this year's other findings keep saying. The strength sweet-spot work flagged earlier this year, the short-burst protocols, the steady drumbeat about walking — they are not competing claims so much as instruments in the same portfolio. The longevity question is less "which exercise?" and more "how many different ones, sustained for how many years?"

The Long Read · an essay

Peace Hath Her Victories

A signature was finally added to a thing every capital had described and none had signed. History is blunt about what comes next: the ceremony is the easy part, and the peace is the work that follows it.

In 1652, with the English civil wars behind him and the harder business of governing a republic ahead, John Milton wrote a sonnet warning Oliver Cromwell that the fighting had been the simpler labour. "Peace hath her victories," he wrote, "no less renown'd than war." It is a strange, double-edged line — at once a congratulation and a caution. The victories of peace are real, Milton insists, and they are victories, requiring as much resolve as any battle. But they are won later, quietly, by the unglamorous work of building something that holds. This week, after this magazine spent twenty-two editions describing a Middle-Eastern settlement that was sketched in every capital and signed in none, a signature finally arrived. Milton's line is the right one to read it by.

The temptation, on a week like this, is to treat the signing as the achievement. It is not. The signing is the moment the real question begins: was this peace structured, in its terms and its incentives, to make the next war unprofitable — or merely to stop the present one? History has run this experiment many times, and it has a clear, uncomfortable answer. The character of a peace is decided not at the ceremony but in the years of reconstruction that either bind the former enemies into a shared interest or leave them nursing the grievance that produces the sequel.

Consider the two most famous peaces of the last century, and the gap between them. In June 1919, in the Hall of Mirrors at Versailles, the victors of the First World War signed a settlement designed to punish.1 Within months a young Treasury official who had walked out of the negotiations in disgust published a small, furious book — The Economic Consequences of the Peace — arguing that a settlement built to humiliate and bankrupt a defeated nation was not a peace at all but a deferred war.2 John Maynard Keynes was right in the worst possible way. The punitive peace sowed the next conflict almost on schedule.

Now set beside it the other peace. In June 1947, from a podium at Harvard, the American Secretary of State proposed something with no precedent in the long history of victors and vanquished: that the winning side should pour its own treasure into rebuilding the economies of the defeated.3 The Marshall Plan, enacted the following spring, moved billions into a shattered Europe over four years — not as charity but as architecture, on the calculation that prosperous neighbours bound by trade do not march on one another. It worked. The peace of 1948 held where the peace of 1919 had failed, and the difference was not the absence of grievance but the presence of a structure that made cooperation more profitable than revenge.

The distinction the two episodes draw is the one that matters this week. A peace can be punitive — it stops the fighting and stores up the next round — or reconstructive — it stops the fighting and builds the shared interest that keeps it stopped. The terms decide which one you have signed. And the terms, crucially, are tested not in the signing but in the doing: in whether the reconstruction clause is funded, whether the reopening is real, whether the former combatants find themselves, a year on, with more to lose from another war than from the peace.

Which brings us to the most exact precedent of all, and a hopeful one. The strait in this week's news is not the first waterway closed by war and reopened by diplomacy. In June 1967 the Suez Canal shut in the middle of a war and stayed shut for eight years; it did not reopen until June 1975, and even then the ceremony was the smallest part of it.4 Before a single ship could pass, navies had to clear the mines and raise the wrecks sunk along its length — a slow, dangerous, unglamorous labour that took many months after the politicians had shaken hands. The canal reopened by treaty; it reopened in practice by minesweeping.

That is the Hormuz parallel, almost exactly. The desks in this issue have been blunt about it: the oil price relaxed on the news, but the analysts cautioned that a strait can be declared open long before it is safely, fully working — idled fields must be restarted, insurance must re-price risk, the first uncontested tanker must actually sail.5 The signature reopens the strait on paper. The minesweeping — physical and financial — reopens it in fact. Milton's "victories of peace" are precisely this after-work, and they are won by clearing channels, not signing pages.

If the history offers a warning and a hope, it also offers a test — a short checklist for reading any peace, including this one, in the months before its character is obvious. First: does the settlement give the former enemies something to build together, or merely something to stop doing? Versailles failed the first question; the Marshall Plan passed it. Second: is the hardest physical work — the equivalent of clearing the canal — actually funded and underway, or only promised? Suez was reopened by budgets and minesweepers, not communiqués. Third, and most human: have the people on the ground been given a reason to prefer the peace to the grievance — a job, a route, a price that falls — within a timeframe they can feel?

These are not abstractions. They are the questions an oil trader is already asking about the first tanker, a Singaporean household is asking about the July tariff, a parent is asking at the dinner table about whether the grave faces can really stop. The same structure runs through all of them: the announcement lands in an instant; the consequences arrive on a schedule of their own, and whether they arrive at all depends on work that the ceremony cannot do.

There is a quieter point underneath, and it is the one worth carrying out of this issue. We are conditioned to treat arrivals as endings — the deal is done, the war is over, the credits roll. Almost nothing important works that way. The gravitational-wave catalogue this week did not finish the universe by counting 161 more collisions; it opened the population questions that were impossible before. The film everyone is talking about ends on first contact, which is to say it ends where the actual story would begin. Even a reconciliation, the Human Desk reminds us, is not the apology but the fortnight after it. Arrival is the beginning of the part that counts.

So the right response to a long-awaited signature is neither the cynic's shrug nor the celebrant's relief, but Milton's: to recognise that a victory has been won, and that a different, harder kind of victory now becomes possible — and is not yet assured. Peace hath her victories. They are real, and they are renowned. But they are won in the reconstruction, the minesweeping, the funded clause and the kept word — in the day-after work that no ceremony can perform and no signature can replace. The thing has arrived. Now comes the part where we find out what arrived.
