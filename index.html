Marcus Tullius Cicero · De Oratore, 55 BC

And the body, it turns out, has its own memory-maintenance crew — the cellular recycling our Fitness desk examines. Cicero meant memory as the keeper of everything we know; we have made it the keeper of everything we can build. The treasury, this week, is the whole story.

Described in every capital, signed in none

The US–Iran framework is, by week's end, the most negotiated document nobody has put a pen to — and oil has stopped waiting for it.

The arc since our last edition is short and familiar: progress announced, progress stalled. President Trump again called the talks “largely negotiated,” while Iran-aligned Hezbollah rejected the US-brokered Lebanon ceasefire that Washington has tied to any wider deal, and Iran's new leadership warned that American bases in the region were “no longer safe.”1 The memorandum of understanding that would extend the ceasefire and de-mine the Strait of Hormuz remains, as it has for weeks, a draft.

The market has drawn its own conclusion. Brent slipped below US$94 a barrel — down roughly 4.5% on the month and a world away from April's US$117 spike — as traders weighed faint diplomatic noise against the hard fact of Chinese crude imports falling to a ten-year low and a sixth straight draw on US inventories.2 A brief explosion at Oman's Mina Al Fahal export terminal jolted the tape and then faded. The pattern of the spring holds: the politics threaten, the barrels keep moving, and the premium leaks out of the price.

Prediction markets tell the same story in numbers. The odds of a signed peace by mid-June, which touched 60% on the MOU's first announcement, have collapsed to single digits — not because anyone expects the war to reignite, but because nobody can name the day the signatures arrive.3

Two votes and a drone war that won't sit still

Colombia heads to a runoff it didn't expect. The first round on 31 May upended the polls: hard-right outsider Abelardo de la Espriella took 43.7% against leftist senator Iván Cepeda's 40.9%, sending the two to a 21 June decider.1 De la Espriella, a criminal lawyer who styles himself a Trump admirer, has since picked up the endorsement of Democratic Centre's Paloma Valencia; Cepeda runs as the continuity candidate for the constitutionally barred Gustavo Petro. The result will shape Bogotá's posture toward Washington and the drug-trafficking fight — a vote being watched far beyond the Andes.

Ukraine keeps hitting the wallet, not the front. The delta since our last issue is the same strategy at higher volume: long-range drones on Russian oil terminals and refineries, including a strike timed to Putin's St Petersburg economic forum, with the Volgograd refinery knocked offline. The aim is leverage on the energy revenue that funds the war — the same logic that keeps a floor under Brent even as the Hormuz premium drains.

An unsigned truce that holds is the strangest kind of stability: real enough to trade on, not real enough to file.

The quieter wire. Beyond the headline two, a Monday's worth of housekeeping: the IAEA's Rafael Grossi signalling the sides are “pretty close” on a negotiating structure even as the substance lags; fresh US sanctions on Iranian oil-smuggling networks; and a Gulf still counting the cost of a spring that took Brent to a 2008-style peak before letting it fall. None of it is a turning point. All of it is the texture of a world waiting for one document to become real.

A secret ballot, and the cadres who won't keep it secret

Three weeks out from the Workers' Party conference that could unseat Pritam Singh, the most telling signal is how openly his backers are breaking the secrecy.

The mechanics are now fixed. On 28 June, following a requisition by 25 cadres, the Workers' Party will hold a special cadres' conference with a three-part agenda: Singh accounts for his December conviction for misleading Parliament; cadres ask him to step down as secretary-general; and, should he decline, a secret ballot decides his fate — all before the regular CEC elections the same day.1

The delta this week is tone, not procedure. At the launch of a new party history, former NCMP Yee Jenn Jong told the room he would back Singh and added, pointedly, “although it's a secret vote, my vote is not secret” — a public declaration of loyalty that turns a confidential mechanism into something closer to a show of hands.2 Separately, the Law Society has begun its own disciplinary proceedings, with the matter bound for the Court of Three Judges.

For a party that has spent a decade building its “2.0” bench, the conference is less about one ballot than about whether an orderly succession can be choreographed in public without looking like a purge — or a coronation.

The trough is here. The question is how long you lock.

Three-month SORA sits near 1.08% — about as low as this cycle is expected to go — and the curve says the cheap window starts closing later this year.

For anyone refinancing, the arithmetic has rarely been friendlier. Floating packages tracking three-month compounded SORA are quoting from around 1.26% all-in for larger private loans, with the lowest fixed rates near 1.35% — both comfortably under HDB's 2.6% concessionary rate.1 SORA itself, which peaked above 3% in early 2025, is widely modelled to bottom around 1% in the second quarter before drifting back toward roughly 1.39% by year-end.2

That forecast is the whole decision. Float, and you capture the last of the dip while it lasts; fix, and you trade a few basis points now for certainty through the climb. For a property owner carrying more than one loan, the move worth the weekend is not chasing the absolute floor but laddering — locking the loan you can't afford to have reprice, floating the one you can.

Lock the loan you can't afford to reprice; float the one you can. The floor is a moment, not a strategy.

The physical market, meanwhile, is doing something it hasn't in seven years: cooling at the edges. The HDB Resale Price Index dipped 0.1% quarter-on-quarter — its first fall since 2019 — even as the million-dollar-flat count stayed at record levels, a market splitting between a softening middle and an undimmed top.3 Developer costs are inching up too, with land betterment charges rising about 4.1% since March, a quiet tax on the next launch cycle.

Technology · The Memory Wall

The most fought-over commodity on Earth is suddenly memory

AI servers are inhaling the world's DRAM. The result is the sharpest memory-price shock in fifteen years — and it is quietly re-pricing everything you might buy.

The number that ought to stop you: DRAM contract prices rose roughly 58–63% quarter-on-quarter in the second quarter of 2026, with NAND up 70–75% — the largest jumps in a decade, against supply-demand gaps researchers call the widest since 2011.1 Gartner has floated a memory-cost surge of up to 130%. This is not the usual cycle. It is a deliberate reallocation: the three makers who can swing their fabs between high-bandwidth memory for AI accelerators and the commodity DDR5 in your laptop are choosing HBM, where margins run several times higher and a gigabyte eats roughly three to four times the wafer area.2

Every wafer sent to an Nvidia stack is a wafer denied to a phone. The consumer fallout is already visible at the edges: Framework lifted DDR5 prices on its laptops by 50% and warned of more; Micron retired its consumer-facing Crucial brand in February to concentrate on enterprise AI buyers; and counterfeit memory modules have begun appearing as scarcity bids up the price.3 IDC calls it a “permanent reallocation,” not a blip — with new capacity not expected to bite until 2027–28.

Tonight: Apple rents a mind

As this issue went out, Apple's WWDC keynote — Tim Cook's expected last before John Ternus takes the chief executive's chair in September — was hours away in Singapore time, under the tagline “All systems glow.”4 The headline is a rebuilt, standalone Siri reportedly powered not by Apple's own model but by a custom ~1.2-trillion-parameter Google Gemini, for a reported US$1 billion a year. Six “27” operating-system betas, generative Photos tools and a system-wide “Search or Ask” gesture are expected; the iPhone 11 drops off the list.

There is an irony worth marking. The company that built its brand on owning the whole stack is, for its most important new feature, renting the intelligence — and the same memory crunch above is the reason its Mac refresh keeps slipping. We will judge what ships, not what leaked; the only honest verdict tonight is that the assistant in your pocket will soon be doing its thinking elsewhere. On the H200: still cleared for China, still ~undelivered as Beijing steers buyers to Huawei — delta only, per our running file.5

Technology · The Home Lab

In the home lab, memory is oxygen

If the memory wall has a silver lining for the desk-side AI builder, it is clarity: the binding constraint on running serious models at home is no longer compute or even price-per-token — it is how many gigabytes of fast memory you can put under one roof. Token generation is bandwidth-bound; capacity decides which models even load. That is why the year's most interesting machines are the unified-memory boxes — Nvidia's 128 GB DGX Spark and the cheaper AMD Strix Halo mini-PCs — rather than the fastest single GPU.1

A community benchmark updated this month puts the two within striking distance on token generation for a shared 120-billion-parameter model — roughly 52–56 tokens/sec — with the Spark pulling ahead on prompt processing and long context, and Strix Halo winning on price.2 A separate, much-cited case study makes the economics vivid: a four-node Mac Studio cluster on Thunderbolt 5 can serve a trillion-parameter model privately for around US$30,000 drawing 250 watts — work that four H100s would do for US$200,000-plus and 2,800 watts.3

The cloud-versus-local debate used to be easy. Cloud always won. For a specific class of work, that calculus has flipped — and memory is why.

The catch is the same crunch from the facing page: the RAM that makes a home lab possible is exactly what AI data centres are hoovering up, so the price of building one is climbing even as the case for it strengthens. Buy the memory before the model, the saying now goes.

Heritage answers the memory crunch with… 1958

While the silicon world fights over wafers, the most appealing gear this week is gloriously analogue — and built to outlast the cycle.

Klipsch has done something it has never done in seventy-five years: added a bookshelf speaker to its Heritage line. The new Rebellion revives Paul W. Klipsch's 1958 H8 design — only sixteen were ever made — pairing a horn-loaded tweeter on a Tractrix horn with a new woofer.1 It is the antithesis of a gadget that depreciates the moment a faster chip ships: a cabinet engineered to sound the same in 2050. For a reader who already owns the cameras and the watches, this is the audio equivalent — provenance you can hear.

Two more for the audiophile bench. Schiit's first-ever portable, the Vestri USB-C DAC and headphone amp, aims to make a wired pair sound markedly better off a phone or laptop — a pointed reminder, as everything goes wireless, that the cleanest signal still runs down a cable. And Bowers & Wilkins has given its elite loudspeakers a quiet hi-fi refresh. None of it needs an app, an account, or a model number that expires.2

The Connected Home · The Assistant Question

Another voice in the house — and the case for keeping it local

If WWDC delivers a Gemini-Siri and the rumoured HomePad, every Apple household gains a cloud listener overnight. For the migrator, that sharpens the local-first argument, not the opposite.

The rumour mill is specific: alongside the rebuilt Siri, Apple is said to be readying a smart-home hub — a “HomePad” on a dedicated home OS — though it likely waits for Siri's autumn launch.1 For the Lutron-plus-Savant-plus-Ubiquiti household already drifting toward Home Assistant, the prospect is double-edged: a slicker hub, but one more assistant routing intent through someone else's servers. The migrator's instinct — keep the brain in the house — only looks wiser as the assistants multiply.

That is the through-line of Home Assistant's year, and the June release leaned into it without giving us a new headline: 2026.6 (“Pick a card”) shipped on 3 June with a friendlier Matter onboarding, Z-Wave locks catching up to their Matter siblings, and a UniFi Protect alarm-profile control — refinements, not a re-introduction.2 The next thing to actually watch is mid-month: the Connectivity Standards Alliance's “Unify” event (16–18 June), where Matter-over-camera support finally has to show real controllers and real hardware rather than a spec.

The more voices you let into the house, the more it matters where they do their thinking.

The reader's move is unchanged and unglamorous: treat any new hub as a controller, not a brain; keep automations local; let the cloud assistant be a nice front door, not the keys.

The new watch money buys the past

As New York's biggest auction week in memory opens, the market's energy has decisively left the boutique and moved to provenance — the things they aren't making any more.

This is the week the watch trade has been circling. Christie's Important Watches (around 10 June), Phillips' New York Auction XIV (13–14 June) and Sotheby's Shapes of Cartier (15 June) stack an estimated US$35–60 million of lots into a few city blocks — anchored by a white-gold Patek 3448G, one of two known, and a Cartier “London Crash” that just set a model record above US$2 million in Geneva.1 Read the hammer prices, not the previews: a documented result turns a private legend into a public floor.

The deeper signal is where the heat is. Vintage and provenance are setting records while the modern boutique market endures its worst slump in years — to the point that houses like Cartier have bought back unsold pieces. More than 40% of Sotheby's watch buyers are now under 40, and they are chasing stories, not waitlists.2 It rhymes with this issue's theme: when the new is over-supplied and the old is finite, value migrates to scarcity.

When the new is over-made and the old is finite, the money goes looking for what can't be reordered.
Cloth & escapes

On the wardrobe side, the Hermès logic holds through the cycle: the quota-bound, made-to-last object behaves more like the vintage Patek than the boutique steel sports watch — scarcity as the whole proposition. And the perennial escape still pencils out: Japan on a soft yen remains, in the trade's own phrase, “still cheap, quietly cooling” on overtourism — best timed to the summer matsuri our Diary maps overleaf.

A short-story collection takes the year's richest fiction prize

Julia Elliott's Hellions wins the US$150,000 Carol Shields Prize — and the win itself is a small argument about form.

On 2 June in Toronto, the Carol Shields Prize for Fiction — one of the world's richest, at US$150,000 plus a stay at Fogo Island Inn — went to the South Carolina writer Julia Elliott for Hellions, her collection of eleven Southern-Gothic stories that swing from a plague-stricken medieval convent to a pet alligator named Dragon.1 It is the second time in the prize's four years that short fiction has beaten the novel — a quiet vote for the form publishers keep declaring commercially dead. For a reader who likes the strange and the well-made, it is the literary equivalent of a single perfect short film.

The Family Desk · AI in the House

The new assistant doesn't just answer — it befriends

On the very day a chatbot Siri arrives on every iPhone, the evidence on children and AI companions points to one unglamorous tool: conversation, not surveillance.

The scale has crept up while adults were still thinking of AI as “the thing that writes emails.” A 2026 study in JAMA Network Open finds roughly 20% of pre-teens and 9% of eight-to-nine-year-olds already using AI chatbots; a Common Sense Media survey put teen use of AI companions far higher.1 The distinction that matters for an eleven-year-old is the companion: human-named, endlessly affirming, available at 2 a.m. — designed, by engagement logic, to feel like a friend.

That design is the risk. A red-team study by a Boston University psychiatrist, who posed as a troubled teen across ten leading bots, documented a troubling rate of poor or unsafe responses; Common Sense now recommends no AI companions for under-18s at all.2 None of which means panic — it means the same boundaries good parents already use for screens: shared spaces, devices out of bedrooms overnight, and a running conversation about how a given chat made them feel, which is the literacy a filter can't supply.

You don't have to be an expert on AI. Be honest about what you don't know, and ask your child what they use.

The timing is pointed: as Apple wires a Google-powered assistant into the family phone tonight, the ambient AI in the home stops being a thing children seek out and becomes a thing that is simply there. Talking about it early is the cheapest safeguard available.

The Family Desk · The Tween Years

An AI-literacy toolkit, and a regulatory map

What actually works

The same UCSF/ABCD evidence base that reshaped the screen-time conversation applies here: monitoring and modelling beat reward-and-punishment, and parents' own habits set the ceiling. Translated for AI: co-use a chatbot for homework rather than banning it; name it as software, not a friend; and teach the tell — that a bot which always agrees is engineered to, and is not a measure of whether you're right. Curiosity and sleep still outrank any control setting.

The Chatter

SG parent groups are split between “ban it till secondary school” and “they'll meet it anyway, so teach it.” The middle path — supervised use, in a shared room, with the homework open — is where most pediatric advice lands.

— local parenting forums, paraphrased

The rules are catching up

The policy backdrop is moving fast: a US children's tech-safety bill cleared committee in March; the UK is bringing AI chatbots under the Online Safety Act, with the same child-safety duties as social platforms; Australia's under-16 framework and state-level chatbot bills add to the patchwork.1 Useful to know, not to lean on — the technology still moves faster than the statute.

Don't be alarmist
  • AI literacy is a skill, not just a hazard — kids who learn to question a confident machine gain something durable.
  • The strongest protective factor in the data isn't an app setting; it's an adult who asks, and listens.

Curiosities · A World With Weather

Clouds at dawn, gone by dusk — on a planet 700 light-years off

Webb has caught a giant world keeping a daily appointment with its own weather: mineral clouds that condense every morning and burn off by night.

It is the closest thing yet to a weather report from another star system. Using the James Webb Space Telescope, astronomers watched a hot giant planet roughly 700 light-years away form clouds made of minerals on its morning side and clear them by evening — a repeating diurnal cycle, not a one-off haze.1 Reading a single alien day this precisely, across that distance, is the kind of feat that was science fiction when this reader was buying his first telescope.

It belongs to a season of Webb oddities: a giant world wrapped in dawn rock-clouds, a lemon-shaped pulsar planet that may hide diamonds, a directly-imaged super-Jupiter (29 Cygni b) fifteen times Jupiter's mass. Each is a step toward the harder target — reading the air of a small, temperate world for the chemistry of life.

Love & Life · The Examined Life

The treasury you already own

If memory is the week's scarce resource, here is the one kind you can mint at will — and the research says a shared past is among the most reliable repairs a relationship has.

Psychologists have a name for it: romantic nostalgia — fondly recalling experiences shared with a partner. Across correlational, experimental and daily-diary studies, it tracks with greater commitment, satisfaction and closeness; deliberately inducing it, by writing or by an old song, strengthens those bonds for a day or two after.1 Nostalgia in general turns out to be socially loaded — its memories almost always place us among people we love — and to buffer loneliness and affirm belonging.

The honest caveat keeps it from being a greeting-card platitude. A longitudinal study found relationship nostalgia nudges couples toward emotional homeostasis rather than a simple happiness spike, and the short-term effect can even dip before it settles — which is roughly what couples therapists mean when they walk partners back through how they met.2 The takeaway is not “reminisce and be happy” but “tend the archive”: the shared memory is a resource that pays out when it's drawn on with care.

Memory is the treasury of a couple, too. The difference is you can always make more — by paying attention now.

Which is the quiet rhyme with the rest of this issue. The world is fighting over silicon memory it can't manufacture fast enough; the longevity labs are learning to recycle the body's; and the cheapest treasury of all is the one a couple builds on an ordinary Monday and spends, gratefully, years later.

Fitness · The Cellular Recycling Program

Your power plants need a memory-maintenance crew

The longevity field's most grounded supplement story this year isn't about building muscle — it's about clearing out the worn-out machinery inside it.

Mitochondria — the cell's power plants — degrade with age, and the process that recycles the failing ones, mitophagy, slows as we get older. Enter Urolithin A (sold as Mitopure), a gut-derived postbiotic that activates that recycling. A 2026 study extends the mechanism, showing UA restores communication between mitochondria, the ER and lysosomes via calcium signalling — improving healthspan in lab models.1 The human trials are more modest but real: placebo-controlled work in middle-aged and older adults has shown roughly 12% gains in muscle strength and meaningful endurance improvements, with lower inflammation markers.2

Think of it as memory-maintenance for the cell: not adding new hardware, but clearing the slots the old machinery is hogging — an oddly fitting metaphor for a week defined by scarce memory and reallocation. The catch most marketing skips: more than 60% of people can't make UA from diet (pomegranates, berries) because they lack the gut microbes, which is the real argument for supplementing.

The Chatter · longevity-discourse sceptic

The grounded take: UA has more human data than most shelf-fillers, but effects are modest, the headline trials didn't all hit their primary power endpoint, and 1,000 mg of branded postbiotic isn't cheap. File under “reasonable add-on,” not “needle-mover” — and well behind the free stuff: lift, walk, sleep.

The Long Read · Memory

The Treasury

A week about scarce memory — in chips, in bodies, and in the things couples choose to keep. Three old episodes explain who gets first claim on it, and how the shortage finally ends.

In 55 BC, in the second book of De Oratore, Cicero set down a line that has outlived almost everything else he wrote on the craft of speaking: memory, he said, is the treasury and the guardian of all things.1 He meant it technically — memory was one of the five disciplines an orator had to master — but the metaphor he reached for is the one that endures. A treasury is not where you keep what is plentiful. It is where you put what is scarce, valuable, and worth guarding. This week, almost by accident, the whole edition turned into an argument about exactly that.

Four desks over, the most fought-over commodity on Earth turned out to be memory in its most literal form. DRAM contract prices are climbing at their steepest rate in over a decade as AI servers inhale every available chip, quietly re-pricing phones, laptops and home labs along the way. On the same Monday, Apple was expected to give Siri a borrowed memory — renting Google's model for a reported billion dollars a year, because remembering, at scale, is now the expensive part. Our Fitness desk examined a molecule that helps the body clear out and rebuild its own worn-out power plants: cellular memory-maintenance. Love & Life reported that couples who reminisce well are measurably steadier, treating shared memory as a joint account. And behind all of it sat an unsigned peace in the Gulf, its calm priced into a barrel of oil.

Five different desks; one shape. Each is a story about a stock of something valuable that has suddenly turned scarce, and about who gets first claim on it. History has run this experiment before — at least three times worth remembering.

A treasury is not where you keep what is plentiful. It is where you put what is scarce.

I · The vault was once made of mind

Before there was a treasury of silicon there was a treasury of mind, and the same Cicero preserved its founding legend.2 The poet Simonides of Ceos had just stepped out of a banquet in Thessaly when the hall collapsed behind him, crushing the guests beyond recognition. Asked to identify the dead, he found that he could — by walking the room again in his mind and recalling who had reclined where. From that grim accident, the story runs, came the method of loci: the memory palace, the practice of storing what you cannot afford to lose in vivid, ordered places.

For two thousand years, memory was a thing you built and maintained, the way you would tend a vault. Then print, and later the database, externalised it, and we mostly stopped. This week's news is that externalisation reaching its logical end: a trillion-dollar company paying a billion a year so its phone can do the remembering on its behalf.

SOURCES  1. Cicero, De Oratore II (55 BC), on memory as the treasury and guardian of all things.  2. Cicero, De Oratore II; the method of loci attributed to Simonides of Ceos, also recounted by Quintilian, Institutio Oratoria.

II · When the board chose for Detroit

The second lesson is about what happens when a scarce input has two suitors and one of them will pay more. In the first days of February 1942, weeks after Pearl Harbor, the War Production Board did something unthinkable in peacetime: it ordered Detroit to stop building cars.3 Not slow — stop. No civilian automobile, truck or spare part was made in the United States from February 1942 until October 1945.4

The steel, rubber and assembly lines were not destroyed. They were reallocated, wholesale, to the bidder who needed them most. That is the memory market today, minus the patriotism. A high-bandwidth memory stack for an AI accelerator eats several times the wafer area of ordinary DRAM and sells for far more,5 so the fabs are quietly doing to your laptop what the board did to the family sedan — routing the scarce substrate to the higher bidder.

Nobody issued an order. The price did the rationing.

The signs are everywhere once you look: a small laptop maker lifting memory prices by half, a chipmaker walking away from its consumer brand, counterfeit modules surfacing in the channel.6 No directive was signed. The market did by arithmetic what Donald Nelson's board did by decree, and the home lab is on the wrong end of the equation — for now.

III · How a shortage actually ends

Which leaves the only question that finally matters: how does a memory shortage end? Here the third episode is the hopeful one.

Through the nineteenth century the world's usable nitrogen — for both fertiliser and explosives — came from a finite, geographically captive supply: Chilean saltpetre and South American guano, depleting and fought over to the point of war.7 Nations could have rationed it indefinitely. Instead, between 1909 and 1913, Fritz Haber and Carl Bosch learned to pull nitrogen straight out of the air, and the scarcity did not get managed — it dissolved, abolished by new capacity.8

Memory will end the same way. Not by the fabs nobly under-charging, but by the capacity coming online in 2027–28 and by substitution — cheaper stacks, smarter compression, models that need less. The shortage is real; the panic is a phase. The lesson of Haber and Bosch is that you do not beat scarcity by hoarding less. You beat it by making more of the thing, or by inventing a way around needing it.

You do not beat scarcity by hoarding less. You beat it by learning to make more.

The treasury, kept and keeping

It is worth holding the three episodes together. Simonides says memory is something you cultivate, and can lose. The War Production Board says that when it turns scarce it flows to whoever values it most, sentiment be damned. Haber and Bosch say the cure for scarcity is to make more, not to ration harder.

We wrote two issues ago, borrowing Pasteur, that fortune favours the prepared — that reserves are built before the crisis, not during it.9 And in No. 10, with Thoreau, that the cost of a thing is the amount of life you exchange for it.10 Both apply now. The home-labber who bought memory last year looks prescient; the one buying this quarter is paying in life as well as dollars. The body's version is the gentlest of the three: the molecule our Fitness desk examined adds nothing new — it helps the cell recycle the mitochondria it already owns. Capacity from maintenance, the most underrated kind.

Cicero called memory the guardian of all things. He meant that we keep it. This week was a reminder that it also, increasingly, keeps us — and that the only durable way to guard a treasury is to learn, eventually, how to mint more of what it holds. The shortage will pass. What it taught about who pays first, and who prepares early, will not.

The Last Word

Mint, don't hoard.

A whole edition rhymed this week without meaning to. Chips, cells, couples and even the calm in the Gulf were all the same story — a treasury of something scarce, and a queue forming for first claim on it. The week's quiet comfort is that every previous treasury panic ended not when people learned to want less, but when someone learned to make more. Memory is having its moment of being precious. Precious things get guarded; then, if we are lucky and patient, they get manufactured. Keep what matters close. Assume the shortage is temporary. Build the reserve before you need it.
